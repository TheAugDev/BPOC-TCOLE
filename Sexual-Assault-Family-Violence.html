Sexual Assault & Family Violence

BPOC Chapter 17 | TCOLE Preparation

Family Violence Definitions (Family Code Ch. 71)

Family Violence (§71.004): An act by a member of a family or household against another member of the family or household that is intended to result in physical harm, bodily injury, assault, or sexual assault or that is a threat that places the member in fear of imminent physical harm, bodily injury, assault, or sexual assault, but does not include defensive measures to protect oneself.

Family (§71.003): Individuals related by consanguinity (blood) or affinity (marriage), individuals who are former spouses of each other, individuals who are the parents of the same child, without regard to marriage, and a foster child and foster parent, without regard to whether those individuals reside together.

Household (§71.005): A unit composed of persons living together in the same dwelling, without regard to whether they are related to each other.

Member of a Household (§71.006): Includes a person who previously lived in a household.

Dating Violence (§71.021): An act, other than a defensive measure to protect oneself, by an actor that is committed against a victim or applicant for a protective order with whom the actor has or has had a dating relationship... that is intended to result in physical harm, bodily injury, assault, or sexual assault or that is a threat that reasonably places the victim or applicant in fear of imminent physical harm.

Consent (PC §1.07(11))

Consent means assent in fact, whether expressed or apparent. It is NOT consent if accomplished through force, threats, or coercion, or if the victim is silent, unconscious, physically unable to resist, or intoxicated to the point of being unaware.

Any sexual act against a victim younger than 17 is nonconsensual by law.

Strangulation vs. Choking

Strangulation

External pressure applied to the neck that obstructs breathing or blood flow. This is a felony (Assault by Impeding Breath/Circulation) and is a high-risk indicator for future lethality.

Choking

Internal blockage of the airway (e.g., by food). This is a medical emergency but not typically a criminal offense in this context.

Victims often use the word "choked" when they mean they were strangled. It is critical for officers to ask clarifying questions to determine if external pressure was applied to the neck.

Sexual Assault (PC §22.011)

Punishment Level: Felony of the Second Degree

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Intentionally or knowingly
  • Causes the penetration of the anus or sexual organ of another person by any means, without that person's consent; OR
  • Causes the penetration of the mouth of another person by the sexual organ of the actor, without that person's consent; OR
  • Causes the sexual organ of another person, without that person's consent, to contact or penetrate the mouth, anus, or sexual organ of another person, including the actor.

Practical Example: A person uses threats to force their acquaintance to perform oral sex. This is Sexual Assault, even if no physical force was used.

Sexual Assault of a Child

Punishment Level: Felony of the First Degree (if against a child under 14) or Felony of the Second Degree (if against a child 14-16).

Elements Checklist:

  • Commits an act of sexual assault as defined above, AND
  • The victim is a child (under 17).

Practical Example: An adult engages in sexual intercourse with a 15-year-old. Even if the minor appeared to agree, it is legally Sexual Assault because a child cannot give consent.

Victim Rights & Officer Duties

Mandatory Duties on Scene (CCP Art. 56A.403)

Peace officers responding to a sexual assault call **shall**:

  • Provide Written Referrals: Give the victim written info for the nearest sexual assault program.
  • Provide Written Notice of Rights: Inform the victim of their rights, including the right to a pseudonym and information about the Track-Kit system for sexual assault evidence kits (SAEKs).
  • Offer Forensic Medical Exam (SANE): Offer to request a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) exam. The victim has the right to the exam (within 120 hours) and the right to refuse it. There is no cost to the victim.

Additional Victim Rights (CCP Art. 56A.052)

Victims of sexual assault have a right to a SANE exam and HIV/AIDS testing. Upon request, they also have the right to:

  • Be notified of the status of the evidence collected.
  • Receive counseling regarding HIV/AIDS.
  • Receive information about the disposition of the case.
  • Use a pseudonym to protect their identity (CCP Ch. 58).

Trauma-Informed Approach

An evidence-based approach that understands the effects of trauma on the brain. The goal is to build rapport, reduce re-traumatization, and increase victim participation in the justice process.

Do:

  • Be patient and use a calm tone.
  • Meet basic needs first (water, safety).
  • Ask open-ended, non-judgmental questions ("What do you remember feeling?" not "Why didn't you leave?").
  • Explain what to expect next.
  • Respect their right to choose whether to participate in the investigation.

Don't:

  • Interrupt or rush the victim.
  • Threaten prosecution for false reporting.
  • Use judgmental language ("alleged", "their story").
  • Ask "why" questions that imply blame.
  • Have the victim write a statement (can create inconsistencies).

Responding to Family Violence

Family violence calls are among the most dangerous for officers due to high emotions and unpredictability. The primary goals are to protect the victim and hold the correct person accountable.

  • Officer Safety: Approach with caution, at least two officers if possible, park away from the residence, and be alert for people leaving the scene.
  • Scene Management: De-escalate, separate parties, locate and secure weapons, and interview all parties and witnesses independently.
  • Officer Duties (CCP Art. 5.05): An officer who investigates a family violence allegation **shall** make a written report.
  • Victim Notification (Notice to Adult Victims): Officers must give a written notice of a victim's legal rights and local resources to victims of family violence.

Determining the Predominant Aggressor

It is crucial to investigate beyond the immediate incident to determine who is the primary aggressor versus who may have been acting in self-defense. Consider:

  • Injuries: Relative severity of injuries? Are there signs of defensive wounds?
  • Fear: Is one party genuinely afraid of the other?
  • History: Is there a history of violence by one person against the other?
  • Credibility: Do the stories make sense? Do they align with the evidence at the scene?
  • Future Risk: Who poses the most danger to the other in the future?

Power and Control Dynamics

Abusers use a pattern of tactics to exert power and control over their victims, making it difficult for them to leave. These tactics are often subtle and escalate over time.

Violation of Protective Order or Bond Condition (PC §25.07)

Punishment Level: Class A Misdemeanor, but can be a State Jail Felony or Third Degree Felony with prior convictions or enhancements.

Elements Checklist:

  • A person who is subject to a condition of bond or a protective order
  • Knowingly or intentionally
  • Commits an act in violation of the order/condition (e.g., communicating with the protected person, going to a prohibited location, possessing a firearm).

Crucially, the offender must have been served with the order for it to be criminally enforceable.

Practical Example: A person has a protective order against their ex-partner. The ex-partner, who has been served, sends them a text message. This is a violation of the protective order and a criminal offense.

Types of Protective Orders

Magistrate's Order for Emergency Protection (EPO) (CCP Art. 17.292)

Issued by a magistrate following an arrest for an offense involving family violence, sexual assault, or stalking. It is criminally enforceable and lasts for 31-91 days.

Family Violence Protective Order (FC §85.025)

A civil order filed by a victim of family violence. Can last for up to 2 years, or longer in some cases. It becomes criminally enforceable once the respondent is served.

Sexual Assault/Stalking Protective Order (SAPO)

A civil order available to victims of sexual assault or stalking, regardless of their relationship to the offender. Can be issued for life.

Officer Powers & Bond Conditions

Arrest for Violation of PO Not in View (CCP Art. 14.03(a)(3))

A peace officer **may** arrest, without a warrant, a person the peace officer has probable cause to believe has violated a protective order, if the offense is not committed in the presence of the peace officer.

Practical Example: A victim shows an officer a text message sent by her ex-husband a few hours ago, which violates the "no contact" provision of a served protective order. Even though the officer did not see the violation occur, they have probable cause and may arrest the ex-husband.

Conditions for Defendant Charged with FV (CCP Art. 17.49)

A magistrate may require a defendant charged with a family violence offense to stay away from the victim's residence and place of employment as a condition of their bond.

Practical Example: A person is arrested for assaulting their spouse. As a condition of their release on bond, the judge orders them not to return to the family home until the case is resolved. Violating this bond condition can lead to their bond being revoked and re-arrest.

Mandatory Reporting (FC §261.101)

A person having cause to believe that a child's physical or mental health or welfare has been adversely affected by abuse or neglect by any person shall immediately make a report. This applies to everyone, but professionals (like police officers) who fail to report can be charged with a Class A Misdemeanor.

Taking Possession of a Child

In an Emergency Without Court Order (FC §262.104)

A law enforcement officer may take possession of a child without a court order if the officer has information that would lead a person of ordinary prudence and caution to believe that there is an immediate danger to the physical health or safety of the child.

Practical Example: An officer responds to a home and finds two adults unconscious from a drug overdose with a toddler wandering nearby. The officer can take possession of the child due to the immediate danger.

Other Scenarios for Taking Possession

  • Voluntary Delivery (FC §262.004): An officer may take possession of a child if the parent or guardian voluntarily delivers the child to them.
  • Missing Child (FC §262.007): An officer who finds a missing child during an investigation and believes they may be concealed or taken shall take possession.
  • Temporary Possession to Return Home (FC §262.110): An officer may take temporary possession of a child in a dangerous situation for the sole purpose of delivering them to a parent or guardian.

Important Considerations

  • The child may NOT be held in a jail or other secure detention facility (FC §262.108).
  • An officer is immune from civil liability for taking possession if they have reasonable cause (FC §262.003).

Medical Consent for a Child (FC Ch. 32)

Generally, a parent or guardian must consent to medical treatment for a child. However, there are exceptions:

  • Officer Consent (§32.001): An officer who has lawfully taken custody of a minor may consent to treatment if they believe the minor needs immediate medical care.
  • Child Consent (§32.003): A child 16 or older can consent to their own treatment if they are unmarried and reside apart from their parents.
  • Examination without Consent (§32.005): A physician may examine a child without consent if they have reasonable grounds to believe the child has been abused or neglected.
