Civil Process and Liability

BPOC Chapter 14 | TCOLE Preparation

Officer Liability

A misapplication of law or procedure can result in civil action against an officer and/or their agency. To reduce this potential, officers must understand their responsibilities and have a thorough knowledge of state laws and constitutional restraints.

Elements Checklist (Who Can Be Held Liable?):

  • A level of government: State, county, municipality, or special district.
  • An agency: Any law enforcement agency, regardless of how it was created.
  • An individual peace officer: May be held liable in their individual capacity.

Statutes on Officer Liability

Liability for Refusal or Neglect in Performance of Official Duties (CPRC 7.001)

A clerk, sheriff, or other officer who neglects or refuses to perform a duty required under the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure is liable for actual damages to the injured person. They may also be punished for contempt of court with a fine of not less than $10 or more than $100.

Liability Regarding Execution of Writs (CPRC 7.003)

An officer is NOT liable for damages from executing a writ if the officer acts in "good faith." Good faith is shown if a reasonably prudent officer, under similar circumstances, could have believed their conduct was justified based on the information they possessed.

Execution of Process; Penalty (Sheriff) (LGC 85.021)

A sheriff commits an offense if they fail to return a process or precept as required, or if they make a false return. This is punishable by contempt with a fine up to $100, and the sheriff is liable for all damages sustained by the injured person.

Failure to Execute Process (Constable) (LGC 86.024)

If a constable fails or refuses to execute and return a process, they shall be fined for contempt between $10 and $100 for the benefit of the injured person.

Liability Scenarios (Case Studies)

Case Study 1: Officer-Involved Shooting

Scenario: An officer returns fire at an active shooter outside a liquor store. The suspect is killed, but two bystanders inside the store are critically injured by the officer's gunfire, and one later dies.

Potential Liability Analysis:
  • Personal Civil Liability: The officer's actions would be scrutinized for reasonableness under the circumstances. Was the use of deadly force justified? Did the officer consider the backdrop and potential for injuring bystanders?
  • Agency Liability: The agency could be held liable if its training on use of force, particularly in crowded environments, was inadequate.
  • Criminal Considerations: The conduct would be evaluated under PC 9.05 (Reckless Injury of Innocent Third Person) and PC 9.06 (Civil Remedies Unaffected). Even if the use of force was justified against the suspect, the officer can still be held civilly liable for reckless injury to an innocent third person.

Case Study 2: In-Custody Death

Scenario: After a struggle, officers secure a suspect in a patrol car. In violation of policy, they leave the suspect unobserved while they get a soda. They return to find the suspect has died of a heart attack.

Potential Liability Analysis:
  • Officer Liability (Civil): The officers were negligent by violating a policy designed for suspect safety. Their failure to maintain constant observation directly contradicts the agency's required standard of care.
  • Agency Liability: The agency may be liable if the policy was unclear, or if there was a pattern of such policy violations being ignored (failure to supervise/discipline).
  • Criminal Liability: The officers' actions could be investigated for a potential offense under PC Chapter 19 (Criminal Homicide), such as Criminally Negligent Homicide, if their failure to render aid and observe the prisoner constituted a gross deviation from the standard of care.

Criminal Law vs. Civil Law

Criminal Law

  • Purpose: Regulates conduct considered unacceptable by the state.
  • Violation: Called a "crime," committed "Against the Peace and Dignity of the State."
  • Parties: The State vs. an individual defendant.
  • Burden of Proof: Proof Beyond a Reasonable Doubt.
  • Penalty: Fine, imprisonment, or execution.
  • Governing Code: Texas Penal Code, Code of Criminal Procedure.

Civil Law

  • Purpose: Resolves legal disputes between two individual parties.
  • Violation: Called a "tort" or legal harm.
  • Parties: Plaintiff (aggrieved party) vs. Defendant.
  • Burden of Proof: Preponderance of the Evidence.
  • Remedy: Usually monetary compensation or an injunction.
  • Governing Code: Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code, Rules of Civil Procedure.

Practical Example: An officer responds to a dispute where a neighbor opened the wrong irrigation gate, flooding and damaging another's pecan trees. The officer should advise the property owner that this is a civil matter (a tort against property). The owner can file a civil suit seeking compensation for the damaged trees. However, if the neighbor acted intentionally or knowingly, it could also be a criminal offense (Criminal Mischief, PC Chap 28), and criminal charges could be filed by the state.

Texas Courts & Jurisdiction

Creation of Courts

  • Constitutional Courts: Created by the Texas Constitution.
  • Courts at Law: Created by acts of the state legislature.
  • Municipal Courts: Created by acts of a chartered municipality's governing body.

Jurisdiction

The power of a court to hear a case. In criminal cases, it's determined by the severity of the offense. In civil cases, it's determined by the amount of compensation sought or the area of law.

Practical Example: A deputy attempts to seize property based on a District Court order for a $125,000 judgment. The property owner resists. The deputy has authority because a District Court has civil jurisdiction over cases with amounts in controversy of that size. The owner's resistance could lead to criminal charges or contempt of court.

Contempt of Court (GC 21.002)

Conduct that obstructs the proper administration of justice or brings the authority of the law into disrespect.

Direct Contempt

Occurs in the presence of the court.

Constructive (Indirect) Contempt

Occurs outside the presence of the court. Requires a formal hearing to consider evidence.

Punishments:

  • Higher Courts (District, County): Fine up to $500, jail up to 6 months, or both.
  • Lower Courts (JP, Municipal): Fine up to $100, jail up to 3 days, or both.

The Civil Action Process

A step-by-step overview of how a typical civil lawsuit proceeds.

  1. Petition: An aggrieved party (Plaintiff) files a petition with the court clerk, detailing the tort and the requested remedy.
  2. Citation: The party alleged to have caused the harm (Defendant) receives legal notice of the suit via service of a citation.
  3. Answer: The defendant files an "answer" with the court, responding to the plaintiff's claims.
  4. Default Judgment: If the defendant fails to answer, the court may render a default judgment in favor of the plaintiff.
  5. Trial/Settlement: If the parties cannot settle, the case may go to trial, where a court renders a decision.
  6. Judgment: The final decision of the court, which instructs a party on actions to be taken. It is enforceable by law.
