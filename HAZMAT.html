Hazardous Materials (HAZMAT)

Official DOT Definition:

A substance or material capable of posing an unreasonable risk to health, safety, or property when transported in commerce. The term includes hazardous substances, hazardous wastes, marine pollutants, elevated temperature materials, and materials designated as hazardous in the Hazardous Materials Table (See 49 CFR 172.101).

CBRNE

A simplified way to group hazardous materials that is useful for first responders.

Elements Checklist (The Components & Examples):

  • Chemical: Toxic/Poisonous substances (e.g., chlorine, pesticides, nerve agents), Flammables (e.g., gasoline, propane), Oxidizers (e.g., oxygen, hydrogen peroxide), and Corrosives (e.g., sulfuric acid).
  • Biological: Living organisms or their toxins that cause injury or death (e.g., bacteria like Anthrax, viruses like Smallpox, biological toxins like Ricin).
  • Radiological: Materials that emit ionizing radiation (e.g., Alpha particles, Beta particles, Gamma rays).
  • Nuclear: Involves the fission or fusion of atoms, resulting in a powerful explosion and radiation.
  • Explosive: Materials that burn or decompose quickly, releasing a large amount of heat and gas (e.g., dynamite, C4, ANFO).

Basic Toxicology Terms

Toxicology: The study of adverse effects of chemicals or physical agents on living organisms.

Acute vs. Chronic Exposure: Acute is a short-term, high-dose event with harmful consequences. Chronic is a long-term, low-dose exposure with harmful consequences.

Threshold Limit Value (TLV): The level a worker can be exposed to day after day for a working lifetime without adverse health effects.

Immediately Dangerous to Life and Health (IDLH): Exposure likely to cause death, permanent adverse health effects, or prevent escape from such an environment.

Lethal Concentration (LC50): The concentration of a substance in the air required to kill half of a tested population.

Lethal Dose (LD50): The dose of a substance required to kill half of a tested population.

Material Safety Data Sheets (MSDS/SDS): A document with information on potential health effects and safe handling procedures for chemical products.

Radioactive Exposure: The level of radiation flux to which material or living tissue is exposed.

Common Routes of Exposure

Elements Checklist:

  • Inhalation: Breathing in hazardous materials (gases, vapors, aerosols). The most common and rapid route.
  • Absorption: The material passes through skin, eyes, or mucous membranes.
  • Ingestion: Taking the material into the body through the mouth, either by eating/drinking a contaminated product (primary) or transferring it from a contaminated object (secondary).
  • Injection: The material enters the body through a puncture, cut, or abrasion.

Potential Effects of a CBRNE Incident

Health Impacts:

  • Death
  • Temporary impairment
  • Permanent disability
  • Psychological stress (PTSD)

Property & Environment:

  • Damage to water sources
  • Loss of productive lands
  • Destruction of food products
  • Inability to occupy structures

Infrastructure Impacts:

  • Disruption of transportation
  • Budget/resource strains
  • Reduction in services
  • Overwhelmed health care system

The RAIN Principle

The basic procedure for safeguarding lives at a CBRNE event.

Recognize: Recognize the presence of a CBRNE threat.

  • Observations: Use your senses (odors, noises) and look for indicators (placards, signs, wind direction).
  • Assessment: Evaluate hazard level, casualties, and need for additional resources.
  • Plan: Formulate a response, exit strategy, and precautions.
  • Communicate: Relay information to dispatch, casualties, and others.
  • Respond: Establish perimeter, render first aid, and enable ICS.

Avoid: Avoid contamination and exposure. Stay uphill, upwind, and use distance and physical barriers. Be alert to changes in wind direction.

Isolate: Isolate the threat and the immediate area. Set and enforce a perimeter to limit the spread of contamination.

Notify: Notify the appropriate response agency. Stay outside the perimeter and wait for qualified assistance.

Sources of On-Site Information

Use these sources to identify the hazardous materials involved.

  • Warnings and Indicators: Shipping manifests, placards, and container labels. The Emergency Response Guidebook (ERG) is a key tool for interpreting these.
  • Interviews: Question the driver, witnesses, and victims about the materials.
  • Communications: Use TLETS to get information, and contact the shipper at the point-of-origin if possible.

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Warning: Only those trained in the use of PPE should attempt to use it. Law enforcement responders should not use any PPE above gloves and a particulate/droplet mask, except to escape a hazardous situation.

Levels of Personal Protection:

  • Level D: Basic work uniform, gloves, mask.
  • Level C: Air-purifying respirator, chemical-resistant clothing, double-layered gloves.
  • Level B: Self-Contained Breathing Apparatus (SCBA) + Level C protection.
  • Level A: Fully encapsulated, vapor-tight suit. Highest level of protection.

Decontamination (Decon) Concepts

The goal is to "End the Exposure!"

Elements Checklist (Decon Methods):

  • Remove: Remove the contaminated clothing and items.
  • Dilute: Flush with large quantities of water.
  • Absorb: Use absorbent materials like sand or clay.
  • Neutralize: Use chemicals to make the substance less harmful (for trained personnel only).
  • Isolate: Contain the contaminated items.

National Incident Management System (NIMS)

FEMA Definition:

A systematic, proactive approach to guide departments and agencies at all levels to work together seamlessly and manage incidents involving all threats and hazards.

Key Benefits:

  • Provides a unified approach and standardized command structure.
  • Emphasizes preparedness, mutual aid, and resource management.
  • Scalable and flexible for all incidents.

Incident Command System (ICS)

Definition:

A standardized on-scene emergency management system designed to enable effective and efficient incident management by integrating facilities, equipment, personnel, procedures, and communications within a common organizational structure.

ICS is a key component of NIMS, used by public and private agencies to organize field-level operations.
