Fitness & Wellness Concepts

Physical Fitness: The condition of the body that enables an individual to use their body in activities without undue fatigue and exhaustion. It equates to performance and readiness.

Wellness: Consists of a person's health/disease status and risk potential. It's a state of positive well-being, not just the absence of disease. A person can be physically fit but not "well".

Law Enforcement Fitness Status: Officers are often hired at an average or above-average fitness level, but incumbent field officers are generally below average in fitness compared to the general population.

The Wellness Continuum

Improving Your Position on the Wellness Continuum:

  • Stop harmful drugs, smoking, and chewing tobacco.
  • Limit alcohol.
  • Exercise regularly.
  • Eat less animal fat, cholesterol, and sodium.
  • Eat more complex carbohydrates and drink more water.
  • Achieve and maintain a healthy body weight.
  • Get regular medical check-ups and self-exams.

Principles of an Exercise Program

Progressive Overload

For fitness to improve, the demands placed on the body must be greater than those of daily activities. As the body adapts, the demand must be gradually increased.

Regularity and Recovery

An exercise program should be consistent, but also provide time for the body to recover. Fitness begins to decline after 96 hours of inactivity. Allow 48 hours between heavy exercises targeting the same muscle groups.

Specificity

The body adapts specifically to the type of demand placed on it. For example, running improves endurance, while strength training improves the ability to handle physical encounters.

Balance

The program should work opposing muscles (e.g., biceps and triceps) and include a balance of cardiovascular, strength, and flexibility training.

FITT Guidelines for Training

Cardiovascular Training

Purpose: Improve efficiency of heart, lungs, and blood vessels. Controls weight, increases energy and endurance.

  • Frequency: Minimum 3 days a week.
  • Intensity: Target Heart Rate (THR) zone of 60-80% of Predicted Maximum.
  • Time: Minimum of 20 minutes at THR.
  • Type: Aerobic exercises (running, swimming, cycling).

Strength Training

Purpose: Increase lean muscle mass and strength; enhance performance, decrease injuries, and improve professional presence.

  • Frequency: 2-4 days a week (allow 48 hours rest for same muscle groups).
  • Intensity: Muscle endurance (low weight, 12-20 reps) or muscle strength (high weight, 8-12 reps).
  • Time: Time to complete 1-3 sets.
  • Type: Weight training or calisthenics.

Flexibility Training

Purpose: Prevent injuries, improve posture, reduce soreness, and increase range of motion.

  • Frequency: 3-7 days a week.
  • Intensity: Hold to an easy point of tension (do not bounce).
  • Time: 10-20 seconds per stretch.
  • Type: Best done after cardiovascular exercise. Slow, sustained static stretches.

Anaerobic Training

Purpose: Increase ability for short, intense bursts of maximum effort (sprinting, pushing, defensive tactics). Essential for officer survival.

  • Frequency: 1-2 days a week.
  • Intensity: All-out effort (90% of Max HR).
  • Time: Repeat short bursts (20-60 seconds) followed by rest.
  • Type: Sprints (running, cycling, stair climbing).

Nutrition and Performance

Proper nutrition is key to performance. The right fuel ensures energy for essential job tasks, such as producing a 100% energy output for a few seconds, fueling an 18-hour search and rescue shift, or staying alert in the early morning hours.

Fuel Nutrients

  • Carbohydrates (55-60%): Body's preferred energy source. Complex carbs (potatoes, whole grains) provide slow, gradual energy. Simple carbs (sweets) provide quick but short-lived energy.
  • Protein (10-15%): "Building blocks" for repairing tissue. Slower energy source than carbs, but lasts longer.
  • Fats (15-30%): Concentrated energy source. Choose unsaturated fats (olive oil, avocados) over saturated fats. Unsaturated fats help control cholesterol.

Cholesterol & Non-Fuel Nutrients

  • HDL Cholesterol: "Good" cholesterol that helps clear arteries.
  • LDL Cholesterol: "Bad" cholesterol that tends to clog arteries.
  • Vitamins & Minerals: Essential for metabolism and growth.
  • Water: The most important mineral. Drink 6-8 glasses per day.

Healthy Nutritional Strategies

Dietary Guidelines:

Foods to Increase:
  • Make half your plate fruits & vegetables.
  • Make half your grains whole grains.
  • Switch to fat-free or low-fat milk.
Foods to Reduce:
  • Foods high in sodium (soups, frozen meals).
  • Sugary drinks (drink water instead).
  • Saturated fat, cholesterol, and trans fats.

The 80/20 Rule: Eat healthy 80% of the time, but allow for "treats" 20% of the time to make a healthy plan sustainable.

Understanding Stress

Concept Definition:

Stress is the natural reaction of the mind and body to a demand placed on it. It's not inherently bad; unmanaged stress, however, can seriously affect physical and mental well-being.

Types of Stress (Checklist):

  • Eustress: Positive stress (promotion, marriage, achievement).
  • Distress: Negative stress (injury, illness, divorce).
  • Acute Stress: Short-term with a quick resolution (a threatening dog). The body handles this best.
  • Chronic Stress: Prolonged, unrelieved stress (cumulative stress) that leads to wear and tear.

The "Fight or Flight" Response & Symptoms

When the Central Nervous System perceives a threat, it activates the Sympathetic Nervous System (accelerator) and reduces the Parasympathetic Nervous System (brake).

Long-Term Physical Symptoms

  • Digestive disorders
  • Headaches
  • Excessive Illnesses
  • High Blood Pressure
  • Sleep Disorders

Cognitive/Emotional Symptoms

  • Cognitive Impairment (impaired decision making, memory)
  • Alcoholism / Suicide
  • Abrupt change in behavior
  • Rapid mood changes
  • Overly suspicious or irritable

Stress Management Strategies

Three Basic Approaches: Avoid, Alter, Adapt/Accept

Avoid: Reduce exposure to stressors (e.g., leave early to avoid traffic, set boundaries, say no).

Alter: Minimize the physiological response by changing something (e.g., problem solving, direct communication).

Adapt/Accept: Build resistance or change perspective.

  • Psychological: Nurture hobbies, take time away from work.
  • Physical: Get rest, exercise to "play out" the fight-or-flight response, eat well.
  • Social: Engage with family, friends, and community.
  • Spiritual/Faith: Nurture this area to give meaning to life.
  • Change Perspective: Use positive self-talk and humor.

Hypervigilance Rollercoaster

Hypervigilance is the elevated alertness required for officer survival. Off-duty, this can lead to a "crash," where the officer becomes fatigued, detached, and emotionally withdrawn as the Parasympathetic system takes over. This imbalance can cause problems in personal relationships and lead to the officer's primary identity revolving around their job.

Critical Incidents & PTSD

Concept Definitions:

  • Critical Incident Stress (CIS): A "normal" response to an abnormal, extreme event. Causes psychological and physical discomfort for 2 days to 1 month.
  • Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD): Occurs when CIS symptoms are prolonged past 1 month. The person is unable to make the event part of their past and may require professional help. The response to a similar stressor is similar in intensity to the original event (e.g., a car backfiring triggers the same fear as the original gunfight, not just a memory).

Effects of Stress on Performance

The "Inverted U Principle" shows that as heart rate increases, performance improves, then drops.

Symptoms of High Heart Rate Engagement:

  • ~115 BPM: Fine motor skills deteriorate.
  • ~145 BPM: Complex motor skills deteriorate.
  • ~175+ BPM: Auditory changes, tunnel vision, loss of depth perception. Time may appear to slow down. Irrational behavior may occur.
  • Memory Lapses: It may be difficult to remember details like number of shots fired. Most memory returns in 24-48 hours. Reports written during this time should be considered preliminary.

Post-Incident Support & Suicide Awareness

How to Assist a Coworker After a Critical Incident

  • Assist with basic needs first (ensure safety, notify family).
  • Provide physical presence; listen without offering answers.
  • Give continued reassurance that what they are experiencing is normal.
  • Assist in keeping a routine (rest, exercise, eating).
  • Connect them with resources (CISM, EAP, Peer Support).

Suicide Statistics

  • 90% of people who die by suicide have a diagnosable/treatable disorder.
  • Men are nearly 4x more likely to die by suicide than women.
  • Leading causes for officer suicide are marital/relational problems and legal trouble.
  • Rate increases drastically at retirement.

Alcohol & Drug Abuse in Law Enforcement

Law enforcement is not immune to substance abuse. High levels of stress, peer pressure, and isolation can be risk factors. Hypervigilance can also drive officers to seek extreme means (like alcohol) to relax.

Signs and Symptoms of Abuse:

  • Failed attempts to cut back or quit.
  • Declining performance at work.
  • Feeling guilty about use or getting angry when confronted.
  • Hiding alcohol or drugs.
  • Blackouts or lapses in memory.
  • Relationship, financial, or legal problems related to use.

Myths & Realities of Alcoholism

Myth: Once an alcoholic, always an alcoholic.

Reality: Alcoholism is a disease for which recovery is possible with a dedicated program.

Myth: Alcohol helps to sleep.

Reality: It may help with sleep onset, but it interferes with necessary REM sleep, causing impairment.

Myth: All people experiencing alcoholism are "skid-row drunks."

Reality: Many hold high-level jobs and function well for years before their performance is noticeably affected.

Assisting a Coworker

The responsibility of an officer is twofold: to evaluate their own use and to assist coworkers who may have a problem. Avoiding the problem only contributes to the abuser's denial.
