Three Basic Concepts of Weaponless Strategies

Self-Control

The police role is to protect the public and take the violator into custody. Officers must maintain physical and emotional control to ensure safety for all parties.

Balance

Mental balance is preparing to control your emotions and the situation. Physical balance is a position that allows movement while maintaining strength and advantage (e.g., bladed stance).

Awareness

Observe the entire situation: suspect's hands, weapons, associates, escape routes, and your own footing.

Principles of Self-Defense

Elements Checklist:

  • Prevention: The best strategy is to prevent an attack from happening.
  • Awareness of Dangers: Be constantly aware of potential threats in your environment.
  • Maintain Distance: Stay just outside a suspect's reach to allow for adequate reaction time.
  • Avoid Overextending: Do not compromise your balance or position by reaching too far.
  • Move Out of the Line of Force: If attacked, it's better to move laterally out of the attack's path rather than trying to stop it head-on.

Practical Example: Proper Interview Stance

An officer conducts an interview with a cooperative but nervous subject. The officer demonstrates a proper interview stance by:

  • Blading their body at a 45-degree angle.
  • Keeping their reaction hand (non-gun side) closer to the subject.
  • Maintaining a balanced position that allows for quick movement.
  • Keeping their gun side away from the subject.

Weaponless Defense Strategies

Standing Techniques

  • Touching
  • Striking (open hand and closed fist)
  • Joint locking
  • Pressure points
  • Hand strikes and foot strikes
  • Blocks (using hands, arms, legs)

Ground Techniques

  • Base get up
  • Full guard position
  • Full mount position
  • Trap and roll
  • Side mount

Less-Lethal Force Weapons

These are chemical, electrical, or kinetic weapons used to gain control of a situation and are a step above weaponless strategies but below deadly force. A tactical transition to one of these weapons may be necessary. (PC 9.55)

  • Chemical & Electrical Devices: Taser, OC pepper spray, similar weapons.
  • Impact Weapons: Straight baton, expandable baton, side-handle baton.

Impact Weapons (Baton)

A baton is classified as a weapon capable of inflicting serious bodily injury or death. Its use is justified when force greater than weaponless strategies is needed, but less than deadly force is required. (PC 46.01(1), PC 46.15)

Situations Necessitating Baton Use:

  • For a tactical squad in crowd/riot control.
  • When attacked by a suspect with a non-firearm weapon.
  • When assaulted by an unarmed suspect to disable or defend.
  • When confronted by multiple threatening suspects.
  • When a suspect who has committed a crime refuses verbal commands.

Justification for Baton Use (Checklist):

The officer carries the burden to justify baton use based on the totality of the circumstances. (PC 9.51) Factors include:

  • The physical stature of the suspect compared to the officer.
  • The suspect's skill level (e.g., martial arts) or history of violence.
  • The suspect's assumption of an aggressive stance.
  • The suspect's inability to be restrained by lesser means.

Guidelines for Baton Use

  • Position the baton between yourself and the suspect.
  • Maintain a good defensive position.
  • Do not intentionally strike the head or throat unless deadly force is justified.

Strike & Non-Strike Areas

STRIKE AREAS

  • Center mass of arms
  • Center mass of legs
  • Abdomen

NON-STRIKE AREAS

  • Groin, Center of back/kidneys, Chest
  • Anything above the plane of the shoulders

Basic Concepts of Weapons Retention

The high number of officers shot with their own weapon highlights the critical need for training in weapons retention. The goal is to maintain possession of your weapon(s) during a physical confrontation.

Elements Checklist:

  • Holstered Retention: Techniques to keep the weapon secured in the holster when a suspect is attempting to take it.
  • Un-holstered Retention: Techniques to maintain control of the weapon once it has been drawn.
  • Positional Awareness: Keeping your weapon side away from a suspect during an approach or interview.
  • Multi-Weapon Retention: Retention skills should apply to handguns, rifles, shotguns, and intermediate weapons from all angles and positions (standing, kneeling, prone).

Practical Example: Attempted Disarm

An officer is clearing an unsecured building after a report of an open door. While moving through a hallway, a suspect lunges from a side room and grabs the officer's holstered handgun.

Required Action: The student officer must immediately demonstrate proper holstered weapon retention techniques to defeat the suspect's attempt to disarm them. This could involve using their body weight to pin the weapon, striking the suspect to create distance, and maintaining control until the threat is neutralized.

Deadly vs. Non-Deadly Force

Use of Non-Deadly Force

Legal Definition: Force not likely to cause death or great bodily harm. It can be used when you reasonably believe it's necessary to defend yourself or another against an attacker's imminent use of unlawful force.

Use of Deadly Force

Legal Definition: Peace officers may use deadly force to protect themselves or others when and to the degree they reasonably believe an immediate threat of death or serious bodily injury exists.

Applying Deadly Force Concepts

The concept of deadly force is not limited to firearms. Any object or action can constitute deadly force if it is used in a manner capable of causing death or serious bodily injury. Officers must consider this for:

  • Empty Hand Techniques: Strikes to the head, throat, or spine could be considered deadly force.
  • Control Weapons (Batons): Intentionally striking the head, throat, or other vital areas with an impact weapon constitutes deadly force.
  • Chemical & Electrical Devices: While generally less-lethal, prolonged or improper use against a vulnerable person could potentially be construed as deadly force.
  • Firearms: Any discharge of a firearm at a person is considered deadly force.
  • Vehicles: Using a vehicle to ram another vehicle or drive towards a person is considered deadly force.

Relevant Statutes for Deadly Force

  • PC 9.32 Deadly Force in Defense of Person: Governs the use of deadly force to protect oneself.
  • PC 9.33 Defense of Third Person: Governs the use of deadly force to protect another person.
  • PC 9.51(a) and (g) Arrest and Search: Specifies when force and deadly force may be used in making an arrest or preventing an escape.
  • PC 22.05 Deadly Conduct: Defines the offense of recklessly engaging in conduct that places another in imminent danger of serious bodily injury.

Discretionary Authority & Risk Factors

Factors Influencing Discretion

An officer must use discretion in arrest vs. non-arrest situations, guided by law and circumstances.

  • The likelihood of behavioral disorders in the suspect.
  • Legal mandates, such as CCP 14.06(b) which requires taking an offender before a magistrate.

Risk Factors in an Encounter

Officers must constantly assess risk factors to maintain control.

  • Suspect's mental attitude / behavioral disorders.
  • Time and location of the encounter.
  • Intoxication (suspect).
  • Presence of a supportive crowd toward the suspect.
  • Officer's own attitude.

Approach, Confrontation & Frisk

Direct Approach

Advantage: Observe all movements.
Disadvantage: Lose surprise, vulnerable to resistance.

Rear Approach

Advantage: Surprise, reduces direct attack.
Disadvantage: May provoke defensive response.

Side Approach

Advantage: Suspect is off-balance.
Disadvantage: Cannot observe all movement.

Interviewing & Physical Contact

  • Interview Position: Gun side away, non-restricted stance, hands free. Two officers use a 'V' position with one in command.
  • Physical Contact Position: Approach from the side or rear when possible to keep the subject off-balance.
  • Verbal Commands: Keep the subject verbally informed of your actions and expectations.

Conducting a Frisk:

  • Justification: A frisk is justified for weapons to ensure officer and/or public safety.
  • Procedure: It is a "pat down" of the outer clothing, not a full search.
  • Considerations: Stress safety, be aware of clothing type (heavy vs. light), and adapt technique for one vs. two officers.

Handcuffing Procedures

Application Checklist:

  • Apply from a position of advantage (Standing, Sitting, Prone, Kneeling).
  • Hands to the rear, palms facing out.
  • Check for tightness (one fingertip space).
  • Handcuffs are double-locked.
  • Advise the subject of the reason for arrest to reduce confusion.

Handcuff Basics:

  • Know handcuff nomenclature (parts).
  • Follow department policy for method of carry.
  • Perform regular handcuff maintenance.

Special Handcuffing Scenarios:

  • Two Suspects, One Set of Cuffs: Cuff right hand to right hand OR left hand to left hand to impede running.
  • Two Suspects, Two Sets of Cuffs: Place suspects hands-to-rear and interlock their arms before cuffing.
  • Supplements: In an emergency, items like a tie, belt, shoelaces, or plastic cuffs can be used.

Remember, handcuffs are a temporary restraining device only!

Physical Search, Escort & Transport

Searching Suspects

  • Eyes on Subject: Let your hands do the searching; keep your eyes on the subject.
  • Control Advantage: Holding the suspect's waistband from the rear allows you to pull them off-balance.
  • Search Methods: Standing, Prone, or Kneeling searches. Wall searches are high-risk.

Escorting & Transporting

  • Search Vehicle First: Before shift and after every transport.
  • Walking Escorts: Follow policy for various officer/suspect combinations (1-on-1, 1-on-2, 2-on-1, 2-on-2).
  • Vehicle Transport: Adhere to agency policy. A common safe practice for two officers and one suspect is placing the suspect in the right rear, with the second officer in the left rear.

Components of a Successful Arrest

A successful arrest is a combination of multiple skills applied effectively and safely.

  • Control: Keeping the subject in sight and using proper escort positions.
  • Communication: Using clear verbal commands throughout the process.
  • Restraint: Applying handcuffs correctly and safely.
  • Positioning: Safely and efficiently moving a cuffed suspect from prone or kneeling to standing.
  • Assessment: Constantly assessing for safety, communication opportunities, and de-escalation.
  • Transport: Moving the suspect securely according to policy.
