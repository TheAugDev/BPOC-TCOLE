Courtroom Testimony

TCOLE Preparation

Preparing for Testimony

Good work in the field leads to success in court. Your reputation and the department's can be enhanced or diminished by your appearance and testimony.

Elements Checklist (Preparation Steps):

  • Review Case Materials: Read all your reports and watch all associated videos.
  • Meet with the Prosecutor: Discuss evidence, conclusions, strengths, and weaknesses of the case.
  • Review Technical Information: Go over photos, lab reports, and your original notes.
  • Anticipate Questions: Plan your responses to likely questions from both the prosecution and defense.
  • Use of Notes: You may use notes to refresh your memory if they were prepared at or near the time of the investigation. However, if used, they are subject to cross-examination and may be admitted into evidence.

Courtroom Demeanor & Appearance

First impressions are critical. Your appearance and conduct will be examined and will impact your credibility.

Appearance:

  • Attire: Wear a dress uniform or professional business attire.
  • Arrival: Enter the courtroom confidently. Walk directly to the witness stand. Do not look at the accused or the jury.

Demeanor:

  • Be cool, calm, collected, and knowledgeable.
  • Remain articulate, impartial, and non-argumentative.
  • Be prepared and know the facts of the case.

Courtroom Roles

Judge/Magistrate: Presides over the trial, rules on evidence, instructs the jury.

Prosecutor: Represents "The State" and must prove every element of the offense.

Defense Counsel: Represents the defendant.

Court Reporter: Transcribes the official record of the proceedings.

Bailiff: Maintains order in the courtroom.

Jury: The trier of fact.

Phases of a Trial

  1. Jury Selection (Voir Dire): Attorneys question potential jurors.
  2. Opening Statements: Attorneys for each side outline their case.
  3. Direct Examination: The prosecutor elicits facts from their witness (you).
  4. Cross Examination: The defense counsel questions the witness.
  5. Redirect / Re-cross Examination: The attorneys may ask follow-up questions to clarify points. This cycle can continue several times.
  6. Closing Arguments: Attorneys summarize their cases.
  7. Jury Deliberation & Finding: The jury decides the verdict.

Handling Objections: If an attorney objects to a question, you must stop talking immediately and wait for the judge to rule. The judge will say either "Sustained" (you may not answer) or "Overruled" (you may answer).

The "Rule" - Exclusion of Witnesses (TRE 614, CCP 36.03)

Legal Definition:

At a party's request, the court must order witnesses excluded so that they cannot hear other witnesses' testimony. This is often called "invoking the rule."

Elements Checklist (What to do when "under the rule"):

  • Leave the courtroom immediately when instructed.
  • Stay close to the courtroom so you can be called when it is your turn.
  • Do not discuss the case or anyone's testimony with other witnesses or jurors.
  • Do not read any report of or comment upon the testimony in the case.
  • After testifying, leave the courtroom again unless instructed otherwise.

Practical Example: At the start of a trial, the defense attorney says, "Your honor, we invoke the rule." The judge then orders all witnesses in the case, including all police officers who will testify, to leave the courtroom and wait in the hallway. The officers must not talk to each other about what they are going to say on the stand.

Principles of Witnessmanship

These principles help maintain your credibility on the stand.

Honesty: Display a sincere interest in accuracy. Present a modest demeanor.

Brevity: Be brief and to the point. Do not volunteer information or argue with attorneys.

Clarity: Avoid errors, inconsistencies, and confusion, as they undermine your credibility.

Objectivity: Present an impartial, conscientious picture. Be courteous to both attorneys.

Emotional Control: Ignore insults, badgering, and innuendos. A display of anger diminishes credibility.

Responding to Questions

Answer only the questions you are qualified to answer and stay within the limits of your known information.

  • "I don't know": Use this when the facts were not observed directly. It is a complete answer.
  • "I don't remember" / "That is all I can recall": Use these statements when you are unsure or have exhausted your memory on a point.
  • Avoid Speculation: Stick to the facts. If asked for an opinion, ensure it is based on facts and make the distinction clear.
  • Pause Before Answering: When the defense attorney asks a question, pause long enough to give the prosecutor time to object.

Defense Attorney Tactics

The defense's goal is often to challenge your credibility and create reasonable doubt. Be prepared for these common tactics.

Offensive: Rapid-fire questions intended to confuse you. Your Response: Stay calm, take time to consider each question, and ask for it to be repeated if necessary.

Condescending: An overly benevolent or patronizing tone to make you seem inept. Your Response: Ask for improperly phrased questions to be repeated, then answer in a confident, decisive manner.

Friendly: An overly courteous approach to lull you into a false sense of security. Your Response: Stay alert and remember their goal is to diminish your testimony.

Badgering / Belligerent: An aggressive approach intended to provoke you into losing emotional control. Your Response: Ignore the behavior, stay calm, speak deliberately, and give the prosecutor time to object.

Perjury (PC 37.02)

Legal Definition:

A person commits an offense if, with intent to deceive and with knowledge of the statement's meaning, he makes a false statement under oath or swears to the truth of a false statement previously made and the statement is required or authorized by law to be made under oath.

Punishment Level:

Class A Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • With intent to deceive and knowledge of the meaning
  • Makes a false statement under oath (or swears to a previous false statement)
  • The statement is required or authorized by law to be made under oath.

Practical Example: During the police hiring process, an applicant signs an official background statement, sworn before a notary, falsely claiming they have no criminal record above a Class C misdemeanor. An investigator discovers a prior conviction for DWI, a Class B Misdemeanor.

Aggravated Perjury (PC 37.03)

Legal Definition:

A person commits an offense if he commits perjury as defined in Section 37.02, and the false statement is made during or in connection with an official proceeding AND is material.

Punishment Level:

Felony of the 3rd Degree.

Elements Checklist:

  • Commits the offense of Perjury, AND
  • The false statement is made during or in connection with an official proceeding (e.g., a trial, grand jury hearing), AND
  • The statement is "material" (it could have affected the course or outcome of the proceeding, per PC 37.04).

Practical Example: During a murder trial, a jailhouse informant testifies under oath that the defendant confessed to him. It is later discovered through jail records that the informant and the defendant were never housed together and could not have had contact. The false testimony was material because it could have directly influenced the jury's verdict.
