PC Ch. 9: Justification Excluding Criminal Responsibility

Chapter 9 of the Texas Penal Code outlines the circumstances under which conduct, including the use of force, is justified by law. It provides a defense to prosecution, meaning that if the conduct meets these criteria, it is not a criminal offense.

PC ยง9.01 - Definitions: "Deadly Force"

Definition

"Deadly Force" means force that is intended or known by the actor to cause, or in the manner of its use or intended use is capable of causing, death or serious bodily injury.

Elements Checklist:

  • Force is intended or known to cause death/SBI, OR
  • The way the force is used makes it capable of causing death/SBI.

Practical Example: Firing a handgun at a person is deadly force. Striking someone in the head with a heavy iron pipe is also deadly force, because the manner of its use is capable of causing death or serious bodily injury.

PC ยง9.21 - Public Duty

Justification / Defense

Conduct is justified if the actor reasonably believes the conduct is required or authorized by law, by the judgment or order of a competent court, or in the execution of legal process.

Elements Checklist:

  • Actor reasonably believes conduct is required/authorized by law.
  • For example, executing a lawful arrest warrant.

Practical Example: A peace officer acting under a valid arrest warrant uses the force necessary to enter a home and effect the arrest. The conduct of entering and arresting is justified under public duty.

View Statute

PC ยง9.22 - Necessity

Justification / Defense

Conduct is justified if the actor reasonably believes the conduct is immediately necessary to avoid imminent harm, and the desirability and urgency of avoiding the harm clearly outweigh the harm sought to be prevented by the law proscribing the conduct.

Elements Checklist:

  • Actor reasonably believes conduct is immediately necessary.
  • To avoid imminent harm.
  • The urgency to avoid the harm outweighs the harm of the illegal act.

Practical Example: An officer, seeing a child locked in a hot car who is clearly in distress, breaks the car window to rescue the child. The act of breaking the window (criminal mischief) is justified by the necessity of preventing imminent serious bodily injury or death to the child.

View Statute

PC ยง9.31 - Self-Defense

Justification / Defense

A person is justified in using force against another when and to the degree the actor reasonably believes the force is immediately necessary to protect the actor against the other's use or attempted use of unlawful force.

Elements Checklist:

  • Actor reasonably believes force is needed.
  • Force is immediately necessary.
  • To protect against another's unlawful force.
  • The actor did not provoke the person against whom force is used.

Practical Example: A subject shoves an officer during an interview. The officer is justified in using physical force, such as a takedown, to stop the assault and gain control, as it's immediately necessary to prevent further unlawful force.

PC ยง9.32 - Deadly Force in Defense of Person

Justification / Defense

A person is justified in using deadly force against another if they would be justified in using force under ยง9.31, and when they reasonably believe deadly force is immediately necessary to protect against death or serious bodily injury.

Elements Checklist:

  • Standard self-defense is justified.
  • Reasonably believes deadly force is immediately necessary.
  • To protect against another's use/attempted use of deadly force, OR
  • To prevent an imminent aggravated kidnapping, murder, sexual assault, or robbery.

Practical Example: A suspect pulls a knife and lunges at an officer. The officer reasonably believes their life is in danger and is justified in using deadly force to stop the imminent threat of serious bodily injury or death.

PC ยง9.33 - Defense of Third Person

Justification / Defense

A person is justified in using force or deadly force against another to protect a third person if, under the circumstances as the actor reasonably believes them to be, the third person would be justified in using force or deadly force to protect himself.

Elements Checklist:

  • Actor reasonably believes force is needed to protect a third person.
  • The third person would be justified in using that level of force themselves.
  • The actor reasonably believes their intervention is immediately necessary.

Practical Example: An officer sees a suspect actively assaulting a victim on the street. The officer is justified in using the force reasonably necessary to stop the assault and protect the victim, stepping into the shoes of the victim, who would be justified in self-defense.

PC ยง9.41 & ยง9.42 - Protection of Property

Justification / Defense

A person may use force (but not deadly force) when they reasonably believe it is immediately necessary to prevent trespass or theft. Deadly force is only justified to protect property to prevent arson, robbery, burglary, theft during the nighttime, or criminal mischief during the nighttime, AND the actor reasonably believes the property cannot be protected by any other means.

Elements Checklist:

  • (Force) Reasonably believe force is immediately necessary to prevent trespass or interference with property.
  • (Deadly Force) Only justified for specific crimes (arson, robbery, night-time burglary/theft).
  • (Deadly Force) AND reasonably believe land/property cannot be protected by other means.

Practical Example: A property owner sees someone trying to steal their lawnmower during the day. They can use reasonable force to stop them. They cannot use deadly force. If they see someone breaking into their home at night to commit a theft, deadly force may be justified.

PC ยง9.51 - Arrest and Search

Justification / Defense

A peace officer is justified in using force against another when the actor reasonably believes the force is immediately necessary to make or assist in making an arrest or search, or to prevent escape after arrest.

Elements Checklist:

  • Actor is a peace officer (or assisting one).
  • Reasonably believes force is immediately necessary.
  • To make an arrest, search, or prevent escape.
  • The officer reasonably believes the arrest or search is lawful.

Practical Example: A suspect pulls away and tries to run while an officer is attempting to place them in handcuffs. The officer is justified in using the amount of force reasonably necessary, like a takedown or control hold, to complete the arrest and prevent escape.

View Statute

PC ยง9.52 - Prevention of Escape from Custody

Justification / Defense

The use of force to prevent the escape of an arrested person from custody is justifiable when the force could have been employed to effect the arrest under which the person is in custody.

Elements Checklist:

  • A person is under arrest and in custody.
  • The officer uses force to prevent an escape.
  • The level of force used would have been justified to make the initial arrest.

Practical Example: An officer is transporting an inmate who suddenly kicks out a window of the patrol car and attempts to flee. The officer is justified in using the same level of force they would have used in the original arrest to prevent the escape.

PC ยง9.61-9.63 - Special Relationships

Justification / Defense

Force is justified by those with special responsibilities, such as a parent, educator, or guardian, when they reasonably believe the force is necessary to discipline a child, maintain order, or safeguard and promote the welfare of the incompetent person.

Elements Checklist:

  • Actor is a parent, educator, or guardian with legal responsibility.
  • Force is used for discipline or to maintain order/welfare.
  • The force used is not deadly force and does not create a substantial risk of death, serious bodily injury, or disfigurement.

Practical Example: A teacher physically separates two students who are fighting in a classroom to maintain order. This use of force is justified under their role as an educator.

Key Court Cases

Graham v. Connor, 490 U.S. 386 (1989)

Established the "objective reasonableness" standard. The reasonableness of a particular use of force must be judged from the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene, rather than with 20/20 hindsight. It requires consideration of the facts and circumstances of each case, including the severity of the crime, whether the suspect poses an immediate threat, and whether they are actively resisting or attempting to evade arrest.

Tennessee v. Garner, 471 U.S. 1 (1985)

Deadly force may not be used against a fleeing felon unless it is necessary to prevent the escape AND the officer has probable cause to believe that the suspect poses a significant threat of death or serious physical injury to the officer or others. It is unconstitutional to use deadly force to stop a fleeing suspect who is unarmed and non-dangerous.

Psychological Aspects & Control

The Officer's Role is Defensive

In a physical arrest, the police role is defensiveโserving to protect the community from criminality. This initiative is not aggression, but an act to resist or prevent aggression or attack.

Concept of Control

Control is the degree of influence an officer must exert over a violator to take them safely into custody. This requires complete self-control; an officer who is not in control of their own emotions cannot effectively control a violator. The objective is to elicit cooperation.

Emotions, Behavior, and Prejudices

Arrests are emotional events. Uncertainty can lead to compensating behaviors like hesitation, verbal abuse, or unnecessary force. An officer must be aware of their own prejudices and remain firm but fair to reduce conflict.

Assessing Reasonable Force

An officer should consider the following factors when assessing the level of force that is reasonable under the circumstances:

  • Is the suspect submitting peacefully or resisting?
  • Is the suspect armed?
  • What is the nature of the crime?
  • Does the suspect have a history of violence?
  • How many suspects are involved versus how many officers are available?
  • What is the risk of injury to bystanders or other officers?

Moral Considerations & Professionalism

An officer's decision to use force, especially deadly force, is affected by multiple layers of controls and considerations:

  • Legal Standards: Is the force justifiable under statutory and case law?
  • Agency Policy: Departmental policy should be at least as restrictive as the law.
  • Informal Norms: The informal culture of law enforcement can influence decisions.
  • Individual Conscience: An officer's personal ethics and beliefs provide the final inner control.
  • A professional officer must be able to move from words to force and back to words, accurately assess situations, and articulate the reasons for their actions.

Use of Force Options

Officers have a range of tools and strategies. The appropriate level of force depends on the totality of the circumstances, particularly the subject's actions and level of resistance. A key concept is that force must be no more than what a reasonable officer would use under the same circumstances.

Professional Presence

The deterrent effect of a visible, uniformed officer.

Verbal Communications

Commands, instructions, and warnings.

Weaponless Strategies

Control holds, pressure points, takedowns.

Weapon Strategies

Baton, OC spray, ECD/Taser.

Deadly Force

Firearms and any force likely to cause death or SBI.

Principal Considerations:

  • Ineffective Control: The level of force is less than the subject's resistance.
  • Excessive Control: The level of force is unreasonably greater than the subject's resistance.
  • The force must be reasonable, lawful, and align with departmental policy.

Communication Strategies

Communication is a critical professional skill. An estimated 97% of an officer's duties involve verbal skills, while only 3% require physical force. Approximately 93% of a message's reception is based on *how* it is said (tone and body language), not *what* is said.

Best Practices for Verbal Persuasion:

  • Listen: Understand the real problem.
  • Empathize: See the situation from their perspective.
  • Ask: Use questions to gain control, buy time, and show concern.
  • Paraphrase: Repeat what you've heard to confirm understanding.
  • Summarize: State the resolution and provide the bottom line.

Types of Verbal Appeals:

  • Ethical: Based on your position as a professional officer; assures and persuades.
  • Rational: Appeals to common sense, reason, and community standards.
  • Practical: Based on an urgent need to change the circumstance; a short-term solution.
  • Personal: Addresses an individual's own needs and desires.

When Words Are Not Enough:

  • When a person seriously threatens bodily harm to an officer or others.
  • When a suspect begins walking or running away, or escapes custody.
  • When an officer is forced to repeat the same words or ideas with no effect.
  • Remember: When words and actions disagree, trust actions.

Crew Resource Management (CRM)

CRM is a management model that gives every team member an active voice in situations where human error can have devastating effects. It is designed to prevent accidents by improving communication and crew coordination.

Benefits of CRM in Law Enforcement:

  • Supports the Duty to Intervene: Encourages officers to speak up if they see a partner's emotions (anger, frustration) potentially leading to a bad decision.
  • Fosters Communication: Improves teamwork between officers and with outside resources (Fire, EMS).
  • Maintains Accountability: Every officer on a scene is responsible for what happens. CRM reinforces this shared responsibility.
  • Alleviates Emotional Responses: By involving all parties and listening to concerns, it can slow a situation down and allow rational thought to prevail over emotion.

Excessive Professional Courtesy

A major danger identified in CRM is "Excessive Professional Courtesy"โwhen a junior member of a team shows excessive deference to a senior member and fails to challenge a potentially bad decision. This was a factor in the George Floyd case, where a junior officer's concerns were dismissed by his senior Field Training Officer.

How to Intervene Using CRM (5 Steps):

  1. Get Attention: Address the individual by name/rank. ("Sarge, listen!")
  2. State Your Concern: "I am concerned for the suspect's health."
  3. State the Problem: "It looks like he is suffering from positional asphyxia."
  4. Suggest a Solution: "I think we should roll him onto his side to help him breathe."
  5. Obtain Agreement: "Does that sound good to you?"

Consequences of Excessive Force

Officers can be held personally liable for using excessive force under both state and federal law. This can include criminal charges and civil lawsuits.

Federal Criminal Liability:

  • 18 U.S.C. ยง 241 - Conspiracy Against Rights: Makes it unlawful for two or more persons to conspire to injure, oppress, threaten, or intimidate any person in the free exercise of any right secured by the Constitution.
  • 18 U.S.C. ยง 242 - Deprivation of Rights Under Color of Law: Makes it a crime for a person acting under color of any law to willfully deprive a person of a right protected by the Constitution or laws of the United States. This is the primary statute used to prosecute officers for excessive force.

State Criminal Liability:

  • PC ยง39.04 - Violation of Civil Rights of Person in Custody: An official or employee of a correctional facility commits an offense if he intentionally subjects a person in custody to bodily injury.

Duty to Intervene

An officer may face personal liability for failing to stop other officers from using excessive force in their presence. Both non-supervisory and supervisory officers have an affirmative duty to intervene.

Practical Example: Officer A sees their partner, Officer B, continuing to strike a subject who is already handcuffed and no longer resisting. Officer A has a legal duty to step in and stop Officer B's actions. Failure to do so could result in Officer A also being held liable.

Court Factors & Post-Incident Procedures

Factors Courts Use to Determine Excessive Force

  • The 4th Amendment limits the level of force to what is reasonable.
  • Reasonableness is based on the individual facts and circumstances.
  • The need for force will be evaluated against the feasibility of alternatives.
  • The extent of the injury inflicted is a key consideration.
  • An officer can be liable even for a justified use of force if their negligent conduct prior to the use of force created the danger.

Post-Incident Procedures (Garrity Warning)

Following a critical incident like an officer-involved shooting, an officer will be subject to an administrative (internal) investigation and potentially a separate criminal investigation. Under Garrity v. New Jersey, an officer can be compelled to provide a statement for the administrative investigation under threat of discipline, but that statement cannot be used against them in a criminal trial.
