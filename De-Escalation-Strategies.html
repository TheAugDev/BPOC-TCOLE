The Critical Decision-Making Model (CDM)

The CDM provides officers with an organized way of making decisions about how they will act in any situation, including situations that may involve uses of force.

Key Principles of the CDM

  • Police Ethics: Upholding the fundamental duties and moral obligations of a peace officer.
  • Agency Values: Adhering to the specific values and mission of your law enforcement agency.
  • Proportionality: Ensuring the level of force or response is reasonable in relation to the threat or incident.
  • The Sanctity of Human Life: Holding the preservation of life as the highest priority in all interactions.

The Five Steps of the CDM

Step 1: Collect Information

Gather as much information as possible from dispatch, witnesses, and your own observations before and upon arrival.

Practical Example: While en route to a "disturbance" call, you learn from dispatch it involves a person yelling in a park. You ask if weapons are involved or if the person is known to have mental health issues. This is collecting information.

Step 2: Assess Situation, Threats, and Risks

Evaluate the known information to determine the level of threat, potential risks to officers and the public, and the overall nature of the situation.

Practical Example: Arriving at the park, you see the person is unarmed, gesturing wildly, and talking to themselves, but no one else is nearby. You assess the immediate threat as low, but recognize the risk of the person's unpredictable behavior.

Step 3: Consider Police Powers and Agency Policy

Review your legal authority to act and the specific policies of your agency that apply to the situation (e.g., use of force, mental health response).

Practical Example: You consider that you have the authority to detain the individual for evaluation if they are a danger to themselves or others. You also recall your department's policy emphasizing de-escalation for individuals in apparent crisis.

Step 4: Identify Options and Determine Best Course of Action

Based on the first three steps, develop a range of possible responses and select the one that is most appropriate, safe, and consistent with your principles and policies.

Practical Example: Your options are: (A) Approach aggressively and detain immediately. (B) Keep your distance, call for a mental health officer, and attempt to start a calm conversation. You choose Option B as the best course of action.

Step 5: Act, Review, and Re-assess

Execute your chosen plan while continuously observing the situation and the subject's reactions. Be prepared to adjust your plan as new information becomes available.

Practical Example: You begin speaking to the person, who then becomes agitated by your presence. You re-assess the situation, take a step back to create more space (a tactical repositioning), and decide to wait for the specialized unit while maintaining observation.

Benefits of Using the CDM & The Role of Bias

  • Organizes Decision-Making: Provides a structured framework for thinking under pressure.
  • Promotes Better Decisions: Encourages a more thorough and thoughtful assessment, leading to safer and more effective outcomes.
  • Enhances Credibility: Assists officers in clearly and rationally explaining their actions after an incident, which increases accountability and public trust.

Considering Implicit Bias

Throughout the CDM process, and especially during Step 2 (Assess Situation), officers must be aware of and actively question their own implicit biases. These are the unconscious stereotypes that can affect our perceptions and decisions. Acknowledging the potential for bias is a critical step in ensuring that assessments are fair, objective, and based on the actual behavior of the individual, not on preconceived notions.

Responding to Persons in Behavioral Crisis

The primary mission is not to diagnose or solve the underlying issues, but to verbally defuse and stabilize the situation. The goal is to guide the person to a state where they can function and better reason, so that voluntary compliance can be achieved.

Behavioral Crisis Checklist (Common Signs):

  • Not responding to verbal commands.
  • Lack of coherence or nonsensical speech.
  • High levels of agitation or excitement.
  • Talking to themselves or reacting to unseen stimuli.
  • Poor personal hygiene (in some cases).

Best Practices for Response:

  • Prioritize De-escalation: Your top priority is to verbally defuse, stabilize, and minimize the stress level of the situation.
  • Request Backup: Call for other officers and specialized help (e.g., Mental Health Officers, EMS) early.
  • Don't Rush: Slow the situation down unless immediate action is required for safety.
  • Communicate: Use active listening and watch body language (theirs and yours).
  • Assess and Re-assess: Continuously use the CDM model to evaluate the situation as it evolves.
  • Be Respectful: Always communicate with respect; never be dismissive of their situation or feelings.

Emotional-Rational Thinking Scale

This concept illustrates the inverse relationship between intense emotions and rational thought.

  • As emotions rise, rational thinking declines. A highly emotional person is less capable of logical reasoning.
  • Lowering emotions can increase rational thought. By helping someone calm down, you can help them think more clearly and make better decisions.
  • While not always possible, attempting to lower the emotional intensity is a key de-escalation goal.

Practical Example: A person is screaming about a parking ticket. Instead of arguing about the law (appealing to logic), you say, "I can see this has really upset you. Let's talk about what happened." This acknowledges their emotion, which can help lower it and open the door for a more rational conversation.

Active Listening

Active listening means paying close attention to what others are saying and communicating non-verbally. It's a tactical advantage because it helps you gather intelligence and build rapport.

Elements Checklist:

  • The 80/20 Rule: Spend 80% of your time listening and only 20% talking.
  • Goal: Your primary goal is to understand what the person is thinking and feeling.
  • Avoid Distractions: Focus on the person, not your radio, phone, or other officers.
  • Show You're Listening: Use verbal and non-verbal cues like nodding, saying "I see," and summarizing what they said.
  • Use Silence: Don't feel the need to fill every pause. Silence can encourage the person to share more information.

Non-Verbal & Verbal Communication

Non-Verbal Skills (Body Language)

Your body language often communicates more than your words.

  • Project Calmness: A calm presence can influence others to be calm (emotional contagion).
  • Make Eye Contact: Shows you are engaged and confident.
  • Use Open Gestures: Avoid crossing your arms. Use open-handed gestures to appear non-threatening.
  • Modulate Your Voice: A controlled, lower tone of voice is more calming than a high-pitched, excited one.

Verbal Skills (What You Say)

  • Use the Team Concept: Have only one officer speak to the subject to avoid confusion.
  • Establish Rapport: Introduce yourself and try to find common ground.
  • Ask Clear Questions: Make requests one at a time. Use open-ended "what" and "how" questions to encourage conversation, but do not dominate it.
  • Provide Options: Giving a person choices can empower them and lead to voluntary compliance.
  • Emotional Contagion: Be aware that your emotions—positive or negative—can be transferred to the person you are interacting with.

Alternatives to Hostile Phrases

Changing your wording can dramatically alter the tone of an interaction.

  • Instead of: "What's your problem?"
    Try: "What can I do to help you?"
  • Instead of: "Calm down!"
    Try: "I see that you're upset. Please tell me about it so I can understand."
  • Instead of: "You need to leave now."
    Try: "Is there somewhere else we can go to talk about this more privately?"

Key Concepts for Incident Response (Review)

Successfully navigating a critical incident relies on the seamless integration of several core skills. Before acting, an officer should mentally review these key concepts:

  • Critical Decision-Making Model (CDM): The foundational framework for all actions.
  • Crisis Recognition and Response: The ability to identify a behavioral crisis and apply specialized communication techniques.
  • Tactical Communication: Using verbal and non-verbal skills to achieve a tactical advantage and promote de-escalation.
  • Emotional Contagion: Awareness that your own emotional state can directly influence the subject's.
  • Operational Safety Tactics: The practical application of concepts like tactical pause, distance, cover, and time.

Managing Critical Incidents

Effective management of critical incidents requires sound tactics, clear communication, and defined team roles.

Key Tactical Concepts:

  • Prepare Yourself: Before you arrive, use dispatch information to begin mentally preparing for what you might encounter. This is the start of the CDM process.
  • Tactical Pause: If there is no immediate threat, slow the situation down. Use this time to collect information and prepare yourself before engaging.
  • Distance + Cover = Time: When officers have distance from a threat, cover to protect themselves, and more time, their options multiply. Close quarters and time pressure reduce options.
  • Tactical Repositioning: Consciously moving to a position of advantage and maintaining a safety zone. Never give up distance and cover unnecessarily.

Practical Example: On a call involving a man with a knife in his own home, officers stop at the doorway instead of rushing in. This is a tactical pause. They then step back behind the door frame (cover), creating distance and giving them time to communicate. This is tactical repositioning.

Team Roles & After-Action Review (AAR)

Team Roles in a Critical Incident

  • One Communicator: Only one officer should communicate directly with the subject to avoid conflicting messages.
  • Defined Support Roles: Other officers should have clear roles, such as providing cover, using less-lethal weapons if needed, establishing perimeters, or managing bystanders.

After-Action Review (AAR)

An AAR is conducted after an incident to uncover strengths and weaknesses, with the purpose of improving future performance. It is a tool for learning, not for placing blame.

  • The CDM serves as a framework for the AAR, examining the decisions made at each of the five steps.
  • The review should always include a discussion of whether implicit bias may have played a role in the incident.

Putting It All Into Practice

Reading and understanding these concepts is the first step. True proficiency comes from applying them in realistic, scenario-based training. These exercises allow officers to practice decision-making, communication, and tactics in a controlled environment, building the muscle memory needed for real-world critical incidents.
