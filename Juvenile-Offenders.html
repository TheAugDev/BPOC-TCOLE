Juvenile Offenders

BPOC Chapter 33 | TCOLE Preparation

Purpose of the Juvenile Justice Code (FC 51.01)

The purpose of Title 3 of the Texas Family Code is to provide for the care, protection, and wholesome moral, mental, and physical development of children. It aims to protect the welfare of the community and control the commission of unlawful acts by children, consistent with the protection of the public and the best interest of the child.

Key Definitions (FC 51.02)

Child: A person who is 10 years of age or older and under 17 years of age.
Custodian: The adult with whom the child resides.
Guardian: The person who, under court order, is the guardian of the person of the child.
Juvenile Court: A court designated under Section 51.04 to exercise jurisdiction over juvenile matters.
Parent: The mother or the father of a child, but does not include a parent whose parental rights have been terminated.
Status Offender: A child who is accused, adjudicated, or convicted for conduct that would not, under state law, be a crime if committed by an adult (e.g., truancy, running away).
Non-offender: A child who is subject to jurisdiction of a court under abuse, neglect, or dependency statutes.
Secure Detention Facility: A public or private facility used for the temporary placement of juveniles accused of delinquent conduct.
Secure Correctional Facility: A facility for juveniles who have been adjudicated as having engaged in delinquent conduct.
Traffic Offense: A violation of a law or ordinance governing motor vehicle traffic, other than certain intoxication or felony offenses.
Valid Court Order: An order issued by a juvenile court for a child who has been found to be a status offender.
Referral to Juvenile Court: The referral of a child or a child's case to an office or official designated by the juvenile board.

Types of Juvenile Conduct (FC 51.03)

Delinquent Conduct

More Serious Offenses

Legal Definition:

Conduct, other than a traffic offense, that violates a penal law of this state or of the United States punishable by imprisonment or by confinement in jail; or a lawful order of a court under circumstances that would constitute contempt of that court.

Elements Checklist:

  • Violates a penal law (state or federal).
  • Punishable by jail or imprisonment.
  • Includes DWI/DUI (PC Ch. 49).
  • Includes violating a valid court order (contempt).

Practical Example:

A 15-year-old breaks into a vehicle and steals a laptop. This act violates the Texas Penal Code (Burglary of a Vehicle) and is punishable by jail time, therefore it is classified as Delinquent Conduct.

Conduct Indicating a Need for Supervision (CINS)

Less Serious Offenses / Status Offenses

Legal Definition:

Conduct, other than a traffic offense, that on three or more occasions violates penal laws of the grade of Class C misdemeanor; or conduct that involves running away from home, violating school compulsory attendance laws, or violating a city ordinance or state law relating to curfew.

Elements Checklist:

  • Three or more Class C Misdemeanors (non-traffic).
  • Running away from home.
  • Truancy (failure to attend school).
  • Violation of curfew ordinances.
  • DUI by a Minor (ABC 106.041) is specifically listed as CINS.

Practical Example:

A 14-year-old is picked up by police for the fourth time in a month for violating the city's 11 PM curfew. This repeated violation constitutes Conduct Indicating a Need for Supervision.

Jurisdiction of Juvenile Courts (FC 51.04)

Each county in Texas must have a designated juvenile court. The county's juvenile board designates one or more district, criminal district, domestic relations, county, or county courts at law to handle juvenile cases.

Key Points:

  • If the designated juvenile judge is unavailable, any magistrate may conduct detention hearings.
  • A "referee" (a licensed Texas attorney) may also be appointed to conduct these hearings.
  • Juvenile court has exclusive original jurisdiction over cases involving delinquent conduct or CINS by a person who was a child at the time of the offense.

The Juvenile Court Process Flow

The path a juvenile case takes is similar in principle to the adult system, but the terminology and timelines are different. The focus is on rehabilitation rather than punishment.

  1. 1. Custody / Referral

    An officer takes a child into custody (FC 52.01) or a referral is made to juvenile probation.

  2. 2. Release or Detain (FC 53.02)

    An intake officer investigates and determines if the child should be released or detained. Detention is warranted if the child is likely to abscond, has no suitable supervision, is a danger to self or public, or is a repeat offender.

  3. 3. Detention Hearing (FC 54.01)

    If detained, a hearing must be held by the second working day (or first, if taken into custody on a Friday/Saturday) to determine if continued detention is necessary.

  4. 4. Petition

    If the case proceeds, the prosecutor files a petition, similar to an indictment, alleging delinquent conduct or CINS.

  5. 5. Adjudication Hearing

    This is the trial phase. The judge or jury determines if the child engaged in the alleged conduct (a plea of "true" or "not true").

  6. 6. Disposition Hearing

    This is the sentencing phase. The judge determines the most appropriate outcome, which can range from probation to commitment to a state facility.

Disposition Without Referral & Diversion Programs

Disposition Without Referral to Court (FC 52.03)

For minor offenses (like Class Cs), an officer may dispose of a case without referring it to court if authorized by guidelines from the county juvenile board. This cannot involve keeping the child in custody or requiring them to report to law enforcement.

First Offender Program (FC 52.031)

A juvenile board may establish a program for the referral of children accused of CINS or minor delinquent conduct. If the child and parent consent and the child successfully completes the program, the case is closed without a formal court referral.

Effect of Adjudication & Parental Rights

Effect of Adjudication or Disposition (FC 51.13)

An order of adjudication or disposition in a juvenile case is not a conviction of a crime and does not impose any civil disabilities (e.g., cannot be used to disqualify from civil service).

Rights and Duties of a Parent (FC 151.001)

A parent has the right to physical possession, the duty of care, control, protection, and reasonable discipline, the duty to support, and the right to consent to medical treatment and make decisions concerning the child's education.

Waiver of Rights (FC 51.09)

Any right granted to a child by the Constitution or laws can be waived in juvenile proceedings, but only under strict conditions.

Elements Checklist for a Valid Waiver:

  • Waiver must be made by the child AND the child's attorney.
  • Both child and attorney must be informed of and understand the right and the consequences of waiving it.
  • The waiver must be voluntary.
  • It must be made in writing or in recorded court proceedings.

Admissibility of a Juvenile's Statement (FC 51.095)

For a statement made by a child in custody to be admissible, strict legal requirements must be met.

Written Statement Checklist:

  • The child must first be taken before a magistrate.
  • The magistrate must give the child statutory warnings.
  • The child must knowingly, intelligently, and voluntarily waive their rights.
  • The child must sign the statement in the presence of the magistrate.
  • Crucially: No law enforcement officer or prosecutor may be present when the child signs.
  • The magistrate must certify in writing that all requirements were met.

Oral/Recorded Statement Checklist:

  • For a recorded statement, the magistrate warnings and waiver must be part of the recording.
  • All voices on the recording must be identified.
  • An unrecorded oral statement may be admissible if it leads to evidence of guilt.
  • Res Gestae (excited utterance) statements are also admissible.

Age Considerations & Gray Areas

  • 17 years old: An "adult" in Texas. If the crime occurs at 17 or older, adult rules (CCP 38.22) apply.
  • Crime under 17, now 17: Juvenile rules (FC 51.095) still apply because the offense was committed when they were a child.
  • Crime under 17, now 18+: Adult rules apply for the interview, even though the case is handled by the juvenile system.
  • Unknown Age: If a suspect might be a juvenile, it's safest to follow juvenile procedures, as they will satisfy adult requirements, but not vice versa.

Fingerprints, Photos, & Polygraphs

A child may not be photographed or fingerprinted without juvenile court consent unless the child is taken into custody for conduct that would be a felony or jailable misdemeanor if committed by an adult. (FC 58.002)

  • Records Retention (FC 58.001): If a child is taken into custody, law enforcement shall forward all information (reports, fingerprints, photos) to the juvenile court within 10 calendar days. If missed, records must be destroyed.
  • Comparison/Runaway Identification: Fingerprints and photos may also be taken for comparison with evidence from a crime scene (FC 58.0021) or to identify a runaway child (FC 58.0022).
  • Polygraph Exam (FC 51.151): A polygraph examination of a child requires the consent of the child's attorney or the juvenile court.

Investigation of Child Abuse or Neglect (FC Ch. 261)

Abuse (FC 261.001):

Includes mental or emotional injury, physical injury that results in substantial harm, sexual abuse, or causing or permitting the child to be in a situation where the child sustains a mental or physical injury.

Neglect (FC 261.001):

Includes leaving a child in a situation where the child would be exposed to a substantial risk of physical or mental harm, or failing to provide a child with food, clothing, shelter, or medical care.

Mandatory Reporting

Who Must Report? (FC 261.101)

A person having cause to believe that a child's physical or mental health or welfare has been adversely affected by abuse or neglect by any person shall immediately make a report.

Elements Checklist:

  • The requirement applies to every person. Professionals have a heightened duty.
  • The report must be made immediately (within 48 hours is the outer limit).
  • Reports should be made to any local or state law enforcement agency or DFPS.
  • Reporters are granted immunity from liability if the report is made in good faith (FC 261.106).

Penalties:

  • Failure to Report (FC 261.109): Class A Misdemeanor.
  • False Report (FC 261.107): State Jail Felony.

Emergency Possession & Medical Consent

Taking Possession Without a Court Order (FC 262.104)

A DFPS representative or law enforcement officer may take possession of a child without a court order if they believe the child is in immediate danger of physical or sexual abuse.

Consent to Treatment by a Child (FC 32.003)

A child can consent to their own medical treatment if they are in the armed services, are 16 or older and live apart from parents, or are unmarried and pregnant.

Child Advocacy Centers (CACs) (FC 264.403):

CACs are community partnerships that bring together law enforcement, CPS, prosecutors, and medical/mental health professionals to provide a coordinated, multi-disciplinary response to child abuse allegations.

Missing Children & Persons (CCP Ch. 63)

Texas law provides strict guidelines and timelines for law enforcement response to missing child reports. There is NO waiting period to report a child missing.

Definitions (CCP 63.001):

  • Child: A person under 18 years of age.
  • Missing Child: A child whose whereabouts are unknown to the child's legal custodian.
  • Clearinghouse: The state Missing Children and Missing Persons Information Clearinghouse, managed by the DPS.

Law Enforcement Requirements (CCP 63.009)

Upon receiving a report of a missing child, a law enforcement agency SHALL:

Immediate Actions Checklist:

  • Immediately begin an investigation.
  • Immediately, but no later than 2 hours, enter the child's info into the state Clearinghouse (TCIC/TLETS) and NCIC.
  • Inform the person who filed the report about the actions being taken.

Follow-Up Actions Checklist:

  • Within 48 hours, submit the report to all LE agencies within a 200-mile radius.
  • Within 30 days, enter the child's name into the National Missing and Unidentified Persons System (NamUs).

High-Risk Children (CCP 63.0091):

If a child is "high risk" (e.g., has special needs, at risk of trafficking, has a mental illness), the agency must escalate its response and notify all LE agencies within 100 miles within 2 hours.

AMBER Alert System & School Records

AMBER Alert Activation Criteria (GC 411.355):

  • Confirm a child 17 or younger has been abducted.
  • Believe the child is in imminent danger of serious bodily injury or death.
  • A preliminary investigation verifies the abduction.
  • Sufficient information exists to disseminate to the public.

School Record Flagging (CCP 63.020):

If a child under 11 is reported missing, law enforcement must immediately notify their school. The school must "flag" the child's records. If a request for the flagged records is made, the school must immediately notify law enforcement.

Youth Gangs: Risk Factors

The presence of multiple risk factors across different domains significantly increases the likelihood of gang involvement.

Individual

  • Problematic behavior
  • Victimization history
  • Negative life events
  • Low self-esteem

Family

  • Disrupted family structure
  • Poor parental supervision
  • Inconsistent discipline
  • Lack of parental involvement

School

  • Poor academic performance
  • Low attachment to school
  • Unsafe school environment
  • Learning disabilities

Peers

  • Alienation from mainstream
  • Anti-social behavior
  • Friends in gangs (strongest factor)

Community

  • High poverty levels
  • Neighborhood disorganization
  • Environmental instability
  • Access to drugs/firearms

Why Juveniles Join Gangs

Gangs often fulfill perceived needs not being met in a youth's life. Most youth are not forced to join, and it is often not a permanent decision.

Needs Met by Gangs:

Status & Self-Worth

Acceptance & Bonding

Protection

Fun & Excitement

Credibility & Notoriety

Wealth & Materialism

Underlying Societal Strains:

  • High unemployment
  • Atmosphere of discrimination or abuse
  • Community seen as uncaring
  • Environment that uses intimidation and aggression to solve problems
