Texas Alcoholic Beverage Code

BPOC Chapter 16 | TCOLE Preparation

Key Definitions (TABC §1.04)

Alcoholic Beverage: Alcohol, or any beverage containing more than one-half of one percent of alcohol by volume, which is capable of use for beverage purposes, either alone or when diluted.

Distilled Spirits: Alcohol, spirits of wine, whiskey, rum, brandy, gin, or any liquor produced in whole or in part by the process of distillation.

Illicit Beverage: An alcoholic beverage manufactured, distributed, bought, sold, transported, or possessed in violation of this code.

Licensee: A person who is the holder of a license provided for in this code, or any agent, servant, or employee of that person.

Malt Beverage: A fermented beverage of any name or description containing one-half of one percent or more of alcohol by volume, brewed or produced from malt.

Mixed Beverage: One or more servings of a beverage composed in whole or part of an alcoholic beverage in a sealed or unsealed container of any legal size for consumption on the premises.

Permittee: A person who is the holder of a permit provided for in this code, or any agent, servant, or employee of that person.

Minor: A person under 21 years of age (§106.01).

Premises: The grounds and all buildings, vehicles, and appurtenances pertaining to the grounds, including any adjacent premises if they are directly or indirectly under the control of the same person (§11.49).

General Penalty (§1.05): A person who violates a provision of this code for which a specific penalty is not provided is guilty of a misdemeanor and on conviction is punishable by a fine of not less than $100 nor more than $1,000 or by confinement in the county jail for not more than one year, or both.

Search vs. Inspection

Inspection (§101.04)

By accepting a license or permit, a person consents for TABC or any peace officer to enter and inspect the premises at any time to perform any duty imposed by the code. This is a broad authority to check for TABC violations on licensed premises.

Search (§101.03)

A search requires a search warrant, which may be issued by a magistrate on a sworn complaint showing probable cause that an offense is occurring, to search a suspected place and seize illicit beverages and equipment used to manufacture them.

Criminal vs. Administrative Actions

A single violation of the TABC can result in two separate and parallel actions because they are different types of law (criminal vs. civil). Double jeopardy does not apply.

Criminal Action

Taken against an **individual** (bartender, clerk, owner). Results in fines, jail time, and a criminal record.

Administrative Action

Taken by TABC against the **business license/permit**. Results in suspension or cancellation of the permit, or civil penalties (fines).

Peace Officer Duties & Powers

Duty of Peace Officers (§101.07): All peace officers in the state have a duty to seize illicit beverages and arrest any person they see violating any provision of the TABC.

Arrest Without Warrant (§101.02): A peace officer may arrest without a warrant any person they see violating the code, but the person must be taken before a magistrate and a complaint filed.

Seizure of Illicit Beverages (§103.03): A peace officer may seize, without a warrant, any illicit beverage, its container, and any vehicle used to transport it.

Arrest of Person in Possession (§103.04): A peace officer shall arrest without a warrant any person found in possession of an illicit beverage.

Report of Seizure (§103.05): An officer who makes a seizure must make a sworn report to the attorney general or county/district attorney, stating a list of items seized and the person from whom they were seized.

Beverage Seized by Peace Officer (§103.06): Any beverage seized by a peace officer shall be delivered to the TABC.

Offenses Involving Minors

Purchase / Attempt to Purchase Alcohol by a Minor (§106.02, §106.025)

Punishment Level: Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A minor
  • With specific intent to commit an offense (purchase)
  • Does an act that amounts to more than mere preparation
  • That tends but fails to effect the commission of the offense intended.

Practical Example: An officer sees a 19-year-old hand cash to a clerk for a six-pack of beer. The clerk asks for ID, sees the person is a minor, and refuses the sale. The minor has committed Attempt to Purchase Alcohol.

Sale of Alcohol to a Minor (§106.03)

Punishment Level: Class A Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • With criminal negligence
  • Sells an alcoholic beverage
  • To a minor.

Practical Example: A convenience store clerk sells beer to a 17-year-old without asking for their ID. The clerk has committed an offense.

Consumption of Alcohol by a Minor (§106.04)

Punishment Level: Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A minor
  • Consumes an alcoholic beverage.

Practical Example: An officer is dispatched to a park and observes a group of teenagers drinking beer. One of them, age 20, is seen taking a drink from a can. This is Consumption of Alcohol by a Minor. (Exception: only if in the visible presence of their adult parent/guardian/spouse).

Driving or Operating Watercraft Under Influence by Minor (§106.041)

Punishment Level: Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A minor
  • Operates a motor vehicle or watercraft
  • In a public place
  • While having any detectable amount of alcohol in the minor's system.

Practical Example: An officer stops a 19-year-old for speeding. The officer smells alcohol on the driver's breath. A portable breath test shows the presence of alcohol, but the driver is not intoxicated. The driver has committed DUIA by a Minor.

Possession of Alcohol by a Minor (§106.05)

Punishment Level: Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A minor
  • Possesses an alcoholic beverage.

Practical Example: An officer stops a car for a traffic violation and sees an 18-year-old passenger holding an unopened can of beer. This is Possession of Alcohol by a Minor. (Exceptions: in the scope of employment, or in the visible presence of parent/guardian/spouse).

Furnishing Alcohol to a Minor (§106.06)

Punishment Level: Class A Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Purchases for or gives or makes available an alcoholic beverage
  • To a minor
  • With criminal negligence.

Practical Example: An 18-year-old's parent hosts a graduation party and allows the teenagers to drink beer. The parent has committed the offense of Furnishing Alcohol to a Minor.

Misrepresentation of Age by a Minor (§106.07)

Punishment Level: Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A minor
  • Falsely states they are 21 years of age or older
  • Or presents any document that indicates they are 21 or older
  • To a person engaged in the sale of alcoholic beverages.

Practical Example: A 20-year-old uses their older sibling's driver's license to try to buy alcohol at a liquor store. They have committed Misrepresentation of Age by a Minor.

General Offenses

Sale to Certain Persons (§101.63)

Punishment Level: Fine of $100-$500; and/or up to 1 year in jail.

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Sells an alcoholic beverage
  • To a habitual drunkard or an intoxicated or insane person.

Practical Example: A bartender serves another beer to a patron who is slurring their words, stumbling, and showing other clear signs of intoxication. The bartender has committed an offense.

Consumption of Alcohol Near Schools (§101.75)

Punishment Level: Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Possesses an open container or consumes an alcoholic beverage
  • On a public street, alley, or sidewalk
  • Within 1000 feet of a public or private school.

Practical Example: A person is walking down the sidewalk in front of an elementary school while drinking a can of beer. They have committed an offense.

Possession of Intoxicants on Public School Grounds (EC §37.122)

Punishment Level: Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Possesses an intoxicating beverage
  • On the grounds of any public school.

Practical Example: A parent brings a flask of whiskey to a high school football game and is found with it in the stands. They have committed an offense.

Open Container (PC §49.031)

Note: This offense is in the Penal Code, not TABC.

Punishment Level: Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Knowingly
  • Possesses an open container
  • In a passenger area of a motor vehicle that is located on a public highway.

Practical Example: A driver is stopped for a traffic violation and the officer sees a half-empty bottle of wine in the passenger seat. The driver has committed Possession of an Open Container.

Lewd, Immoral, Indecent Conduct (§104.01)

Punishment Level: This is an administrative violation against the license/permit, not a direct criminal offense for the patron.

Checklist: A licensee or permittee shall not:

  • Employ a person on the premises who is intoxicated.
  • Permit any lewd or immoral conduct on the premises.
  • Permit any conduct that is loud, boisterous, or profane.

Practical Example: An officer enters a bar and finds an off-duty bartender who is extremely intoxicated and yelling profanities at other customers. The TABC can take administrative action against the bar's permit for this violation.

Hours of Sale & Consumption

Liquor (Package Store) (§105.01)

  • Hours: 10:00 AM to 9:00 PM.
  • Days: Monday - Saturday.
  • Closed: Sunday, Thanksgiving Day, Christmas Day, New Year's Day.

Hours of Sale: Wine and Malt Beverage Retailer (§105.04)

Applies to places like convenience and grocery stores.

  • Monday - Friday: 7:00 AM to Midnight.
  • Saturday: 7:00 AM to 1:00 AM (Sunday).
  • Sunday: 10:00 AM to Midnight. (Previously Noon).

Hours of Consumption (On-Premise) (§105.06)

Applies to places like bars and restaurants.

  • Standard Hours: Ends at 12:15 AM. Patrons have until then to finish drinks.
  • Extended Hours (by local ordinance): Ends at 2:15 AM.
  • Sunday: Can begin serving at 10:00 AM with food.

Sports Venues, Wineries, & Other Events (§105.07-09)

  • Special provisions exist for locations like sports venues, wineries, festivals, and other specific events that may allow for different hours of sale and consumption than standard establishments.

Penalty for Hours Violation (§105.10)

Punishment Level: Subject to the General Penalty under §1.05 (Misdemeanor).

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Sells, offers for sale, or delivers an alcoholic beverage
  • During prohibited hours.

Practical Example: A grocery store clerk sells a case of beer to a customer at 6:30 AM on a Tuesday. The store has committed an offense for selling outside of legal hours.
