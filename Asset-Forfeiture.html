Asset Forfeiture

BPOC Chapter 11 | TCOLE Preparation

Philosophy of Forfeiture

Legal Definition: The main goal of forfeiture is to deprive criminals of the profits and instrumentalities of their crimes in a manner that benefits law enforcement and the State. It is considered a civil, remedial action, not an extra form of punishment.

Elements Checklist (Guiding Principles):

  • It is remedial, not punitive.
  • The 8th Amendment's "Excessive Fines" clause applies.
  • The amount seized must be proportional to the seriousness of the underlying offense.
  • It is not intended to be a means of oppression.
  • The ultimate goal is to seek justice.

Definitions

Contraband (CCP 59.01)

Property of any nature that is used in, intended to be used in, proceeds from, or acquired with proceeds from the commission of certain offenses.

Elements Checklist (Predicate Offenses):

  • Any 1st or 2nd-degree felony under the Penal Code.
  • Any felony under PC Chapters 29 (Robbery), 30 (Burglary), 31 (Theft), 32 (Fraud), 33 (Computer Crimes), 34 (Money Laundering), or 35 (Insurance Fraud).
  • Any felony under the Health and Safety Code (e.g., Texas Controlled Substances Act).
  • Any felony involving a health care program.
  • Any felony under PC Chapter 71 (Engaging in Organized Criminal Activity).
  • Any offense under PC 42.10 (Dog Fighting).

Proceeds (CCP 59.01(7))

Includes income a person receives from reenacting a crime (e.g., movies, books) or from the sale of tangible property whose value is increased by the notoriety of the crime.

Practical Example: A "mom and pop" grocery store knowingly accepts blank checks from a man named Cinco, who pays them a 20% premium in cash. The cash is part of a money-laundering scheme. The extra cash the owners receive, and the new SUV they bought with it, are considered "proceeds" gained from the commission of a felony and are subject to seizure.

Pre-Seizure Planning

Before seizing property, an officer must conduct planning to assess risks, liabilities, and the value of the asset.

Elements Checklist (Planning Steps):

  • Know the Property Owner: Is it community or separate property? Is it leased?
  • Check for Liens: Are there existing liens or mortgages? Is there enough equity to make the seizure worthwhile?
  • Assess Businesses: Review leases of real property and liens against inventory.
  • Evaluate Bank Accounts: Identify account numbers and signatories. Monitor the account to determine the best time to seize funds.

Practical Example: Officers are investigating a used car lot for money laundering. Before seizing the lot, they determine the property is leased, not owned. They also discover through tax records that the cars on the lot have large liens against them, leaving little to no equity. They decide to focus on seizing the business's bank account instead, after monitoring it to find when the balance is highest.

Methods of Seizure

Property can be seized with or without a warrant, depending on the circumstances.

Without a Warrant:

  • Owner knowingly consents.
  • Incident to a search consented to.
  • Property is subject to a prior judgment of forfeiture.
  • Incident to a lawful arrest, search, or lawful search incident to arrest.

With a Warrant:

  • Rules of CCP Chapter 18 apply.
  • The warrant affidavit must show probable cause that a specific felony occurred.
  • The affidavit must describe how the property is contraband under CCP 59.01.

Post-Seizure Procedures

A peace officer who seizes property has custody of it and must follow specific procedures.

Elements Checklist (Officer's Duties):

  • Provide the attorney representing the state with a sworn statement containing a schedule of property seized, an acknowledgment of the seizure, and the reasons supporting it.
  • No later than 72 hours after seizure, the officer must either place the property under seal, remove it to a place ordered by the court, or have the agency take custody.
  • If currency is seized, it must be deposited in an interest-bearing account in the jurisdiction of the state's attorney.

Warning: An officer cannot commit any act which could be construed as inducing a person to waive their rights to their seized property (could be Official Oppression PC 39.03).

Affidavit & Petition Requirements

Affidavit Best Practices:

  • Should be written by the seizing officer in the first person ("I," not "Officer X").
  • Justify the seizure based on facts; circumstantial evidence is okay.
  • Identify key factors connecting the property to the crime (e.g., proximity to drugs, canine alert).
  • Keep it simple; avoid jargon and acronyms.
  • Avoid hearsay unless it falls under a recognized exception.

Petition Requirements:

  • Must include all property seized.
  • Must list all owners or interest holders, including lien holders.
  • Must identify the person who had custody of the property when seized.

Defenses to Forfeiture (CCP 59.02)

Statutory Defense: Innocent Owner

This is an affirmative defense where the owner claims they were not a party to the criminal act.

Elements Checklist (Owner must prove they):

  • Acquired the property before the illegal act; AND
  • Did not know or should not reasonably have known of the act or that it was likely to occur.
  • OR, that the property was used without their effective consent (e.g., it was stolen).

Practical Example: A man uses his wife's car, which she owns separately, to deliver drugs without her knowledge. She can claim the innocent owner defense because she did not know about or consent to the illegal use of her vehicle.

Non-Statutory Defenses & Other Issues

  • Dismissal/Acquittal: Dismissal or acquittal of related criminal charges raises a presumption that the property should not be forfeited.
  • Homestead: Homestead protection against forfeiture is not resolved in Texas.
  • Legitimate Source of Income: A defendant can argue the property was acquired through legitimate means.
  • Fifth Amendment: Invoking the 5th Amendment in a civil forfeiture case can be used against the defendant.
  • Suppression of Evidence: If evidence is suppressed in the criminal case, it generally has no effect on the civil forfeiture case due to different rules.

Forfeiture Proceedings (CCP 59.04)

The legal process for forfeiting property is a civil proceeding.

Elements Checklist (Procedural Steps):

  • The District Attorney must begin forfeiture proceedings within 30 days of the seizure.
  • It is a civil case, so the standard of proof is "preponderance of the evidence."
  • The respondent is not entitled to a court-appointed counsel.
  • All filed documents are public record.

Other Considerations in Forfeiture Cases

  • Civil Discovery: The process can involve interrogatories (written questions) and depositions (sworn out-of-court testimony).
  • Settlement: Many cases are resolved through negotiated settlements.
  • Local Agreement / Federal Adoption: Cases may be handled locally, or a local agency may adopt a federal seizure, which operates under federal rules.
  • Drug Trafficking: A significant source of forfeitable assets, with drug profits in the U.S. estimated to be as high as $100 billion per year.

Use of Forfeited Proceeds (CCP 59.06)

Proceeds from forfeitures are governed by state law and must be spent for a "law enforcement purpose."

Permitted Uses:

  • Equipment & Supplies
  • Investigative and training-related travel
  • Crime prevention programs
  • Facility costs
  • Witness-related costs
  • Legal fees and dues

Prohibited Uses:

  • Political campaign donations
  • Training for any member of the judiciary
  • Purchasing alcoholic beverages
  • Increasing salaries (unless approved by the governing body)
  • Any expenditure not approved by the governing body
