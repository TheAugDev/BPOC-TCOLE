Traffic Code & Crash Investigation

BPOC Chapter 22 | TCOLE Preparation

License to be Carried & Exhibited on Demand - TC §521.025

Legal Definition: A person required to hold a license...shall have the license in the person's possession when operating a motor vehicle and display the license on demand of a magistrate, court officer, or peace officer.

Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person is operating a motor vehicle.
  • The person is required to hold a driver's license.
  • A peace officer demands to see the license.
  • The person fails or refuses to display the license.

Practical Example: An officer stops a driver for speeding. The driver states they have a valid license but left their wallet at home. The driver has committed the offense of Failure to Display Driver's License. (Note: The charge may be dismissed if the person presents a valid license for that time to the court).

Driving While License Invalid (DWLI) - TC §521.457

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person operates a motor vehicle on a highway after the person's driver's license has been canceled, suspended, or revoked...

Class C Misdemeanor Class B Misdemeanor (w/ prior, no insurance, or DWI suspension) Class A Misdemeanor (if causing SBI or death)

Elements Checklist:

  • A person operates a motor vehicle on a public highway.
  • The person's license is currently invalid (suspended, revoked, canceled).
  • The person received actual notice of the suspension.

Practical Example: An officer runs a license plate, and the return shows the owner's license is suspended. The officer confirms the driver is the owner and makes a stop. The driver is arrested for DWLI, a Class B Misdemeanor, as the suspension resulted from traffic convictions and they are operating without insurance.

Operation of Vehicle with Expired License Plate - TC §502.407

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person operates on a public highway a motor vehicle that has a license plate that has been expired for more than five working days.

Misdemeanor (Fine up to $200)

Elements Checklist:

  • A person operates a motor vehicle on a public highway.
  • The vehicle's registration (license plate sticker) is expired.
  • The grace period of five working days has passed.

Practical Example: An officer on patrol on a Wednesday notices a vehicle with a registration sticker that expired at the end of the previous month. More than five working days have passed, so the officer can stop the vehicle and issue a citation for the offense.

Operation of Vehicle without License Plate - TC §504.943

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person operates on a public highway a motor vehicle that is not registered in this state and does not display the license plate or plates required for the vehicle for the current registration period by the state or country in which the vehicle is registered.

Misdemeanor (Fine up to $200)

Elements Checklist:

  • A person operates a motor vehicle on a public highway.
  • The vehicle is required to display license plates.
  • The vehicle fails to display license plates on both the front and rear of the vehicle (for most vehicles).

Practical Example: An officer observes a passenger car driving on the highway with a valid rear license plate but no front license plate. The officer can stop the vehicle and issue a citation for failing to display two license plates.

Operation of Motor Vehicle Without Financial Responsibility - TC §601.191

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person operates a motor vehicle in this state in violation of Section 601.051 (Requirement of Financial Responsibility).

Misdemeanor (Fine $175-$350)

Elements Checklist:

  • A person operates a motor vehicle in Texas.
  • The person does not have evidence of financial responsibility (liability insurance or other approved method).

Practical Example: An officer stops a car for an equipment violation. When the officer asks for proof of insurance, the driver cannot provide a current insurance card. A database check confirms the vehicle's insurance has lapsed. The driver is cited for No Insurance.

Compliance with Traffic-Control Device - TC §544.004

Legal Definition: The operator of a vehicle...shall comply with an applicable official traffic-control device...unless the person is otherwise directed by a traffic or police officer.

Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • An official traffic-control device is in a proper position and sufficiently legible.
  • An operator of a vehicle fails to obey the instruction of the device.
  • (e.g., running a red light, running a stop sign, turning from a straight-only lane).

Practical Example: A driver approaches a red light, slows, but continues through the intersection without coming to a complete stop before making a right turn. The driver has failed to comply with the traffic-control device and can be cited.

Maximum Speed Requirement (Basic Speed Law) - TC §545.351

Legal Definition: An operator may not drive at a speed greater than is reasonable and prudent under the conditions and having regard for actual and potential hazards then existing...

Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • An operator drives at a speed that is not reasonable and prudent for the existing conditions.
  • This can be true even if driving at or below the posted speed limit (e.g., during heavy rain, fog, or icy conditions).

Practical Example: During a thunderstorm, a driver travels at the posted 60 mph limit. Due to low visibility and wet roads, their speed is unsafe, causing them to lose control. They can be cited for violating the basic speed law.

Following Distance - TC §545.062

Legal Definition: An operator shall, if following another vehicle, maintain an assured clear distance between the two vehicles so that...the operator can safely stop without colliding...

Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • An operator is following another vehicle.
  • They fail to maintain an "assured clear distance."
  • The distance is not safe enough to stop without a collision, given the current conditions.

Practical Example: On a busy highway, an officer sees a car tailgating the vehicle in front, leaving less than one car length between them at 65 mph. The driver is violating the following distance law.

Passing a School Bus - TC §545.066

Legal Definition: An operator on a highway, when approaching from either direction a school bus stopped...to receive or discharge a student, shall stop before reaching the school bus when the bus is operating a visual signal...

Misdemeanor (Fine $500-$1250) Class A Misdemeanor (if prior) State Jail Felony (if causing SBI)

Elements Checklist:

  • An operator approaches a stopped school bus from either direction.
  • The bus is displaying its visual signals (flashing red lights).
  • The operator fails to stop before reaching the bus.
  • (Exception: Not required to stop if on a different roadway of a highway with separate roadways).

Practical Example: A driver is on a two-lane road. A school bus in the opposite lane stops and activates its flashing red lights. The driver continues past the bus without stopping and is cited for the offense.

Passing Certain Vehicles ("Move Over / Slow Down") - TC §545.157

Legal Definition: When approaching a stationary authorized emergency vehicle, tow truck, or TxDOT vehicle with its visual signals activated, an operator shall either vacate the lane closest to the vehicle...or slow down to 20 mph less than the posted speed limit...

Misdemeanor (Fine up to $200) Class B Misdemeanor (if bodily injury)

Elements Checklist:

  • An operator approaches a stationary emergency, tow, or TxDOT vehicle.
  • The stationary vehicle is displaying its emergency lights.
  • The operator fails to either move over or slow down as required.

Practical Example: On a three-lane highway, a driver sees a police car with lights on during a stop on the right shoulder. The driver stays in the right lane and passes at the posted 70 mph limit, committing an offense.

Reckless Driving - TC §545.401

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person drives a vehicle in a willful or wanton disregard for the safety of persons or property.

Misdemeanor (Fine up to $200, up to 30 days jail, or both)

Elements Checklist:

  • A person drives a vehicle.
  • They do so with a willful or wanton disregard for safety.

Practical Example: An officer sees a driver weaving rapidly between lanes in heavy traffic at high speed, cutting off other drivers. This demonstrates a clear disregard for safety and constitutes Reckless Driving.

General Offense (Equipment) - TC §547.004

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense...if the person operates or moves...a vehicle that is...not equipped in a manner that complies with the vehicle equipment standards and requirements of this chapter.

Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person operates a vehicle (or owner permits operation).
  • The vehicle is on a public highway.
  • The vehicle's equipment does not comply with Chapter 547.

Practical Example: At night, an officer notices a vehicle with a non-functioning taillamp. The driver violates TC §547.322 (Taillamps Required) and can be cited under the general offense statute.

Child Passenger Safety Seat Systems - TC §545.412

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person operates a passenger vehicle, transports a child who is younger than eight years of age, unless the child is taller than four feet, nine inches, and does not keep the child secured during the operation of the vehicle in a child passenger safety seat system according to the manufacturer's instructions.

Misdemeanor (Fine $25-$250)

Elements Checklist:

  • Operator transports a child in a passenger vehicle.
  • The child is under 8 years old AND shorter than 4'9" (57 inches).
  • The child is not secured in an appropriate child safety seat.

Practical Example: An officer stops a vehicle and sees a 6-year-old child in the back seat secured only by an adult seat belt. The child is visibly shorter than 4'9". The driver has committed an offense because the child should be in a booster seat.

Safety Belts - TC §545.413

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person operates a passenger vehicle that is equipped with safety belts and allows a child who is younger than 17 years of age and who is not required to be secured in a child passenger safety seat system...to ride in the vehicle without requiring the child to be secured by a safety belt.

Misdemeanor (Fine $100-$200)

Elements Checklist:

  • An operator allows a child under 17 to ride in the vehicle.
  • The child is not otherwise required to be in a child safety seat.
  • The child is not secured by a safety belt.
  • (Note: It is also an offense for any person 15 or older in any seat to not wear a seatbelt).

Practical Example: An officer sees a vehicle with a front seat passenger who is not wearing a seatbelt. The officer stops the car and identifies the passenger as being 19 years old. The passenger receives the citation. If the passenger had been 14, the driver would have received the citation.

Muffler Required - TC §547.604

Legal Definition: A motor vehicle must be equipped with a muffler in good working condition that continually operates to prevent excessive or unusual noise...

Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person operates a motor vehicle.
  • The vehicle does not have a muffler, or the muffler is defective.
  • The vehicle produces excessive or unusual noise.

Practical Example: An officer hears an extremely loud car approaching. As it passes, the officer can tell the noise is from a modified or absent exhaust system. The officer stops the vehicle and cites the driver for operating with a defective muffler.

Restrictions on Windows (Window Tint) - TC §547.613

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person operates a motor vehicle on which a material is attached to the windshield or a side or rear window that reduces the light transmission...below the standard...

Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person operates a motor vehicle.
  • The vehicle has tint on front side windows with a light transmission value of less than 25%.
  • Or, the windshield has tint below the AS-1 line, or tint that is red, amber, or blue.

Practical Example: An officer cannot see inside a vehicle due to dark front windows. A stop and measurement with a tint meter reveals a light transmission of 15%. This is below the legal 25% limit, and the driver is cited.

Compulsory Inspection - TC Chapter 548

Legal Definition: A person who operates a motor vehicle that is required to be registered in this state must have the vehicle inspected annually. The inspection must be done at an official inspection station. Operating a vehicle without a valid inspection is an offense.

Misdemeanor (Fine up to $200)

Key Concepts ("Two Steps, One Sticker"):

  • Vehicle inspection is still required annually.
  • Proof of a passing inspection is required to renew vehicle registration.
  • The windshield sticker is now only for registration, but it serves as proof of both valid registration and inspection.
  • An officer can verify inspection status through the registration database.

Practical Example: A vehicle's registration is expired. Since a valid inspection is required for registration, the vehicle is also, by extension, uninspected. The driver can be cited for expired registration, which covers the inspection violation.

Offenses for Not Wearing Protective Headgear (Motorcycle) - TC §661.003

Legal Definition: An operator of a motorcycle or a passenger on a motorcycle commits an offense if the operator or passenger is not wearing protective headgear. Exceptions apply for persons over 21 who have completed a safety course or have adequate health insurance coverage.

Misdemeanor (Fine $10-$50)

Elements Checklist:

  • A person operates or rides as a passenger on a motorcycle.
  • The person is not wearing protective headgear (a helmet).
  • The person does not meet an exception (under 21, no course, no insurance).

Practical Example: An officer stops a motorcycle and the passenger, who appears to be about 18, is not wearing a helmet. Since the passenger is under 21, they are required to wear a helmet regardless of insurance or training. The passenger has committed an offense.

Motorcycle Operator Profiling Awareness

Peace officers should be aware of common misconceptions and stereotypes associated with motorcycle operators to ensure that enforcement actions are based on violations of the law, not appearance. "Violators" are defined by their actions, not how they look or what they ride.

Common Misconceptions to Avoid:

  • Assuming all sport bike riders are speeders.
  • Believing all riders wearing club jackets ("colors") are "outlaw bikers" or gang members.
  • Generalizing that bikers are troublemakers or have outstanding warrants.

Best Practice: When conducting a traffic stop on a motorcycle, the reason for the stop must be a specific, identified violation of law. Use the standard 7-step violator contact, but for safety, consider having the operator remain on the motorcycle with the kickstand up. This keeps them occupied with balancing the bike and reduces the risk of sudden movements.

Rights and Duties (Bicycles) - TC §551.101

Legal Definition: A person operating a bicycle has the rights and duties applicable to a driver operating a vehicle under this subtitle, unless a provision of this chapter alters a right or duty.

Key Concepts:

  • Bicyclists must obey all traffic laws, just like cars (e.g., stopping at stop signs, signaling turns).
  • They must ride as near as practicable to the right curb or edge of the roadway.
  • They may take the full lane when necessary for safety or to make a left turn.
  • At night, a bicycle must have a white lamp on the front and a red reflector or red lamp on the rear.

Practical Example: An officer observes a bicyclist ride through a red light without stopping. The officer can stop and cite the bicyclist for the traffic signal violation, just as they would a motorist.

Pedestrian Right-of-Way at Crosswalk - TC §552.003

Legal Definition: An operator of a vehicle shall yield the right-of-way to a pedestrian crossing a roadway in a crosswalk if: (1) no traffic control signal is in place or in operation; and (2) the pedestrian is on the half of the roadway in which the vehicle is traveling or is approaching so closely from the other half... as to be in danger.

Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A vehicle operator approaches a crosswalk with no traffic signal.
  • A pedestrian is in the crosswalk on the operator's half of the road (or close to it).
  • The operator fails to yield the right-of-way to the pedestrian.

Practical Example: A driver approaching a marked crosswalk sees a pedestrian who has stepped off the curb. The driver continues without slowing, forcing the pedestrian to stop. The driver has failed to yield and can be cited.

Solicitation by Pedestrians - TC §552.007

Legal Definition: A person may not stand in a roadway to solicit a ride, contribution, employment, or business from an occupant of a vehicle.

Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person is standing in the roadway.
  • "Roadway" is the portion of the highway used for vehicular travel, not the shoulder.
  • The person is soliciting something from someone in a car.

Practical Example: A person walks between lanes of stopped traffic at a red light, asking for money. Because they are standing in the roadway to solicit, they are committing an offense.

Permissible Conduct (Emergency Vehicles) - TC §546.001

Legal Definition: When responding to an emergency call, the operator of an authorized emergency vehicle may: (1) park or stand...; (2) proceed past a red or stop signal or stop sign, after slowing as necessary for safe operation; (3) exceed a maximum speed limit...as long as the operator does not endanger life or property; and (4) disregard a regulation governing the direction of movement or turning...

Key Concepts:

  • This privilege applies ONLY when responding to a true emergency.
  • The operator MUST use audible or visual signals (lights and siren) as required by TC §546.003.
  • The operator still has a Duty of Care (TC §546.005) and can be held liable if they drive with reckless disregard for the safety of others.

Collision Involving Personal Injury or Death (FSRA) - TC §550.021

Legal Definition: The operator of a vehicle involved in a collision that results in injury to or death of a person shall immediately stop...and remain at the scene to give information and render aid.

2nd Degree Felony (if death) 3rd Degree Felony (if SBI)

Elements Checklist:

  • An operator is involved in a vehicle collision resulting in injury or death.
  • The operator fails to immediately stop and remain at the scene.
  • The operator fails to render reasonable assistance.

Practical Example: A driver strikes a pedestrian, causing serious injuries. The driver panics and speeds away without stopping to check on the victim or call for help. The driver has committed Failure to Stop and Render Aid, a 3rd Degree Felony.

Collision Involving Damage to Vehicle (FSGI) - TC §550.022

Legal Definition: The operator of a vehicle involved in a collision resulting only in damage to a vehicle that is driven or attended by a person shall immediately stop...and remain to give information.

Class B Misdemeanor (damage ≥ $200) Class C Misdemeanor (damage < $200)

Practical Example: A driver backs into an occupied car, causing a large dent. The driver sees the other person but drives off without exchanging information. The driver has committed Failure to Stop and Give Information.

Crash Investigation & Scene Management

The primary duties of a peace officer at a crash scene are to secure the scene, render aid, and conduct an investigation. This includes managing traffic, interviewing parties, measuring the scene, collecting evidence, and completing the Texas Peace Officer's Crash Report (CR-3).

The 5 Areas of a Work Zone (TIMS):

  • 1. Advanced Warning Area: Alerts traffic to the incident ahead.
  • 2. Transition Area: Moves traffic out of the path using cones/flares.
  • 3. Buffer Area: Provides a safety space for responders.
  • 4. Work Area: Where investigation, medical care, and recovery occur.
  • 5. Termination Area: Allows traffic to return to normal.

Arrest Without Warrant Authorized - TC §543.001

Legal Definition: Any peace officer may arrest without a warrant a person found committing a violation of a law regulating traffic. This grants officers the authority to make custodial arrests for any traffic violation, though departmental policy often dictates issuing citations for most minor offenses.

Elements Checklist:

  • A peace officer.
  • Finds a person.
  • Committing a violation of the Transportation Code.

Practical Example: An officer observes a driver run a stop sign. The officer has the authority to stop the vehicle and place the driver under arrest for the violation, even though it is a Class C misdemeanor normally resolved by citation.

Promise to Appear; Release - TC §543.005

Legal Definition: If an officer arrests a person for a violation of the Transportation Code punishable as a misdemeanor and does not take the person immediately before a magistrate, the person is released from custody after signing a written promise to appear in court. Refusing to sign the promise to appear can result in being taken to jail.

Elements Checklist:

  • An officer arrests a person for a TC misdemeanor.
  • The officer requests the person sign a citation (a promise to appear).
  • If the person signs, they must be released.
  • If the person refuses to sign, they can be taken into custody.

Practical Example: After being stopped for speeding, a driver is issued a citation. The driver becomes argumentative and refuses to sign the ticket. The officer explains that signing is not an admission of guilt but a promise to appear. The driver still refuses. The officer can now lawfully take the driver into custody and transport them to jail.

Implied Consent - TC §724.011

Legal Definition: If a person is arrested for an offense...while operating a motor vehicle in a public place while intoxicated, the person is deemed to have consented to submit to the taking of one or more specimens of the person's breath or blood for analysis...

Key Concepts:

  • Consent is Implied: By driving on a public road, you have already consented to a test if lawfully arrested for DWI.
  • Right of Refusal: A person can still refuse to provide a specimen.
  • Consequences: Refusal results in an automatic driver's license suspension and can be used as evidence in court.
  • Mandatory Blood Draw: An officer must obtain a warrant for a blood specimen from a person who refuses, unless exigent circumstances exist. A mandatory specimen is required if the DWI arrest involves a crash with death or SBI.

Offenses; Presumption (Privileged Parking) - TC §681.011

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person parks a vehicle in a parking space or area designated specifically for persons with disabilities and the vehicle does not display a disabled parking placard or specialty license plates for persons with disabilities.

Misdemeanor (Fine $500-$750)

Elements Checklist:

  • A person parks a vehicle in a designated disabled parking space.
  • The space is properly marked with a sign.
  • The vehicle does not display a valid disabled placard or license plate.
  • It is also an offense to lend a placard to someone not entitled to use it.

Practical Example: A driver pulls into a marked disabled parking spot at a grocery store to wait for a friend. The car has no disabled placard or plate. The driver has committed an offense and is subject to a significant fine and potential towing of their vehicle.
