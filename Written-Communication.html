Components of a Sentence

Understanding the basic building blocks of language is the first step to clear writing.

Adjective: A word that describes or modifies a noun. (e.g., "a brave officer," "a new car").
Adverb: A word that modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb. It answers questions like When?, Where?, How?, and To what degree? (e.g., "moved expeditiously").
Noun: The name of a person, place, thing, quality, concept, or action. (e.g., "The officer received an assignment.").
Preposition: A word that combines with a noun or pronoun to form a phrase. (e.g., "They expected resistance on their part.").
Verb: Expresses an act, occurrence, or mode of being. (e.g., "The suspect ran from the officer.").
Pronoun: A word used as a substitute for a noun. (e.g., "Who is she?").

Sentence Construction & Common Errors

Characteristics of a Good Sentence:

  • Expresses a complete thought.
  • Contains a subject and a predicate.
  • Is short, simple, and concise.
  • Is unmistakably clear to the reader.

Sentence Fragments:

A sentence fragment is an incomplete sentence because it lacks a subject, a verb, or both. It does not express a complete thought.

Incorrect (Fragment): The mirror in the hall closet.
Correct: The mirror in the hall closet fell and shattered.

Run-On Sentences:

A run-on sentence occurs when two or more complete sentences are joined without proper punctuation or a conjunction.

Incorrect (Run-on): The officer chased the suspect, and the suspect had a gun.
Correct: The officer chased the suspect. The suspect had a gun.
Correct: The officer chased the suspect, who had a gun.

Comprehensive Punctuation Guide

Proper punctuation guides the reader and makes your meaning clear. Here are common marks and their uses.

Apostrophe ( ' )
  • Indicates the possessive case of nouns and indefinite pronouns: "the boy's mother," "the boys' mothers," "it is anyone's guess."
  • Marks omissions in contracted words: "didn't," "o'clock."
  • Often forms plurals of letters, figures, and words referred to as words: "dot your i's," "several 8's."
Colon ( : )
  • Introduces a word, clause, or phrase that explains, illustrates, amplifies, or restates what has gone before: "The sentence was poorly constructed: it lacked both unity and coherence."
  • Introduces a series: "Three countries were represented: England, France, and Belgium."
  • Separates data in time-telling and bibliographic references: "8:30 a.m.," "John 4:10."
  • Separates titles and subtitles: "The Tragic Dynasty: A History of the Romanovs."
  • Follows the salutation in formal correspondence: "Dear Sir:"
Comma ( , )
  • Separates main clauses joined by a conjunction (and, but, or, etc.): "She knew very little about him, and he volunteered nothing."
  • Sets off a long adverbial clause or phrase that precedes the main clause: "When she found that her friends had deserted her, she sat down and cried."
  • Sets off transitional words and expressions: "Your second question, on the other hand, remains open."
  • Separates words, phrases, or clauses in a series: "Men, women, and children crowded into the square."
  • Sets off parenthetic elements (like nonrestrictive modifiers): "Our guide, who wore a blue beret, was an experienced traveler."
  • Introduces and terminates direct quotations: "John said, 'I am leaving.'"
  • Sets off words in direct address: "You may go, Mary, if you wish."
  • Separates geographical names, dates, and addresses: "Shreveport, Louisiana, is the site..."
Quotation Marks ( " " )
  • Enclose direct quotations: "He said, 'I am leaving.'"
  • Enclose words used in a special way or slang introduced into formal writing.
  • Enclose titles of short works like articles, short stories, or songs.
  • **Placement Rule:** The period and comma ALWAYS fall within the quotation marks.
  • **Placement Rule:** The semicolon and colon ALWAYS fall outside the quotation marks.
  • **Placement Rule:** The dash, question mark, and exclamation point fall inside when they refer to the quoted matter only; they fall outside when they refer to the whole sentence.
Semicolon ( ; )
  • Links main clauses not joined by a coordinating conjunction: "Some people have the ability to write well; others do not."
  • Links main clauses joined by conjunctive adverbs (e.g., consequently, furthermore, however): "Speeding is illegal; furthermore, it is very dangerous."
  • Links clauses which themselves contain commas, even when joined by a conjunction, to avoid confusion.

Writing with Clarity

Misplaced Modifiers:

A modifier is a word or phrase that qualifies another word. It should be placed as close as possible to the word it modifies. Misplacing it can create confusing or unintentionally humorous sentences.

Incorrect: We saw many swimming pools flying over California.
Correct: While flying over California, we saw many swimming pools.

Double Negatives:

Avoid using two negative words in the same clause (e.g., not, no, never, none, nobody, nothing, barely, hardly, scarcely).

Incorrect: Nobody saw nothing.
Correct: Nobody saw anything. / We saw nothing.

Active vs. Passive Voice:

Active voice is stronger, clearer, and more direct. The subject performs the action. In passive voice, the subject receives the action. Use active voice unless the actor is unknown.

Passive: The bystander was hit by a stray bullet.
Active: A stray bullet hit a bystander.

Slang, Jargon, and Nonstandard Abbreviations:

Write professionally. Avoid informal language unless you are directly quoting someone. If you must use an abbreviation, write it out completely the first time followed by the abbreviation in parentheses.

Incorrect: We ID'd the S from the APÐ’.
Correct: We identified the suspect from the all-points-bulletin (APB).

Commonly Misused Words

Accept vs. Except: To receive vs. to exclude.
Affect vs. Effect: Affect is a verb (to influence). Effect is a noun (the result).
All ready vs. Already: Prepared vs. by this time.
Capital vs. Capitol: Seat of government vs. the actual building.
Cite vs. Site vs. Sight: To quote, a place, and vision.
Conscience vs. Conscious: Sense of right/wrong vs. awake.
Council vs. Counsel: A group vs. advice/to advise.
Elicit vs. Illicit: To draw out vs. illegal.
Its vs. It's: Possessive vs. "it is."
Lay vs. Lie: To set an object down vs. to recline.
Hint: People can tell lies.
Passed vs. Past: Verb (moved by) vs. belonging to a former time.
Precede vs. Proceed: To come before vs. to go forward.
Principal vs. Principle: Most important/head of a school vs. a fundamental truth.
Than vs. Then: Used for comparisons vs. at that time/next.
Their vs. There vs. They're: Possessive, a location, and "they are."

Eliminating "Deadwood" (Wordiness)

Police reports are not prose. Be direct and concise. Avoid unnecessary words and phrases that add length but not substance.

Simplify Phrases:

At this point in time → Now
In the event that → If
In view of the fact that → Because / Since
Make inquiry regarding → Ask about
Subsequent to → After
Utilize → Use

Avoid Redundancy (Needless Repetition):

Adequate enough → Adequate or Enough
Close proximity → Proximity
True facts → Facts
Consensus of opinion → Consensus
Cooperated together → Cooperated
Rate of speed → Speed

The Importance of Field Notes

Definition: Brief notations concerning specific events and circumstances that are recorded while fresh in the officer's mind and used to prepare a report.

Primary Uses:

  • Provides the basis for your official report.
  • Reduces the need to re-contact parties involved.
  • Provides greater accuracy relative to time, statements, and events than memory alone.

Best Practices:

  • Do not include personal comments or non-police information.
  • Notes are subject to scrutiny in court. Ensure they are professional.
  • Be careful when transferring information to your report; ensure there are no discrepancies.
  • Check agency policy regarding the retention or destruction of field notes after a report is completed.

Information to Record in Field Notes

A systematic approach ensures you don't miss critical information. Answer the key questions:

Who?

Correct spelling of names, DOB, address, phone, DL/ID number, aliases, role (victim, suspect, witness).

What?

Type of offense, property involved, means of transport, statements made, unusual actions.

When?

Date and time the offense occurred, was discovered, and was reported. Time of arrests.

Where?

Exact location of offense, evidence, victims, and witnesses. Use reference points.

Why?

Any statements or evidence supporting a possible motive (e.g., revenge, monetary gain).

How?

How the offense occurred, how the suspect approached/exited, how law enforcement was notified.

Describing People, Places, & Things

Describing People:

Use a systematic, head-to-toe method. Note unique identifiers first.

  • Order: Race, Sex, Age, Height, Weight.
  • Head-to-Toe: Hair (color/style), Face (complexion), Eyes, Shirt, Pants, Shoes.
  • Distinguishing Marks: Tattoos, scars, limp, missing teeth, accent, manner of walking.

Describing Vehicles ("CYMBAL"):

  • C - Color (top to bottom)
  • Y - Year
  • M - Make / Model
  • B - Body Style
  • A - And... (distinguishing marks like damage, stickers, aftermarket parts)
  • L - License Plate (state and number)

Using All Senses for Detailed Descriptions:

Go beyond the basics by using all five senses to capture a scene.

Sight

Note colors, speed, facial expressions, size, direction, movement (furtive, steady), neatness, location, proportions, and relation to surroundings.

Hearing

Note volume, pace, distance, and direction of sounds. Identify speech (slurred, raspy, gendered), walking noises (limping, shuffling), car noises (backfire, brake squeal), and other noises (scratching, clicking, slamming, cocking of a weapon).

Touch

Note temperature, texture (powdery, sticky, smooth), wetness, shape, size, firmness, and vibrations. Can be used to identify weapons or other objects in low light.

Smell & Taste

Note distinct odors like perfumes, smoke, gunpowder, fuel, alcohol, drugs, or cooking smells. Taste is often a secondary function of smell (e.g., salty air near the coast).

Structuring a Report Narrative

Chronological vs. Categorical Ordering:

  • Chronological: Arrangement of information in order of occurrence. This is the most common and usually the clearest method for report narratives.
  • Categorical: Arrangement of information by category (e.g., witnesses, suspects, evidence, crime elements). This can be useful for complex cases with multiple people or large amounts of evidence.

Three Basic Kinds of Information:

  1. Identification of involved persons, vehicles, etc.
  2. Narrative description of the offense or incident.
  3. Elements of the offense or probable cause for the arrest.

Report Types & Uses

Common Types of Police Reports:

  • Arrest Reports
  • Incident Reports
  • Offense Reports
  • Crash Reports
  • Supplemental Reports

Official Uses of a Police Report:

  • Permanent record of facts
  • Coordination of follow-up investigation
  • Basis for prosecution and defense
  • Performance evaluations of officer
  • Statistical data and reference material

Fact vs. Opinion & Confidentiality

Separating Fact from Opinion:

Your report must be objective. It should detail the facts of the incident. Opinions, conclusions, and suppositions should be avoided unless they are part of a direct quote or are specifically required by policy (and even then, they must be clearly labeled as such).

Fact: "The suspect had red, glassy eyes and slurred his words."
Opinion: "The suspect was drunk." (This is a conclusion; stick to the observable facts that lead to it).

Confidentiality of Sex Offense Victims - CCP Chapter 58

A victim of a sex offense may choose to use a pseudonym to protect their identity in all public files and records concerning the offense. Your agency policy will dictate the specific procedure for documenting this.
