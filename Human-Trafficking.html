Human Trafficking: An Overview

Human trafficking is a modern-day form of slavery involving the exploitation of humans for sexual gratification or labor. It is a highly profitable, clandestine crime driven by economic/political instability in origin countries and economic prosperity in destination countries. It is the second most profitable international crime, behind only drug trafficking.

Key Definitions (TVPA)

Sex Trafficking

The recruitment, harboring, transportation, provision, or obtaining of a person for a commercial sex act, where the act is induced by force, fraud, or coercion, or the person is under 18 years of age.

Labor Trafficking

The recruitment, harboring, transportation, provision, or obtaining of a person for labor/services, through the use of force, fraud, or coercion for the purpose of subjection to involuntary servitude, peonage, debt bondage, or slavery.

The Three Vital Components

Elements Checklist:

  • Force: The use of a weapon, physical strength/violence to overcome, restrain, or injure a person, or inflicting physical harm to compel submission.
  • Fraud: Deceitful practices or willful devices intended to deprive another of their rights or do them injury, such as false promises of jobs.
  • Coercion: Threats of serious harm or physical restraint; schemes intended to make a person believe failure to act will result in serious harm; or the abuse/threatened abuse of the legal process.

Trafficking vs. Smuggling

Human Trafficking is a crime against a person. Human Smuggling is a crime against a country's sovereignty.

Distinctions Checklist:

  • Consent: Smuggled persons consent to be moved. Trafficked persons do not consent, or their consent is obtained through fraud/deception. Consent is irrelevant in trafficking cases.
  • Exploitation: Smuggling ends when the person reaches their destination. Trafficking involves ongoing exploitation.
  • Transnationality: Smuggling always crosses international borders. Trafficking can occur internationally or domestically.

Prostitution vs. Trafficking

A consensual sex worker is not a human trafficking victim. A trafficking victim is a sexual assault victim.

It is incorrect to say a human trafficking victim was "being prostituted." A person engaged in prostitution can be a victim of trafficking, but a trafficking victim cannot be a prostitute because their involvement is not consensual.

Forms of Trafficking

Forms Checklist:

  • Sex Trafficking: Forced work in the sex industry (prostitution, exotic dancing). Often facilitated online due to anonymity.
  • Labor Trafficking: Forced labor in various sectors like domestic servitude (nanny, maid), agriculture, construction, restaurants, sweatshops, or panhandling.
  • Familial Trafficking: Trafficking or exploitation by a parent, legal guardian, or other family member.
  • Debt Bondage: Traffickers create a situation where a victim must perpetually work to pay off an ever-increasing, unlawful debt (e.g., for travel, room, board).
  • Sex Tourism: When people travel to foreign locations with lax laws to engage in sexual gratification, often with child victims.
  • CSEC (Commercial Sexual Exploitation of Children): A range of crimes involving the sexual abuse of a child for financial benefit. Includes child sex trafficking, child pornography, and survival sex. No proof of force, fraud, or coercion is needed if the victim is under 18.

Factors Fueling Human Trafficking

Several interconnected factors contribute to the prevalence of human trafficking worldwide.

Key Factors Checklist:

  • High Profitability: Trafficking is a low-risk, high-reward crime. Unlike drugs, which are sold once, humans can be sold over and over again for exponential profit.
  • Economic & Political Instability: These are "push factors" in origin countries. Poverty, lack of jobs (especially for women), and war displace populations, making them vulnerable to deceptive recruitment.
  • Globalization & Demand: These are "pull factors" in destination countries. Ease of global travel, technology, and disposable income in affluent nations create a demand for cheap labor and commercial sex.
  • Routine Activities Theory: This theory suggests crime occurs when there is a motivated offender, a suitable target (vulnerable person), and the absence of a capable guardian (e.g., vigilant police and community). Trafficking thrives when the risk of getting caught is low and targets are plentiful.

Trafficking of Persons - PC §20A.02

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person knowingly traffics another person with the intent that the trafficked person engage in forced labor or services, or knowingly traffics another person and causes the person to engage in sexual conduct.

First Degree Felony Second Degree Felony

Elements Checklist (A Person Commits an Offense if they Knowingly):

  • Traffics another person with intent they engage in forced labor/services.
  • Receives a benefit from participating in a venture that involves trafficking for forced labor/services.
  • Traffics a child with intent they engage in forced labor/services.
  • Receives a benefit from participating in a venture that involves trafficking a child for forced labor/services.
  • Traffics another person and causes them to engage in sexual conduct.
  • Receives a benefit from participating in a venture that involves trafficking for sexual conduct.
  • Traffics a child and by any means causes the child to engage in sexual conduct.
  • Receives a benefit from participating in a venture involving the trafficking of a child for sexual conduct.

Practical Example (Brothel Bust): In 2015, Montgomery County SO discovered a brothel operating out of a rented house. Investigation revealed a network of 15 other locations (massage parlors) run by four main suspects. A "madam" ran the operation while two males moved approximately 14 Asian female victims between locations every few weeks to keep them disoriented. The victims were forced to live at the locations and provide sexual services advertised on Backpage.com. Three suspects were arrested and charged with human trafficking, a First Degree Felony, as they knowingly trafficked persons and caused them to engage in sexual conduct for the suspects' financial benefit.

Continuous Trafficking of Persons - PC §20A.03

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if, during a period that is 30 or more days in duration, the person engages in conduct constituting an offense under Section 20A.02 on two or more occasions.

First Degree Felony

Elements Checklist:

  • The person commits two or more acts of Trafficking of Persons (PC 20A.02).
  • The acts occur during a period of 30 days or more.

Practical Example (Nightclub Dancer): A 15-year-old runaway was recruited by a gang member via Snapchat. He gained her trust, then began managing her, coercing her into dancing at an after-hours club for money. He then sold her to another gang member who sexually assaulted her and attempted to force her into prostitution at the club. Because the suspect engaged in recruiting, transporting, and harboring the minor for sexual exploitation on multiple occasions over a period longer than 30 days, he could be charged with Continuous Trafficking of Persons.

Related Texas Statutes

In addition to the primary trafficking offenses, other statutes are crucial for investigations and prosecutions.

Key Statutes Checklist:

  • PC §20A.01. Definitions: Defines key terms like "forced labor or services," "sexual conduct," "traffic," and "child."
  • PC §20A.04. Accomplice Witness: States that a person who is trafficked is NOT an accomplice to the trafficking offense, meaning their testimony does not require corroboration as an accomplice witness would.
  • Family Code §261.001. Definitions (Abuse): Explicitly includes trafficking under the definition of child abuse. This is critical for involving Child Protective Services and Children's Advocacy Centers.

Victim Characteristics & Vulnerabilities

Anyone can be a victim, but some populations are more vulnerable. Traffickers exploit these vulnerabilities.

Common Victim Profile Checklist:

  • Primarily adult women and girls under 17. Male victims are often underreported, especially in labor trafficking.
  • Low level of education or no education.
  • Unemployment or limited job opportunities.
  • Dire economic circumstances (poverty is a key component).
  • Social and economic inequality in their country of origin.
  • Victims of armed conflict, military occupation, or political strife.

Key Risk Factors Checklist:

  • Recent migration or relocation.
  • Substance use and/or mental health concerns.
  • Involvement with the child welfare system.
  • Being a runaway or homeless youth.
  • History of physical/sexual abuse, neglect, or dating violence.

Offender Characteristics & Roles

Trafficking operations can be run by men, women, or both, and often involve individuals known to the victim. Traffickers frequently have criminal histories but may also use legitimate businesses as cover. Operations can range from large, sophisticated networks to small, local 'mom and pop shops'.

Common Trafficker Roles Checklist:

  • Recruiter: Finds and brings victims into the industry, often through deception.
  • Agent (Employment/Travel): Arranges "legitimate" jobs or trips as a front for the operation.
  • Document Forger: Steals or creates false documentation for travel.
  • Transporter (Coyote): Accompanies victims to their destination.
  • Employer (Pimp/Procurer): Purchases the victim and provides the place of employment (brothel, club, etc.).
  • Enforcer (Guard): Provides protection for the business, ensures victims comply, and prevents escape.

Stages of Recruitment and Exploitation

Stages Checklist:

  • Recruitment: Almost always begins with a local recruiter who knows the victim's culture and vulnerabilities. This is most often recruitment-by-persuasion, using false promises and gaining trust. Less common is recruitment-by-force (abduction, kidnapping).
  • Transportation: While common, transportation is not required for trafficking to occur. Victims can be trafficked from their own homes.
  • Exploitation: Begins upon arrival (or when control is established). Passports and documents are confiscated, and the victim learns of the deception and the "debt" they now owe.
  • Control: Maintained through violence (physical/sexual assault), psychological manipulation, isolation, constant supervision, and debt bondage.
  • Resolution: For some, the ordeal ends in death. For those who escape or are freed, they often face shame, ostracism, and life-long health and psychological problems, making them vulnerable to re-trafficking.

The Art of Control

Traffickers use various methods to establish and maintain absolute control over their victims.

Control Methods Checklist:

  • Document Control: Confiscating passports, visas, and other ID to remove the victim's legal identity and make escape difficult.
  • Violence: Using physical and sexual assault to "break in" victims and maintain compliance through fear. Threats of violence against the victim or their family are also common.
  • Psychological Manipulation: Lying, brainwashing, and causing victims to distrust authorities. This can lead to Stockholm Syndrome, where victims develop a false emotional attachment to their trafficker.
  • Isolation & Supervision: Confining victims, monitoring their communications, and never allowing them to be unsupervised.
  • Economic Abuse / Debt Bondage: Creating a fraudulent debt that victims are forced to work to repay.

The Human Trafficking Power and Control Wheel illustrates the tactics used to subjugate victims.

Case Study: The El Monte Sweatshop

One of the most infamous examples of forced labor in the U.S.

Case Details Checklist:

  • Victims: 72 Thai garment workers.
  • Location: A guarded, barbed-wire-enclosed apartment complex in El Monte, California.
  • Exploitation: Forced to work over 18 hours a day for less than $2/hour.
  • Control Methods: Passports confiscated, mail censored, conversations monitored, and escape attempts met with beatings and threats. They were trapped in debt bondage, forced to buy necessities from an overpriced company store.
  • Resolution: On August 2, 1995, a multi-agency task force raided the complex, freeing the workers and arresting the operators.

Observational Cues to Identify Victims

Identifying victims is difficult as they often do not self-identify. Officers should look for clusters of indicators.

General Red Flags Checklist (Blue Campaign):

  • Appears disconnected from family, friends, or community.
  • Shows signs of mental or physical abuse (bruises in various stages of healing).
  • Is fearful, timid, submissive, or disoriented.
  • Appears to be coached on what to say or defers to someone else who controls the situation.
  • Lacks personal possessions or a stable living situation.
  • Has no freedom of movement or lives under unreasonable security measures.

Child Victim Red Flags Checklist:

  • Avoids questions, appears frightened or belligerent with law enforcement.
  • Uses trafficking slang (see list below).
  • Has multiple cell phones, large amounts of cash, or prepaid credit cards.
  • Has tattoos or brandings indicating ownership (e.g., name, barcode, "$").
  • Is recovered at a hotel, street track, truck stop, or strip club.

Practical Example (Pulled Over): An HPD officer stops a suspicious vehicle. The driver is an adult male, and the passenger is a juvenile female who avoids eye contact. The driver can't provide the girl's last name. The girl gives a name and a date of birth that doesn't match her stated age. These inconsistencies and behavioral cues (suspicious pairing, avoiding eye contact, conflicting information) led officers to investigate further, discovering the girl was a runaway being trafficked by the driver.

Trafficking Terminology

Familiarity with sex trafficking terminology can be critical during an investigation.

Common Terms Checklist:

  • The Life/The Game: The sex industry.
  • Bottom Bitch/Girl: A victim appointed by the trafficker to manage others.
  • Quota: The amount a victim must earn for their trafficker each night.
  • Daddy: Term a male trafficker might require victims to call him.
  • Branded: A tattoo a trafficker uses to show ownership.
  • Stable: A group of victims under one trafficker's control.
  • Breaking: The process of breaking a person down through force and control.
  • Gorilla Pimp: An enforcer who uses violence.
  • Track/Stroll: A street location for commercial sex.
  • Turnout: When a trafficker puts someone to work for the first time.
  • Tricks/Johns: Buyers of commercial sex.
  • Twelve (12): Police officer(s).

Victim-Centered Interviewing

Understanding a victim's psychological state is key to building rapport and gathering information.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs:

An effective interviewer knows that victims will not disclose information until their most basic needs are met. An individual who feels physically safe (Safety Needs) and accepted by the interviewer (Love/Belonging & Esteem Needs) is far more likely to cooperate.

Best Practices Checklist:

  • Goal of 1st Interview is a 2nd Interview: Focus on building trust and rapport, not just gathering facts.
  • Provide a Safe Environment: Interview in a non-threatening location like a CAC or shelter, not a police interview room.
  • Use Qualified Interpreters: Do not use other victims as interpreters. Ensure the interpreter is trustworthy and neutral.
  • Be Patient & Empathetic: Use informal conversation, not direct interrogation. Allow the victim to tell their story at their own pace. Do not make promises you can't keep.
  • Work with a Multi-Disciplinary Team (MDT): For child victims, partner with the local Children's Advocacy Center (CAC) for specialized forensic interviews.

Collaboration & Victim-Centered Approach

A proactive, collaborative, victim-centered approach is essential for successful investigations.

Best Practices Checklist:

  • Be Proactive: Conduct trainings, educate the community, identify hot-spots and at-risk populations.
  • Collaborate: Work with other LE agencies (local, state, federal) and social service providers (NGOs). Develop a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) to define roles.
  • Safeguard Victims: Your role is investigation, not providing resources. Rely on victim service providers for care. This frees you to focus on evidence collection for a successful prosecution.

Practical Example (The Blackjack Case): Six young girls were rescued from a sex trafficking operation. Two were identified as victims and housed at a homeless shelter. A well-meaning shelter worker, unaware of the legal process, felt sorry for them and gave them money to return to Mexico. Because the victims were no longer available to testify, human trafficking could not be proven, and the trafficker received a much lesser sentence. This case highlights the critical need for law enforcement and victim service providers to work together and educate each other on their respective roles to ensure justice for victims.

Challenges and Barriers in Investigations

Law enforcement agencies face significant challenges in human trafficking cases.

Common Challenges Checklist:

  • Victim Trust: Difficulty gaining a victim's trust and cooperation due to fear, trauma, and distrust of authority.
  • Lack of Resources: Shortages of appropriate shelters, qualified interpreters, and funding.
  • Lack of Training: Limited training on TVPA/state laws and proper investigative techniques.
  • Lack of Collaboration: Insufficient collaboration among agencies and a lack of standard protocols.

Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA) of 2000

The comprehensive U.S. federal law to combat human trafficking. It follows a three-level approach.

TVPA "3 P's" Checklist:

  • Prevention: Educates vulnerable populations and provides funds to foreign governments to help find legitimate employment.
  • Protection: Provides victims in the U.S. with secure shelter, medical/psychological/legal aid. Allows for temporary visas (T-Visas) if they cooperate with the investigation.
  • Prosecution: Seeks to prosecute traffickers. Establishes severe federal penalties, including up to life imprisonment if the victim is a minor, dies, is kidnapped, or endures severe sexual abuse.

TVPA Reauthorizations (Key Provisions)

The TVPA has been reauthorized multiple times, strengthening its provisions.

  • 2003: Created a federal civil cause of action for victims to sue traffickers; allowed state/local LE to assist in identifying victims for immigration purposes.
  • 2005: Authorized grant programs for state/local LE; addressed sex tourism; expanded jurisdiction to U.S. personnel/contractors abroad.
  • 2008: Required human trafficking info be provided to visa applicants; expanded immigration protections (T-Visas, U-Visas); expanded criminal liability and created a new crime for fraud in foreign labor contracting.
  • 2013: Focused on eliminating trafficking from supply chains; extended statute of limitations for civil actions.
  • 2017: Reauthorized grant programs to combat trafficking and authorized grant funding to train school resource officers.

International Perspective & U.S. Reporting

The Palermo Protocol (2000) is the primary international treaty to prevent, suppress, and punish trafficking. The U.S. State Department publishes an annual Trafficking in Persons (TIP) Report that ranks countries into tiers based on their compliance with TVPA minimum standards.

TIP Report Tiers Checklist:

  • Tier 1: Fully comply with TVPA minimum standards.
  • Tier 2: Do not fully comply, but are making significant efforts to do so.
  • Tier 2 Watch List: Require special scrutiny due to a high number of victims or failure to show evidence of efforts.
  • Tier 3: Do not satisfy minimum standards and are not making significant efforts. May be subject to U.S. sanctions.

Victim Benefits & Services (T-Visas)

Victims of "severe forms of trafficking" who cooperate with law enforcement are eligible for benefits and services, including temporary legal status.

Eligibility & Process Checklist:

  • Eligibility: Victim must be determined to be a "severe victim of human trafficking" (involving force, fraud, coercion, or being a minor in sex trafficking).
  • Continued Presence: A temporary status allowing a victim to remain in the U.S. during an investigation/prosecution.
  • T-Visa: Allows a victim to remain in the U.S. for up to 4 years with work authorization. Requires cooperation with law enforcement and an application (Form I-914). After 3 years, a T-Visa holder may apply for permanent residence.
  • T-Visa Application: The victim must provide a personal narrative detailing the trafficking situation. A declaration from a federal LE officer (Form I-914, Supplement B) serves as primary evidence of victimization and cooperation.

Specific Victim Rights & Benefits (TVPA)

The TVPA outlines specific rights and services for certified victims.

Rights Checklist:

  • The right to access information about their rights and translation services.
  • The right to rescue and be removed to a secure shelter.
  • The right to medical care (physical and psychological).
  • The right to social assistance (job counseling, skills training, education) and a work permit.
  • The right to be heard in court and have legal representation.
  • The right to mandatory restitution and civil compensation.
  • The right to privacy and safety.
  • The right to seek residency (T-visa).
  • The right to return to their country of origin.
