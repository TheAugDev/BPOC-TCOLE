Identity Crimes

BPOC Chapter 12 | TCOLE Preparation

Defining Identity Theft & Crime

Identity Theft: The theft of your personal information (name, SSN, etc.) to commit fraud, such as opening new accounts, using existing accounts, or obtaining medical services without your permission.

Identity Crime: The theft or misuse of personal or financial identifiers to gain something of value and/or facilitate other criminal activity.

Consequences for Victims

Identity theft can have serious consequences, including negatively affecting a victim's credit, resulting in them being sued for debts that are not theirs, adding incorrect and potentially harmful information to their medical records, and even leading to their wrongful arrest.

Statistical Overview (2019)

According to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), Texas ranked 4th in the nation for identity theft reports, with 73,553 cases reported.

Top 5 Types of Identity Theft:

  1. Credit card fraud (new accounts)
  2. Miscellaneous identity theft (online shopping, social media fraud, etc.)
  3. Mobile telephone (new account)
  4. Business and personal loans
  5. Auto loan or lease

Associated Crimes

Identity crime is often a facilitator for other criminal activities, providing financing or anonymity. These can include:

Mail Theft/Fraud Narcotics/Drugs Organized Crime Money Laundering Mortgage Fraud Weapons Trafficking Homicide Terrorism Computer Crime

Special forms also include: Tax Identity Theft, Child Identity Theft, and Medical Identity Theft.

Key Terminology

Identifying Information

Information that alone, or in conjunction with other information, identifies a person. This includes a person's name and DOB, unique biometric data (fingerprint, voice print), unique electronic ID number or financial account number, SSN or government ID number, and telecommunication identifying information or access device.

Telecommunication Access Device

A card, plate, code, account number, PIN, or other means of account access that can be used to obtain money, goods, services, or to initiate a transfer of funds.

Fraudulent Use or Possession of Identifying Information

Texas Penal Code § 32.51

Legal Definition

A person commits an offense if the person, with the intent to harm or defraud another, obtains, possesses, transfers, or uses an item of identifying information of another person without the other person's consent; information concerning a deceased person; or identifying information of a child younger than 18.

Punishment Level

State Jail FelonyIf the number of items is less than 5.
3rd Degree FelonyIf the number of items is 5 to 9.
2nd Degree FelonyIf the number of items is 10 to 49.
1st Degree FelonyIf the number of items is 50 or more.

Increased to next higher category if committed against an elderly individual or to facilitate a sex offender registration offense.

A person is presumed to have intent to harm/defraud if they possess identifying information of 3 or more people.

Elements Checklist

  • A person
  • With intent to harm or defraud another
  • Obtains, possesses, transfers, OR uses
  • An item of identifying information of another person, a deceased person, or a child < 18
  • Without the other person's consent/legal authorization

Practical Example

An officer stops a vehicle and finds a wallet containing four different driver's licenses and four corresponding credit cards, none of which belong to the driver. The driver has no legitimate reason for possessing these items. Since the driver possesses identifying information for four people, intent to harm or defraud is presumed, and they can be charged with a State Jail Felony.

Mail Theft

Texas Penal Code § 31.20

Legal Definition

A person commits an offense if the person intentionally appropriates mail from another person's mailbox or premises without the effective consent of the addressee and with the intent to deprive that addressee of the mail.

Punishment Level

Base Offense
Class A MisdemeanorIf mail is from less than 10 addressees.
State Jail FelonyIf mail is from 10 to 29 addressees.
3rd Degree FelonyIf mail is from 30 or more addressees.
Enhanced Offense (if mail contains ID info)
State Jail FelonyIf mail is from less than 10 addressees.
3rd Degree FelonyIf mail is from 10 to 19 addressees.
2nd Degree FelonyIf mail is from 20 to 49 addressees.
1st Degree FelonyIf mail is from 50 or more addressees.

Elements Checklist

  • A person
  • Intentionally appropriates mail
  • From another person's mailbox or premises
  • Without the addressee's effective consent
  • With intent to deprive the addressee of the mail

Practical Example

A resident reports seeing someone walking down the street, opening mailboxes and taking letters. An officer locates the suspect, who has a bag containing mail addressed to 15 different people on that street. Among the stolen items are several pre-approved credit card offers. The suspect can be charged with a State Jail Felony for the base offense, which is enhanced to a 3rd Degree Felony because the stolen mail contained identifying information intended for identity fraud.

Fraudulent Use or Possession of Credit Card or Debit Card Information

Texas Penal Code § 32.315

Legal Definition

A person commits an offense if the person, with the intent to harm or defraud another, obtains, possesses, transfers, or uses: a counterfeit credit card or debit card; the number and expiration date of a card without consent; or the data stored on the digital imprint of a card without consent.

Punishment Level

State Jail FelonyIf the number of items is less than 5.
3rd Degree FelonyIf the number of items is 5 to 9.
2nd Degree FelonyIf the number of items is 10 to 49.
1st Degree FelonyIf the number of items is 50 or more.

A person is presumed to possess items without consent if they possess 5 or more.

Elements Checklist

  • A person
  • With intent to harm or defraud another
  • Obtains, possesses, transfers, OR uses
  • (1) A counterfeit card, (2) Card number/exp. date without consent, OR (3) Digital imprint data without consent

Practical Example

During a search, an officer finds a credit card skimmer device on a suspect. A forensic analysis reveals the device contains the stored digital imprint data from 12 different credit cards. The suspect can be charged with a 2nd Degree Felony under this statute.

Disposing of a Payment Card Skimmer

Texas Business & Commerce Code § 607.103

Legal Definition

A person commits an offense if the person negligently or recklessly disposes of a skimmer installed by another person. A person commits a higher offense if they dispose of a skimmer knowing an investigation or criminal proceeding is ongoing.

Punishment Level

Class B MisdemeanorFor negligent or reckless disposal.
3rd Degree FelonyFor disposal while knowing an investigation is ongoing.

Elements Checklist

  • A person
  • (1) Negligently or recklessly disposes of a skimmer, OR
  • (2) Disposes of a skimmer knowing an investigation is ongoing

Practical Example

A gas station owner finds a skimmer on a pump. Fearing bad publicity, he throws it in a dumpster. An investigation later identifies him. He could be charged with a Class B Misdemeanor. If he was already notified by police of an investigation and then threw it away, the charge would be a 3rd Degree Felony.

Key Federal Statutes

Identity Theft and Assumption Deterrence Act of 1998

This act made it a federal crime to "knowingly transfer or use, without lawful authority, a means of identification of another person with the intent to commit... any unlawful activity that constitutes a violation of federal law, or that constitutes a felony under any applicable state or local law." (18 U.S.C. § 1028)

Identity Theft Penalty Enhancement Act of 2004

Established penalties for "aggravated" identity theft. This requires the court to add a mandatory sentence of two additional years for a general offense and five additional years for a terrorism-related offense on top of the sentence for the underlying felony.

Identity Theft Enforcement and Restitution Act of 2008

Clarified that restitution for victims can include reimbursement for time spent remediating the harm. It also allowed federal courts to prosecute cases where the criminal and victim live in the same state, removing a previous jurisdictional hurdle.

How Identity Crime is Perpetrated

Thieves use a variety of low-tech and high-tech methods to obtain personal information:

Stealing Wallets/Purses: Direct theft of physical items containing IDs, credit cards, and checkbooks.
Stealing Mail: Intercepting bank statements, credit card offers, or tax information.
Filing a Change of Address: Diverting a victim's mail to another location controlled by the thief.
Dumpster Diving: Rummaging through personal or business trash for discarded documents.
Fraudulently Obtaining Credit Reports: Posing as a landlord or employer to get a copy of a victim's credit report.
Theft from Homes: Finding personal information inside a victim's residence.
Business Record Theft: Stealing files from offices, bribing employees, or hacking into company databases.
Shoulder Surfing: Watching someone enter a PIN at an ATM, sometimes using hidden cameras.
Spoofing/Phishing: Creating fake emails and websites that look legitimate to trick users into entering personal data.

Current Trends & Techniques

Criminals constantly evolve their methods. Current trends often involve technology, such as listening to conversations on cellular phones, hacking computers, and conducting sophisticated telephone and email scams. A wide variety of sources, including bookstores and internet retailers, also provide step-by-step instructions on producing false documents.

For the latest information on new trends, scams, and consumer alerts, a valuable resource is the Identity Theft Resource Center at www.idtheftcenter.org.

Writing the Offense Report

A police report is crucial for victims. Credit bureaus and financial institutions often require it to block fraudulent activity and begin the credit repair process. The report should contain:

  • Detailed Information: Use a specific form like the Identity Crime Incident Detail Form to capture all relevant data. This helps organize the investigation.
  • Distinguish the Crime: Determine if it's true identity theft (a new account was opened) or credit/debit card abuse (an existing account was used).
  • Establish Jurisdiction: Under CCP Art. 13A.260, the offense can be prosecuted in the county where it was committed OR in the victim's county of residence. This allows the victim's local agency to take the report.
  • Gather Documentation: Ask the victim to gather bank statements, letters from creditors, and credit reports. This evidence is vital for the investigation.

Governmental & Business Entities to Notify

Depending on the crime, various entities should be notified:

Federal Agencies

  • FBI: For bank fraud, government fraud, or schemes with losses > $100,000.
  • U.S. Secret Service: For any custody arrest involving identity crime devices (skimmers, counterfeit docs).
  • U.S. Postal Service: For financial crimes involving U.S. Mail.
  • Social Security Administration: For misuse of an SSN involving program fraud or significant loss.
  • Federal Trade Commission (FTC): Victims MUST file a report at IdentityTheft.gov to get a recovery plan.

State & Business Entities

  • Texas DPS: Responsible for driver's licenses and criminal records.
  • Credit Reporting Agencies (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion): Victims must place a "fraud alert" on their files.
  • Financial Institutions: Victims should contact their banks and credit card companies immediately.
  • Utility Companies: Victims should check for unusual account activity.

Personal Protection Guidelines

Offline Security

  • Don't give out personal info over the phone unless you initiated the call.
  • Limit what you carry; leave your Social Security card at home.
  • Do not print your SSN or DL# on checks.
  • Pay attention to billing cycles; a missing bill could be a red flag.
  • Read statements from your health insurance plan to check for fraudulent claims.
  • Destroy labels on prescription bottles before discarding them.
  • Shred all documents with personal information before discarding.
  • Secure your SSN; only give it out when absolutely necessary.

Online Security

  • Create complex, unique passwords for different accounts.
  • Be alert for imposters (phishing emails, texts, and calls).
  • Safely wipe personal information from electronics before disposing of them.
  • Use encryption (look for the "lock" icon) before sending financial information.
  • Don't overshare on social media; thieves can use details to answer security questions.

Device Security

  • Use security software (anti-virus, firewall) and keep it updated.
  • Avoid phishing emails and suspicious links/downloads.
  • Be cautious on public Wi-Fi; use a secure network or VPN for sensitive transactions.
  • Do not store financial info or enable auto-login on laptops or mobile devices.

Steps for Victims of Identity Crime

If an identity crime occurs, victims should take these steps immediately:

  1. File a Police Report: Contact the local police or sheriff's office to file a report. Get a copy of the report number and the report itself, as it's essential for the next steps.
  2. Report to the FTC: Go to IdentityTheft.gov to create an Identity Theft Report and get a personalized recovery plan. This is a critical step.
  3. Place a Fraud Alert: Contact one of the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion). That one bureau is required to notify the other two. This will make it harder for someone to open new accounts in the victim's name.
  4. Contact Financial Institutions: Notify banks and credit card companies of the fraud. Close any compromised accounts and get new cards/account numbers. Dispute any fraudulent charges.
  5. Notify the Social Security Administration: Report the unauthorized use of a Social Security number to the SSA's Office of the Inspector General.
  6. Contact DPS: Contact a driver license office for assistance. The victim may need to complete a Forgery Affidavit.
  7. Establish a Stolen Identity File: Contact the local Sheriff's Office to establish a Stolen Identity File, which is reported to a statewide DPS database to help prevent wrongful arrests.
  8. Notify the U.S. Passport Agency: If a passport was lost or stolen, or if there is reason to believe one could be fraudulently obtained, notify the agency to be on alert.

Security Alert vs. Security Freeze

These are two tools consumers can use to protect their credit files, as defined in the Texas Business and Commerce Code, Chapter 20.

Security Alert

A notice on a consumer file that alerts a potential creditor that the consumer's identity may have been used fraudulently.

  • How to Request: By writing or telephone to a credit reporting agency.
  • Timeline: Must be placed within 24 hours.
  • Duration: Remains in effect for at least 45 days.
  • Cost: Free.
  • Special Note: An "active-duty alert" provides extra protection for deployed service members.

Security Freeze

A notice that prohibits a credit reporting agency from releasing a consumer's credit report without express authorization from the consumer.

  • How to Request: By written request via certified mail.
  • Timeline: Must be placed within 5 business days.
  • Duration: Remains until the consumer requests it be lifted.
  • Cost: May involve fees for placing or lifting.
  • Special Note: The agency provides a unique PIN or password for the consumer to use when authorizing access.

Venue for Prosecution

Venue determines the county (or counties) where a criminal case can be legally tried.

CCP Art. 13A.260 - Fraudulent Use of ID Info

An offense under TPC §32.51 may be prosecuted in:

  • Any county where the offense was committed; OR
  • The county of residence of the victim.

CCP Art. 13A.255 - Credit/Debit Card Abuse

An offense under TPC §32.31 may be prosecuted in:

  • Any county where the offense was committed; OR
  • The county of residence of the cardholder.

Procedure for Expunction

Under CCP Art. 55A.256, a person who was arrested solely because an identity thief used their information can apply to have the arrest records expunged (destroyed).

Application Process:

  • The person entitled to expunction files an application with the prosecuting attorney in their county of residence.
  • The application must be verified and include fingerprints.
  • It must include key details of the wrongful arrest (date, offense, agency).
  • It must include a statement that the applicant is not the person who was actually arrested and did not give consent for their identity to be used.
