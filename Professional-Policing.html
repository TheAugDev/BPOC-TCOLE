Historical Development of Policing

American policing has its roots in European methods, particularly from England, with concepts like the Sheriff, County, and Common Law. Early American policing in the northeast used the "watch and ward" system ("watch" at night, "ward" during the day).

1900 - 1940s: The Reform Era

  • Use of the automobile and radio communications led to rapid response but distanced officers from the community.
  • Marked by contributions from August Vollmer and J. Edgar Hoover (FBI) to professionalize policing.
  • Wickersham Commission (1931) stated the "greatest promise for the future of policing is the college or university."

1950 - 1970s: The Professional Era

  • Technology (computers, improved communications) became more common.
  • Emphasis on accountability, standards, and performance metrics (response time, crime stats).
  • President's Commission (1967) recommended 2-4 years of college education for officers.
  • Specialized units (traffic, investigations) became common, removing patrol from follow-up activities.
  • Patrol became an "incident-driven" model, with officers often acting as mere report takers.

1980 - Present: Community Era

  • Continued technological advancements.
  • Development of Community-Oriented and Problem-Oriented Policing models.
  • Renewed emphasis on walking beats and positive citizen interaction as part of professionalization.

Evolution of Policing in Texas

During its time as a province of Mexico, policing in Texas was handled by the Spanish military. After the Battle of San Jacinto in 1836, Texas developed its own unique law enforcement traditions due to its large territory and lack of large urban centers.

Texas Rangers

Legal Definition:

Stephen F. Austin employed a militia group to protect settlers from Indian and bandit raids. This group became known as the Texas Rangers and served as the primary law enforcement in early Texas.

Key Roles:

  • Statewide law enforcement agency.
  • Major violent crime investigations.
  • Public corruption investigations.
  • Officer-involved shooting investigations.
  • Border security operations.

Elected County Law Enforcement

Legal Authority:

  • Constables: Texas Constitution Art. 5, Sec. 18
  • Sheriffs: Texas Constitution Art. 5, Sec. 23

Key Roles:

  • Sheriff: Chief law enforcement officer of the county; operates the county jail; serves civil process.
  • Constable: Works with the Justice of the Peace courts; serves warrants and civil papers.

Municipal Law Enforcement

Legal Authority:

  • Type A General-law: Local Gov't Code 341.001
  • Type C General-law: Local Gov't Code 341.002
  • Home-Rule: Local Gov't Code 341.003

Key Roles:

Provide police services to cities and towns, handling everything from traffic enforcement to criminal investigations within their municipal boundaries.

Traditional Police Service Model

Model Definition:

A reactive, incident-driven model where success is measured by statistics like crime rates and response times. Officers act as report takers responding to calls for service.

Elements Checklist:

  • Reactive Response: Handling crimes that have already occurred.
  • Incident-Driven: Focuses on resolving one incident at a time.
  • Use of Criminal Justice System: Primary method of resolution is arrest and prosecution.
  • Use of Aggregate Statistics: Performance measured by citywide crime/arrest rates.
  • Lack of Community Involvement: Decisions made at management level.
  • Citizen & Employee Expectations: Expectations are often not met, as officers have a low impact on preventing crime in this model.
  • Specialized Units: High use of traffic, investigative, and other specialized units.

Practical Example:

An officer spends their entire shift driving from one dispatched call to the next (a burglary, a theft, a disturbance), writing reports for each, and then waiting for the next call from dispatch. There is little time for proactive work or follow-up.

Community Policing Model

Model Definition:

A philosophy that police and citizens working together can solve community problems related to crime, fear of crime, and social disorder. It shifts focus from handling random calls to proactive problem-solving.

Elements Checklist:

  • New Relationship with Community: Citizens have a voice in setting police priorities.
  • Community Policing Officers (CPOs): Officers assigned to a specific beat to build rapport and trust.
  • Proactive Element: Officers act as problem solvers, planners, and community organizers.
  • Focus on Vulnerable Populations: Stresses protecting juveniles, the elderly, LGBTQIA+, people of color, and other at-risk groups.
  • Partnerships: Works with citizens and other public/private agencies.

Practical Example:

A CPO notices a rise in car break-ins at a city park. Instead of just taking reports, the officer works with the Parks Dept. to improve lighting, organizes a "Park Watch" with local residents, and increases their own random patrols during peak times, leading to a reduction in incidents.

Key Concepts in Community Policing

Importance of Community Involvement

  • Reducing fear by addressing problems of crime.
  • Establishing trust and harmony.
  • Exchanging information to enhance safety.
  • Assisting in identifying and resolving problems.
  • Clarifying responsibilities for citizens and police.
  • Helping define service needs.

Philosophical Differences

  • Traditional model is incident-driven and **reactive**.
  • Community policing is problem-oriented, **pro-active**, and **co-active**.
  • The CPO's role is more complex: Law enforcer, planner, problem solver, and community organizer.

Community Policing vs. Police-Community Relations

Feature Community Policing Police-Community Relations
Goal Solve problems; improved relations are a byproduct. Change attitudes and project a positive image; improved relations are the main focus.
Function Line function: Regular, direct contact between officers and citizens. Staff function: Irregular contact, often through a specific PCR unit.
Officer's Role A full-service officer; leader and catalyst for change. Consultant on crime issues; focus on strained relations.
Agenda Setting "Bottom-up": Citizens nominate problems and help set the police agenda. "Top-down": Committees or leadership identify problems and "preach" to the police.
Accountability Ensured by citizens receiving the service, in addition to administration. Ensured by civilian review boards and formal police supervision.
Impact Meaningful organizational change and restructuring. Traditional organization stays intact with new programs added periodically.

Networking with Community Partners

To effectively solve problems, officers must network with other professionals. It is crucial to recognize when to call on the expertise of other agencies to improve the quality of life in the community.

Legal Services

  • Private Attorneys/Bail Bondsmen: An officer should NEVER make a referral to a private attorney or bail bondsman due to potential conflicts of interest and liability.
  • Local Lawyer Referral Service: Operated by County or District Bar Associations to connect citizens with legal help.
  • County/District Attorney: To directly file criminal cases or apply for protective orders.
  • Small Claims Court: For civil cases involving money matters up to $10,000 where parties can represent themselves (GC 28.003).
  • Grand Jury: Citizens may apply directly to a grand jury to hear a criminal action.

Social & Victim Services

  • State Dept. of Mental Health and Mental Retardation (MHMR): For issues related to alcohol problems, mental illness, and counseling services.
  • Texas Dept. of Health and Human Services: Handles Child Protective Services (CPS) and Adult Protective Services (APS).
  • Local Crisis Centers: Provide shelter and resources for battered/abused women and children.
  • Sexual Assault Response Teams (SARTs): County-level, multi-disciplinary teams (LE, prosecutors, advocates, healthcare) that work to improve the response to adult sexual assault.
  • Multidisciplinary Teams (MDTs): Focus on the investigation and prosecution of child abuse and treatment for child victims.
  • Domestic Violence High Risk Teams (DVHRTs): Multi-disciplinary teams focused on reducing the risk of lethal violence for family violence victims.
  • Commercially Sexually Exploited Youth (CSEY) Teams: Provide comprehensive, holistic services to CSEY victims.
  • Child Fatality Review Teams: Investigate unexpected or preventable deaths of persons under 18.

Crime Stoppers

Program Definition:

A community program that allows citizens to provide anonymous information about criminal activity. Cash rewards are offered for information that leads to an arrest or indictment, encouraging community involvement in fighting crime.

Key Elements:

  • Anonymity: Tipsters are guaranteed anonymity to protect them from retaliation.
  • Partnership: Operates as a partnership between the community, media, and law enforcement.
  • Rewards: Offers financial incentives for useful information.

For more information, visit the Texas Crime Stoppers website.

Procedural Justice & Police Legitimacy

Legal Definition:

Procedural Justice argues that police legitimacy is linked to public judgments about the fairness of the processes through which police make decisions and exercise authority. Police Legitimacy is the community's view of whether police have acted lawfully and legally.

The Four Principles of Procedural Justice (Elements):

  • Give People a Voice: Allow people to tell their side of the story and listen.
  • Neutrality: Make decisions based on facts, not bias (be fair).
  • Respectful Treatment: Treat everyone with dignity and respect.
  • Trustworthiness: Comes from a fair and transparent process where you explain the reason for your actions.

Practical Example:

During a traffic stop for speeding, an officer politely explains the reason for the stop, listens to the driver's explanation without interrupting (Voice), treats the driver with courtesy (Respect), explains that the radar reading requires a citation based on policy (Neutrality), and issues the citation while explaining the court process clearly (Trustworthiness). Even if the driver is unhappy with the ticket, they are more likely to perceive the officer and the process as fair.

Quality Customer Service

Values:

A quality-driven agency sees itself as a business where customer service is highly valued. The desired outcome is a well-served community and a professional, respected, service-oriented organization.

Rewards:

The rewards include building pride within the agency and the community, and enjoying strong, loyal support from the community on a day-to-day basis.

Police Subculture

The informal values, beliefs, and expectations passed on to new officers. Understanding the subculture is key to understanding officer behavior and public perception.

Characteristics of Police Subculture

  • Curiosity and suspiciousness
  • Solidarity (taking care of one another)
  • Secrecy (protection from a perceived hostile environment)
  • Political and social conservatism
  • Cynicism
  • Social isolation
  • Authoritarian approach to control

Subculture Problems & Benefits

Problems:

  • Stress (continuous reinforcement cycle)
  • Commitment to community needs becomes secondary
  • Cynicism and alienation

Benefits:

  • Support during difficult and stressful times

Officers must ultimately choose between the negative aspects of subculture and true professionalism.
