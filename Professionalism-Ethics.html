The Concept of Professionalism

Formal Definitions

Oxford English Dictionary: "The occupation which one professes to be skilled in and to follow. A vocation in which professed knowledge of some branch of learning is used in its application to the affairs of others..."

Webster's Dictionary: "A vocation or occupation requiring advanced training in some liberal art or science, and usually involving mental rather than manual work..."

Value of the Professional Model

Advantages

  • Public is better served
  • Quality of peace officers is improved
  • Stronger community support and respect
  • More effective problem-solving
  • Financial rewards

Disadvantages

  • Higher cost of training and development
  • Higher salaries required
  • Limited entry into the workforce for some due to educational requirements

Characteristics of a Profession

  • Specialized body of knowledge obtained through advanced education or training.
  • Commitment to serving the public and promoting the common good.
  • A professional creed or code of ethics holding members to a higher standard.
  • Standards for entry, practice, and ethical conduct.
  • A professional association to maintain standards.
  • Requirement for continuing education and lifelong learning.

Police Legitimacy & Procedural Justice

Police Legitimacy means the community has trust and confidence in the police, accepts police authority, and believes officers are fair. Officers build this trust by treating people with dignity and respect, making fair decisions, and giving people a voice.

The Four Pillars of Procedural Justice

Fairness & Consistency

Decisions are made based on fair and consistently applied processes, not just outcomes.

Giving Voice

All people want to be heard. Allowing individuals to tell their side of the story builds trust.

Transparency

Processes are open and clear, not secret. This helps people understand and accept decisions.

Impartiality

Decisions are based on relevant evidence and data, free from personal opinion or bias.

Objectivity and Implicit Bias

Objectivity involves the expression or use of facts without distortion by personal feelings or prejudices. In contrast, Implicit Bias describes the automatic association people make between groups of people and stereotypes about those groups. These biases can exist even in the absence of conscious bigotry.

Awareness of personal feelings, prejudices, and blind spots is the first step toward achieving objectivity and mitigating the negative influence of implicit bias in law enforcement decisions.

Community Policing Model

Partnerships

Collaborative partnerships between law enforcement and the community to develop solutions and increase trust.

Problem-Solving (S.A.R.A.)

Proactive and systematic examination of problems to develop effective responses.

  • Scanning: Identifying and prioritizing problems.
  • Analysis: Researching what is known about the problem.
  • Response: Developing solutions for lasting reductions.
  • Assessment: Evaluating the success of the responses.

Organizational Transformation

Aligning agency management, structure, and personnel to support community partnerships and proactive problem-solving.

Ethics in Law Enforcement

Ethics involves defining and achieving what is good or bad, right or wrong, in relation to moral duty. For law enforcement, the motive is as important as the act. The ethical role is performed by being objective, impartial, neutral, and ethical.

Pillars of Character

  • Trustworthiness
  • Respect
  • Responsibility
  • Fairness
  • Caring
  • Citizenship

Ethical Decision-Making Model

  • Is it legal?
  • Is it permitted by your organization's code of conduct?
  • How would it be viewed by your organization's code of ethics?
  • Does your personal code of ethics approve?
  • Is it a true ethical dilemma (both choices appear right)?
  • Can you apply your organization's ethical decision-making model?

Career Attitude Stages

Officers often progress through various attitude stages in their careers, which can influence ethical behavior.

  1. Idealistic: New officers, confident in their values and ability to succeed.
  2. Frustrated: Realism sets in; initial goals clash with the reality of the job.
  3. Defiant: Fear is overcome, but actions can be negative or destructive, driven by resentment.
  4. Resigned: Slips into disappointment and aimlessness; "punching the clock."
  5. Aware: Realizes the damage caused by negative stages and recognizes the need for change.
  6. Decisive: Consciously takes positive steps to take responsibility for one's own life and happiness.
  7. Commitment: A prudent and purposeful devotion to one's ideals and abilities.

Officers are most vulnerable to making poor ethical decisions in the Defiant and Resigned stages.

Physical vs. Moral Courage

Physical Courage

The state of mind that enables one to face danger with self-possession, confidence, and resolution.

  • Facing a barricaded, armed suspect.
  • Physically intervening to stop another's misconduct (CCP 2.1387 Duty to Intervene).

Moral Courage

The courage to take action for moral reasons despite the risk of adverse consequences.

  • Refusing gratuities.
  • Refusing to participate in a cover-up.
  • Refusing to participate in inappropriate humor or jokes.
  • Reporting misconduct of a fellow officer.

Law Enforcement Canons of Ethics

These canons outline the fundamental duties and responsibilities of a law enforcement officer.

  • Primary Responsibility: Protection of the people through upholding laws, especially the Constitution.
  • Limitations of Authority: Know and respect the legal bounds placed upon them.
  • Duty to be Familiar with the Law: Assiduously study and understand the laws they are sworn to uphold.
  • Utilization of Proper Means: The ends do not justify illegal means.
  • Cooperation with Public Officials: Cooperate with other officials in the discharge of their duties.
  • Private Conduct: Lead a decent and honorable life, as their conduct reflects on the entire service.
  • Conduct Toward the Public: Be neither overbearing nor subservient; inspire confidence and trust.
  • Gifts and Favors: Firmly refuse gifts, favors, or gratuities that could be interpreted as influencing their judgment.
  • Presentation of Evidence: Be concerned equally with the prosecution of the wrongdoer and the defense of the innocent. Present evidence impartially.
  • Attitude Toward Profession: Regard their duties as a public trust and an honorable profession.

Chapter 36: Bribery and Corrupt Influence

Offenses related to improperly influencing public servants.

Bribery 2nd Degree Felony

TPC 36.02

Elements Checklist:

  • A person intentionally or knowingly
  • Offers, gives, solicits, or accepts
  • A "benefit"
  • In exchange for an official action or decision.

Practical Example:

A driver offers an officer $100 to not write a speeding ticket. If the officer accepts, both have committed Bribery.

Coercion of Public Servant Class A Misd.

TPC 36.03

Elements Checklist:

  • A person uses coercion (a threat)
  • To influence or attempt to influence a public servant's official duty.

Practical Example:

A city council member threatens to have an officer fired unless the officer drops a ticket for the council member's son.

Tampering with Witness 3rd Degree Felony

TPC 36.05

Elements Checklist:

  • A person, knowing an investigation or proceeding is pending or in progress
  • Offers a benefit, coerces, or harms a witness
  • To influence their testimony or cause them to elude process.

Practical Example:

A suspect in a robbery case offers the convenience store clerk $1,000 to "forget what you saw" when testifying in court.

Obstruction or Retaliation 3rd Degree Felony

TPC 36.06

Elements Checklist:

  • A person intentionally or knowingly
  • Harms or threatens to harm another
  • In retaliation for or to prevent their service as a public servant, witness, or informant.

Practical Example:

An individual who was arrested finds out which citizen reported them and slashes the tires on that citizen's car.

Chapter 37: Perjury and Other Falsification

Offenses related to false statements and tampering with evidence or records.

Aggravated Perjury 3rd Degree Felony

TPC 37.03

Elements Checklist:

  • A person commits Perjury (false statement under oath)
  • The false statement is made during or in connection with an official proceeding
  • And the statement is "material" (could have affected the outcome).

Practical Example:

In a murder trial, a witness testifies under oath that the defendant was with them across town at the time of the killing, knowing this is false. This alibi is material to the case.

Tampering w/ Physical Evidence 3rd Degree Felony

TPC 37.09

Elements Checklist:

  • Knowing an investigation or official proceeding is pending or in progress
  • A person alters, destroys, or conceals any record, document, or thing
  • With intent to impair its verity or availability as evidence.

Practical Example:

An officer finds a small amount of drugs on a suspect but, feeling sympathetic, flushes them down a toilet before they can be logged into evidence.

Tampering w/ Governmental Record Class A Misd.*

TPC 37.10

Elements Checklist:

  • Knowingly makes a false entry in, or alteration of, a governmental record.
  • Or presents/uses a fake document intending for it to be treated as real.
  • *Can be enhanced to a felony if done with intent to defraud or harm.

Practical Example:

An officer intentionally writes in an offense report that a witness identified a suspect, when the witness actually said they weren't sure.

Chapter 39: Abuse of Office

Offenses involving public servants misusing their official position.

Abuse of Official Capacity Class A Misd.*

TPC 39.02

Elements Checklist:

  • A public servant
  • With intent to obtain a benefit or harm another
  • Intentionally or knowingly violates a law relating to their office or misuses government property, services, or personnel.
  • *Punishment varies by the value of the property/service misused.

Practical Example:

A city mechanic uses city tools and the city garage after hours to run a private auto repair business for personal profit.

Official Oppression Class A Misd.

TPC 39.03

Elements Checklist:

  • A public servant acting under color of law
  • Intentionally subjects another to mistreatment, unlawful arrest/search, etc.
  • Or intentionally denies someone's rights, knowing the conduct is unlawful.
  • Or intentionally subjects another to sexual harassment.

Practical Example:

An officer detains a citizen for "contempt of cop" and conducts a search of their car without probable cause, simply because the citizen was disrespectful.

Misuse of Official Information 3rd Degree Felony

TPC 39.06

Elements Checklist:

  • A public servant
  • Uses confidential information obtained through their job
  • To gain a financial benefit for themselves or another.

Practical Example:

An officer learns through an investigation that a new highway will be built at a specific location. Before this is public, the officer tells a friend to buy the land there to sell it for a profit later.

Federal Criminal Law

Unethical conduct can lead to federal prosecution for civil rights violations, often investigated by the FBI out of Washington D.C. to prevent conflicts of interest.

Conspiracy Against Rights

Federal Crime

18 U.S.C. § 241

Elements Checklist:

  • Two or more people
  • Conspire (agree)
  • To injure, oppress, threaten, or intimidate someone
  • In the exercise of their constitutional rights.

Practical Example:

Two officers agree to pull over and harass a specific journalist to intimidate them into stopping their reporting on the police department. This infringes on the journalist's First Amendment rights.

Deprivation of Rights Under Color of Law

Federal Crime

18 U.S.C. § 242

Elements Checklist:

  • A person acting "under color of law" (on duty)
  • Willfully
  • Deprives someone of their constitutional rights.

Practical Example:

An officer, during a traffic stop, intentionally uses excessive force against a compliant subject, violating their Fourth Amendment right to be free from unreasonable seizure.

Civil Penalties & Sexual Harassment

Inappropriate conduct can subject an officer and their department to civil lawsuits and financial penalties.

Sexual harassment is prohibited under both state and federal law. Key statutes and agencies include:

  • TPC 39.03 - Official Oppression (as a criminal offense).
  • Texas Commission on Human Rights Act (Civil penalties).
  • Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) (Federal oversight).
  • 42 U.S.C. § 1983 - Civil Action for Deprivation of Rights (Allows civil suits for rights violations).
