Elements of a Crisis Reaction

Trauma is a disordered psychic or behavioral state resulting from severe mental or emotional stress or physical injury. The crisis reaction is the normal human response to trauma. It follows a predictable pattern and includes physical and psychological components.

Practical Example: An officer responds to a violent aggravated assault on a public street. The victim may be experiencing physical shock ("frozen fright"), a racing heart from the "fight-or-flight" response, and be in the psychological stage of Shock, Disbelief, and Denial as officers arrive. Understanding these reactions helps the officer interact effectively.

Physical Response

Based on animal instincts, the physical response includes physical shock, disorientation, and numbness, often called "frozen fright."

"Fight-or-Flight" Reaction Checklist:

  • Adrenaline pumps through the body.
  • Body may relieve itself of excess materials (e.g., ingested food).
  • Physical senses may become very acute while others "shut down."
  • Heart rate increases.
  • Hyperventilation, sweating, etc.
  • Physical arousal leads to eventual exhaustion.

Psychological Response Stages

Stage 1: Shock, Disbelief, and Denial

A defense mechanism to protect from the full impact of the event. The person truly believes it didn't happen or wasn't as bad as it was.

Stage 2: Cataclysm of Emotions

An emotional roller coaster including anger/rage, fear/terror, grief/sorrow, confusion/frustration, guilt/self-blame, violation/vulnerability, and shame/humiliation.

Stage 3: Reconstruction of Equilibrium

Emotions eventually become balanced as the person integrates the experience.

Additional Psychological Responses Checklist:

  • Slowed thinking
  • Fearful thoughts
  • Memory problems
  • Distressing dreams
  • Blaming
  • Illogical thinking
  • Flashbacks of previous trauma
  • Intrusive thoughts
  • Poor judgment
  • Difficulty making decisions

Behavioral Response

Behavioral Response Checklist:

  • Crying spells
  • Extreme hyperactivity
  • Change in activity level
  • Withdrawal
  • Increase/decrease in appetite, sleep, or sex drive
  • Increased smoking, drug, and/or alcohol use
  • Startles easily
  • Conflicts with others
  • Change in hygiene and/or self-care
  • Change in social patterns/communication
  • Significant increase/decrease in productivity
  • Avoiding reminders of the event
  • Inability to stop focusing on what occurred
  • Getting immersed in recovery-related tasks

The Range of the Crisis Reaction

A victim's response can encompass a wide range of feelings and symptoms.

Full Range Checklist:

  • Shock
  • Depression and loneliness
  • Panic
  • Hostility and resentment
  • Hope
  • Emotion
  • Physical symptoms of distress
  • Guilt
  • Inability to resume normal activities
  • Affirming reality

The Ripple Effect of Victimization

The impact of crime spreads beyond the immediate victim.

Progression Checklist:

  • Immediate Victim: The person the crime was committed against.
  • Secondary Victims: Family and friends of the immediate victim.
  • Service Providers: EMS, police, and other responders who are affected.

Trauma and Associated Losses

Trauma is accompanied by a multitude of losses, leading to grief and bereavement.

Common Losses Checklist:

  • Control over one's life
  • Sense of fairness or justice
  • A sense of immortality and invulnerability
  • Trust in God or in other people
  • Personally significant property, self, or loved ones
  • Future

Trauma and Regression

Trauma can be so overwhelming it causes a person to revert to childlike behaviors, both mentally and physically. For example: feeling "little" or weak, wanting "mommy" or "daddy," singing nursery rhymes, assuming a fetal position, or calling an officer "mommy" or "daddy."

Practical Example: A detective interviews a victim of an aggravated robbery 48 hours after the event. The victim is emotional and may express feelings of great loss (e.g., loss of safety, trust). They may display signs of hostility or resentment (part of the crisis reaction range) and feel that their entire outlook on the world has changed.

Recovery from Immediate Trauma

Recovery is often affected by several factors. While many can recover with informal support from friends and peers, outside intervention can be useful.

Factors Affecting Recovery Checklist:

  • Severity of crisis reaction
  • Ability to understand what happened in retrospect
  • Stability of victim/survivor equilibrium after the event
  • Supportive environment
  • Validation of the experience (e.g., acknowledging their feelings are natural and understandable)

Intensity Continuum: The intensity of the reaction varies based on crime-specific details, victim-specific factors, and cultural differences.

Recovery Issues and Long-Term Reactions

Recovery involves working through several key issues. Many victims continue to experience reactions in response to "trigger events," though the intensity usually decreases over time.

Recovery Issues Checklist:

  • Getting control of the event in the victim's mind
  • Working out an understanding of the event and, as needed, a redefinition of values
  • Re-establishing a new equilibrium/life
  • Re-establishing trust
  • Re-establishing a future
  • Re-establishing meaning

Common "Trigger Events" Checklist:

  • Identification of the assailant
  • Sensing something similar to the trauma (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch)
  • "Anniversaries" of the event
  • Proximity of holidays or significant life events
  • Hearings, trials, appeals or other critical phases of the criminal justice proceeding
  • Media articles about a similar event

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

A disorder experienced by many survivors of severely traumatic events like combat, rape, or disasters. It involves re-living the event, avoiding reminders, and symptoms of physiological hyper-arousal. About half of patients recover within a few months, while others face years of incapacity.

Key Criteria for Diagnosis (DSM-V) Checklist:

  • The Event: Exposure to an event involving actual or threatened death/serious injury with a response of intense fear, horror, or helplessness.
  • Re-experiencing (1+): Intrusive thoughts/images, distressing dreams, flashbacks, or intense psychological/physiological reactions to cues.
  • Avoidance/Numbing (3+): Avoiding thoughts/feelings/places related to the trauma, inability to recall an important feature of the event, loss of interest, feeling detached, restricted emotions, or feeling one's life will be brief.
  • Hyper-arousal (2+): Insomnia, irritability/angry outbursts, poor concentration, excessive vigilance, or increased startle response.
  • Duration/Impairment: Symptoms last longer than one month and cause clinically important distress or impairment in work, social, or personal functioning.

Practical Example: The symptoms of PTSD can help explain the behavior of a battered person. Their "persistent avoidance of stimuli" can be seen as an advanced form of denial—a survival mechanism focused on getting through each day. Hysterical reactions can be related to "persistent symptoms of increased arousal," such as hypervigilance or an exaggerated startle response.

Phases of a Victim's Reaction

1. Impact

Occurs immediately after the crime. Victims feel shock, disbelief, vulnerability, and helplessness. They may not be able to eat or sleep.

2. Recoil

Victims attempt to adapt and reintegrate. They experience a wide variety of emotions like guilt, fear, anger, self-pity, and sadness. Some deny these feelings.

3. Reorganization

After a period of time, feelings of fear and rage diminish. The victim becomes more normalized and puts the experience in perspective, committing energy to living in the present.

Trauma-Informed Investigations

A victim-centered, collaborative approach to understand the impact of trauma, reduce re-traumatization, and enhance healing. It's based on the "4 R's" and six key principles.

The "4 R's" Checklist:

  • Realize the prevalence of trauma and understand its effects.
  • Recognize the signs and symptoms (e.g., exhaustion, confusion, numbness, dissociation, inappropriate laughter, hysteria, blunt affect).
  • Respond by applying trauma-informed principles in language, behaviors, and policies.
  • Resist re-traumatization during interactions.

Six Key Principles Checklist:

  • Safety: Victim feels physically, emotionally, and psychologically safe.
  • Trustworthiness and Transparency: Develop trust and be open.
  • Peer support: Support victims to aid in recovery.
  • Collaboration and mutuality: Partner with victims and share power.
  • Empowerment, voice, and choice: Allow victims to share in decision-making.
  • Cultural, historical, and gender issues: Be responsive to cultural needs and free of bias/stereotypes.

Practical Example: A detective interviews a victim of an acquaintance/date rape sexual assault. The officer must remain professional and sensitive, understanding that the victim's calm demeanor may be a trauma response (e.g., numbness, dissociation). Using a trauma-informed approach—like asking open-ended questions and giving the victim control over the interview pace—is crucial for building trust and gathering information effectively.

How to Help Victims: Effective Response

Officer Actions Checklist:

  • Assure victims their reactions are natural and understandable.
  • Recognize "frozen fright" or disassociation.
  • Understand victims may deny or minimize the impact of the assault.
  • Be aware that victims may experience physical pains (fatigue, muscle tension, illness) as a result of the trauma.
  • Note that victims may become overly fearful and/or hypervigilant.
  • Contact a local victim advocate.
  • Communicate empathy and allow victims to vent to build rapport and cooperation.
  • Help victims regain a sense of control (e.g., ask if they need you to call someone).
  • Establish a team effort ("we are going to review this information together").
  • Consider cultural issues (e.g., lack of eye contact may not be disrespect).
  • Make appropriate notifications and arrangements for victims with specific physical or mental disabilities.

Effective Interviewing Techniques

Use a victim-centered, trauma-informed approach for initial interviews.

Interviewing Guidelines Checklist:

  • Reassure: State that it's okay not to know all the answers and that they can provide details later. This alleviates the urge to fill in memory gaps.
  • Use Categorical Interviewing: Allow victims to share fragmented memories as they remember them, rather than forcing a strict chronological timeline.
  • Ask Open-Ended Questions: e.g., "What are you able to tell me about your experience?" or "What can't you forget?"
  • Ask Non-Leading Questions: e.g., "You mentioned a gun. Tell me more about what you can remember about that."
  • Avoid "Why" Questions: Instead of "Why didn't you leave?", say "Tell me about the conditions that prohibited you from leaving." Explain why you need information (e.g., "There could be evidence on your clothes. Are those the clothes you had on?").
  • Don't Interrupt: Let the victim finish speaking. Take notes and ask for clarification after.
  • Focus on What They Remember: Build on the details they are able to recall.
  • Give Control and Choice: Allow the victim to take breaks and decide where to begin.
  • Use Active Listening: Give undivided attention without judgment. Do not fill in memory gaps for the victim.
  • Be Patient: Give the victim time to process the trauma during the interview.

Avoiding Secondary Victimization

Long-term stress reactions can be worsened by the negative actions of others, known as a "secondary assault" or "secondary injury."

Potential Sources of Secondary Victimization Checklist:

  • The criminal justice system
  • The media
  • Family, friends, or acquaintances
  • Clergy
  • Hospital and emergency-room personnel
  • Health and mental health professionals
  • Social service workers
  • Victim service workers
  • Schools or educators

Economic Impact Checklist:

  • Medical care
  • Lost earnings
  • Property loss/damage

Recommended Procedures for Death Notification

Properly notifying next-of-kin is a critical and sensitive law enforcement duty.

Notification Checklist:

  • Notify as soon as the deceased's identity has been established.
  • Get all the medical information possible about the person(s) to be informed.
  • Go in person. Do not call.
  • Have at least one officer in uniform.
  • Talk about your feelings with your partner beforehand.
  • If a child answers the door, request to speak to their parents.
  • Present your credentials and ask to come inside.
  • Sit down. Don't be stiff and formal.
  • Convey the information simply and directly.
  • Ask parents if they want to tell the children or if they want you to.
  • Don't discount their feelings or yours.
  • Be prepared for the bereaved to go into shock.
  • Empathize with the survivors in their grief.
  • Answer questions honestly.
  • Provide information without jeopardizing a criminal investigation.
  • Offer to make calls for them.
  • Inform survivors it might be necessary for them to identify the deceased.
  • Explain why an autopsy is necessary and how to obtain a copy of the report.
  • Tell hospital personnel what information the family has been given.
  • Inform survivors that law enforcement might need to question them later.
  • Do not leave the survivor(s) alone.
  • Stay with the survivor(s) if they wish and help with decisions.
  • Provide the survivor(s) with the officers' names and telephone numbers.
  • Contact appropriate agencies in other jurisdictions to notify additional surviving family members.
  • Preserve and label the package containing the deceased's clothing and personal effects.
  • Allow hospitals adequate time to prepare the body for identification/viewing.
  • Provide survivors with sufficient time with the deceased.
  • Talk to the media only if the family has given permission.
  • Give the survivor(s) an information sheet with pertinent information.
  • Make a follow-up call the next day.
  • Let the survivors know you care.

Legal Basis for Victims' Rights

Several Texas laws outline the rights of crime victims and the responsibilities of law enforcement.

Key Statutes Checklist:

  • Rights of Crime Victims: Texas Constitution Article 1, Section 30
  • General Rights: Code of Criminal Procedure Art. 56A.051
  • Duties re: Victims of Sexual Assault: Code of Criminal Procedure Art. 56A.403
  • Victim's Rights (Juvenile): Family Code Sec. 57.002
  • Victim's Right to Privacy: Code of Criminal Procedure Art. 56A.101
  • Confidentiality of Sex Offense Victims' Info: Code of Criminal Procedure Art. 58

Victim Liaisons & Advocates

The law provides for specific roles to assist victims through the criminal justice process.

Key Roles Checklist:

  • Victim Assistance Coordinator: A person designated by a prosecutor's office to ensure victims receive their rights. (CCP Art. 56A.201)
  • Presence of Advocate during Exam: A victim of sexual assault has the right to have an advocate present during a forensic medical exam. (CCP Art. 56A.351)
  • Presence of Advocate during Interview: A victim of sexual assault also has the right to have an advocate present during law enforcement interviews. (CCP Art. 56A.3515)

Written Notice Requirements

Law enforcement has a duty to provide written notice of rights and other key information to victims.

Key Notification Statutes Checklist:

  • Definitions: Key terms used in victims' rights statutes are defined in CCP Art. 56A.001 and CCP Art. 56B.003.
  • Notification of Rights: Law enforcement must provide victims of sexual assault with a written notice of their rights. (CCP Art. 56A.401)
  • Notice of Release of Family Violence Offenders: An agency must make a reasonable attempt to notify a victim when a family violence offender is released from custody. (CCP Art. 42.21)
  • Notice by Local Law Enforcement Agency: Local agencies must provide victims with information about the Crime Victims' Compensation program. (CCP Art. 56B.007)

Outcomes of Effective Victim Assistance

Positive Outcomes Checklist:

  • Strengthens faith and confidence in the justice system.
  • Improves relationships and partnerships between law enforcement and the community.
  • Increases knowledge of what works in order to improve public safety and reduce disparities within the justice system.
  • Leads to comprehensive justice system improvements and protections based upon science-informed policy and practice development.
