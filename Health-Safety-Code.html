Health & Safety Code: Controlled Substances

BPOC Chapter 15 | TCOLE Preparation

Key Definitions (HSC §481.002)

Administer: Directly applying a controlled substance to a patient's body.

Controlled Substance: A substance listed in a penalty group.

Controlled Substance Analogue: A substance with a chemical structure substantially similar to a controlled substance in PG 1 or 2.

Counterfeit Substance: A substance that bears an unauthorized trademark or label that falsely identifies it as a controlled substance.

Deliver or Offer to Deliver: To transfer a controlled substance to another person.

Dispense: To deliver a controlled substance based on a lawful prescription.

Distribute: To deliver a controlled substance other than by administering or dispensing.

Drug: A substance intended for use in the diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease.

Drug Paraphernalia: Equipment or material used to plant, cultivate, manufacture, test, package, store, or use a controlled substance.

Immediate Precursor: A substance the director finds to be a principal compound commonly used in the manufacture of a controlled substance.

Manufacture: The production, preparation, or processing of a controlled substance.

Marihuana: The plant Cannabis sativa L., its seeds, and any derivatives with a THC concentration > 0.3%.

Narcotic Drug: Substances produced from opium, opiates, or their derivatives (e.g., cocaine).

Opiate: A substance having an addiction-forming or addiction-sustaining liability similar to morphine.

Person: An individual, corporation, government, business trust, estate, trust, partnership, or association.

Possession: Actual care, custody, control, or management.

Production: The manufacturing, planting, cultivating, growing, or harvesting of a controlled substance.

Marihuana vs. Hemp

The key legal distinction between marihuana and hemp is the concentration of delta-9 tetrahydrocannabinol (THC).

Marihuana (Illegal)

> 0.3% THC

Considered a controlled substance under HSC Chapter 481.

Hemp (Legal)

≤ 0.3% THC

Regulated under Agriculture Code Chapter 121.

Note: A quantitative analysis (lab report) is required to prove the THC concentration in a substance to legally differentiate between hemp and marihuana for prosecution.

Six Major Categories of Controlled Substances

  • Narcotics: Opiates and their derivatives (e.g., heroin, morphine, fentanyl, codeine). Used to relieve pain.
  • Depressants: Drugs that slow down the central nervous system (e.g., barbiturates, benzodiazepines, alcohol).
  • Stimulants: Drugs that speed up the central nervous system (e.g., cocaine, amphetamines, methamphetamine).
  • Hallucinogens: Drugs that alter perception, thoughts, and feelings (e.g., LSD, PCP, psilocybin mushrooms).
  • Marihuana: Cannabis sativa L. with a THC concentration greater than 0.3%.
  • Simulated Controlled Substances: Substances purported to be a controlled substance that are not.

Texas Compassionate-Use Program (OC Ch. 169)

This program allows qualified physicians to prescribe low-THC cannabis for medical use to patients with specific medical conditions like epilepsy, multiple sclerosis, or cancer.

  • Low-THC Cannabis: Defined as containing not more than 0.5% by weight of THC.
  • Medical Use: The ingestion by a means other than smoking of a prescribed dose of low-THC cannabis.
  • Defense to Prosecution: It is a defense if the person charged with possessing low-THC cannabis is a patient under an approved treatment plan.
  • Smoking: Does not include the use of a vaporizer or e-cigarette. The law specifically prohibits smoking.

Offenses by Penalty Group

Penalty Group 1 / 1-B

Includes: Heroin, Cocaine, Methamphetamine, Fentanyl, Oxycodone, GHB.

Possession (§481.115): State Jail Felony (increases with weight)

Manufacture/Delivery (§481.112, §481.1123): Felony of the Second Degree (increases with weight, PG 1-B has higher penalties)

Elements Checklist (Possession):

  • A person
  • Knowingly or intentionally
  • Possesses
  • A substance listed in Penalty Group 1 or 1-B.

Practical Example (Possession): An officer finds a small baggie containing a white powdery substance in a driver's pocket during a search incident to arrest. The substance field tests positive for cocaine. The driver is arrested for POCS PG1.

Penalty Group 2 / 2-A

Includes: Psilocybin (mushrooms), Ecstasy (MDMA), PCP, Synthetic Cannabinoids (K2).

Possession (§481.116): State Jail Felony (increases with weight)

Manufacture/Delivery (§481.113): Felony of the Second Degree (increases with weight)

Elements Checklist (Manufacture/Delivery):

  • A person
  • Knowingly
  • Manufactures, delivers, or possesses with intent to deliver
  • A substance listed in Penalty Group 2 or 2-A.

Practical Example (Manufacture): Officers execute a search warrant on a residence and find chemicals, glassware, and a large quantity of synthetic cannabinoids being packaged for sale. The residents are arrested for MAN/DEL PG2-A.

Penalty Group 3 / 4

Includes: Valium, Xanax, Ritalin, Codeine (in limited quantities), Anabolic Steroids.

Possession (§481.117, §481.118): Class A Misdemeanor (PG3) or Class B Misdemeanor (PG4) (increases with weight)

Manufacture/Delivery (§481.114): State Jail Felony (increases with weight)

Elements Checklist (Possession PG3):

  • A person
  • Knowingly or intentionally
  • Possesses
  • A substance listed in Penalty Group 3.

Practical Example (Possession): During a consent search of a purse, an officer finds a bottle of Xanax pills with no prescription label. The owner admits to buying them from a friend and is arrested for POCS PG3.

Marihuana

Cannabis Sativa L. with THC > 0.3%.

Possession (§481.121): Class B Misdemeanor (for 2oz or less, increases with weight)

Delivery (§481.120): Class B Misdemeanor (for 1/4 oz or less, for no remuneration. Increases otherwise.)

Elements Checklist (Delivery):

  • A person
  • Knowingly or intentionally
  • Delivers
  • Marihuana.

Practical Example (Delivery): An officer observes one person hand a joint to another person in a park. Upon contact, the first person admits they "gave their friend a joint." Since it was a small amount and no money was exchanged, it's Delivery of Marihuana Class B.

Other Key Offenses

Manufacture/Delivery Causing Death or SBI (HSC §481.141)

Punishment Level: The penalty is increased to the next higher category.

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Commits an offense of Man/Del of a controlled substance
  • And a person dies or suffers SBI
  • As a result of injecting, ingesting, inhaling, or introducing the substance into their body.

Practical Example: A dealer sells what they claim is heroin, but it is actually pure fentanyl. The user overdoses and dies. The dealer can be charged with Man/Del, and the penalty will be enhanced to the next higher level.

Simulated Controlled Substances (HSC Ch. 482)

Punishment Level: Class A Misdemeanor for delivery.

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Knowingly or intentionally
  • Delivers
  • A simulated controlled substance with representations that it is a controlled substance.

Practical Example: A person sells a bag of crushed drywall to an undercover officer, claiming it is cocaine. The seller is arrested for Delivery of a Simulated Controlled Substance.

Possession of Chemicals w/ Intent to Manufacture (HSC §481.124)

Punishment Level: Varies based on substance, generally a Second Degree Felony.

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • With intent to unlawfully manufacture a controlled substance
  • Possesses or transports
  • Anhydrous ammonia, or certain precursor chemicals (e.g., pseudoephedrine).

Practical Example: A trooper stops a car and finds an unusual amount of cold medicine containing pseudoephedrine, along with lithium batteries and other known meth precursors. The driver is arrested for Possession of Chemicals with Intent to Manufacture.

Possession or Delivery of Drug Paraphernalia (HSC §481.125)

Punishment Level: Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Knowingly or intentionally
  • Uses, possesses with intent to use, or delivers
  • Drug paraphernalia.

Practical Example: During a traffic stop, an officer sees a glass pipe with marihuana residue in plain view in the car's cup holder. The driver is issued a citation for Possession of Drug Paraphernalia.

Falsifying Drug Test Results (HSC §481.133)

Punishment Level: Class B Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Knowingly or intentionally
  • Uses or possesses with intent to use
  • A substance or device designed to falsify a drug test.

Practical Example: A person on probation is found with a "Whizzinator" or a bottle of synthetic urine before their scheduled drug test. They can be arrested for Falsifying Drug Test Results.

Sale of Kratom to a Minor (HSC §444.004)

Punishment Level: Class C Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Sells, gives, or causes to be sold or given
  • A kratom product
  • To an individual younger than 18 years of age.

Practical Example: A smoke shop employee sells a package of kratom capsules to a 17-year-old without checking their ID. The employee has committed an offense.

Offense Enhancements & Special Circumstances

Deadly Weapon Finding (PC §1.07): If a deadly weapon is used during a drug offense, a State Jail Felony can be punished as a Third Degree Felony, and inmates must serve at least half their sentence before parole eligibility.

Delivery to a Child (HSC §481.122): Delivering any controlled substance or marihuana to a person under 18 is a Felony of the Second Degree.

Manufacture of PG1 in Presence of Child (HSC §481.1122): If a child under 18 is present where a PG1 substance (like methamphetamine) is being manufactured, the penalty is increased to the next higher category.

Endangering a Child (PC §22.041): A person commits a State Jail Felony if they intentionally, knowingly, recklessly, or with criminal negligence engage in conduct that places a child younger than 15 in imminent danger of death or injury, including by manufacturing, possessing, or using a PG1 or 1-B substance in the child's presence.

Directing Activities of Criminal Street Gang (PC §71.023): Penalties are enhanced for manufacturing, delivering, or possessing large amounts of PG 1-B substances in connection with a gang.

Diversion by Registrants (HSC §481.1285): It is a felony offense for a person authorized to dispense controlled substances (like a pharmacist or doctor) to divert those substances for their own use or illegal distribution.

Affirmative Defenses to Possession

Under various sections of the HSC (e.g., §481.115(g)), it is an affirmative defense to prosecution for possession if the actor was:

  • An officer or agent of a law enforcement agency acting in the lawful performance of their duties.
  • A person authorized by law to possess the substance, such as a pharmacist, practitioner, or their agent.
  • An individual who obtained the substance pursuant to a valid prescription or order from a practitioner.
  • An employee of a licensed laboratory or research facility acting within the scope of their employment.

Dangerous Drugs (HSC Chapter 483)

A dangerous drug is a device or a drug that is unsafe for self-medication and that is not included in Penalty Groups I through IV. It essentially means any prescription drug that is not a controlled substance.

A prescription is an order from a practitioner for a dangerous drug.

A manufacturer is a person, other than a pharmacist, who manufactures dangerous drugs.

Possession of Dangerous Drug (HSC §483.041)

Punishment Level: Class A Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Possesses
  • A dangerous drug
  • Unless obtained via a valid prescription.

Practical Example: An officer finds a bottle of antibiotics with someone else's name on it in a person's bag. The person admits they got the pills from a friend. They can be arrested for Possession of a Dangerous Drug.

Delivery or Offer of Delivery of Dangerous Drug (HSC §483.042)

Punishment Level: State Jail Felony

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Delivers or offers to deliver
  • A dangerous drug.

Practical Example: A person sells their prescription for a non-controlled blood pressure medication to someone without a prescription. The seller has committed a State Jail Felony.

Manufacture of Dangerous Drug (HSC §483.043)

Punishment Level: State Jail Felony

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Manufactures
  • A dangerous drug
  • And is not authorized by law to do so.

Practical Example: A person is found operating an unlicensed laboratory in their garage, creating and packaging pills that are determined to be a dangerous drug.

Forging or Altering a Prescription (HSC §483.045)

Punishment Level: Class B Misdemeanor (increases if previously convicted)

Elements Checklist:

  • A person
  • Forges a prescription
  • OR increases the prescribed quantity in a prescription
  • OR issues a prescription bearing a forged signature.

Practical Example: A person gets a prescription for 30 pills, but uses a pen to change the number to 80 before taking it to the pharmacy. They have committed an offense.

Drug Interdiction: What to Look For

Vehicle Indicators

  • Vehicle not registered to driver (especially rentals).
  • Tool markings on screws, bolts, or trim.
  • Vehicle appears heavily laden in the rear.
  • Presence of false compartments.
  • Unusual odors (marihuana, chemicals, air fresheners).

Driver / Occupant Indicators

  • Extreme nervousness, fidgeting, avoids eye contact.
  • Driver attempts to quickly separate from the vehicle.
  • Conflicting stories between occupants.
  • Extended travel with minimal or no luggage.
  • Driver doesn't have a key to the trunk or glovebox.

Interview Techniques & Probable Cause

Effective interviewing is key to developing probable cause. Consider these steps:

  • Separate Suspects: Remove occupants from the vehicle and each other to prevent them from aligning their stories.
  • Establish Identity: Confirm the identity of the driver and any passengers.
  • Inquire about Travel: Ask about their origin, destination, and the purpose of the trip. Compare their answers.
  • Verify Documents: Ask for registration, insurance, and rental agreements. Check that names and dates are consistent with their story.
  • Inquire about Ownership: Ask who owns the vehicle and its contents.

Common Paraphernalia By Use

Injection: Needles, syringes, spoons, lighters, tourniquets.

Inhalation: Plastic bags, balloons, pipes (glass, metal, wood), bongs, foil.

Ingestion: Gelatin capsules, tablets, vials.

Application: Patches, droppers, foam/gel containers.

Common Concealment Locations

On the Person

  • Clothing, Body cavities, Belts, Shoes/socks, Hats, Jewelry, Bags, Hair, Taped to body

In a Place (House)

  • Toilet tanks, Furniture, Appliances, Cabinets, Drains, Food canisters, Ceiling tiles, Vents

In a Vehicle

  • Glove box, Dash, Under carpet, Gas tanks, Hub caps, Headliner, A/C ducts, Trunk, Door panels, Spare tire, Engine compartment, Ash tray

Seizure and Destruction of Evidence

Controlled Substances (HSC §481.159)

Law enforcement agencies may, after a case is closed, destroy seized drugs and paraphernalia. The law requires a written order from the court and a detailed inventory of the destroyed items. Samples must be preserved if required.

Dangerous Drugs (HSC §483.074)

Similar to controlled substances, dangerous drugs seized by law enforcement are subject to forfeiture and can be destroyed by order of the court after the legal proceedings are concluded.

Testimony in Drug Cases

Testimony of Accomplice (CCP Art. 38.14)

A defendant cannot be convicted on the testimony of an accomplice unless it is corroborated by other evidence that tends to connect the defendant to the offense.

Testimony of Undercover Officer (CCP Art. 38.141)

A defendant cannot be convicted of a drug offense if the only evidence is the uncorroborated testimony of an undercover officer or special investigator who the defendant was introduced to for the purpose of the investigation.

Practical Example: If an undercover officer buys drugs from a suspect, the prosecution needs more than just that officer's testimony to get a conviction. This could be audio/video of the transaction, testimony from a confidential informant who was present, or physical evidence linking the suspect to the drugs.

Uncorroborated Testimony (Dangerous Drugs) (HSC §483.072)

A conviction under the Dangerous Drugs Act may be obtained on the uncorroborated testimony of a party to the offense. This is a key difference from the rules for controlled substance cases.

Identifying Suspected Substances

Besides lab analysis, officers can use several resources to help identify drugs in the field:

  • Agency Labs: Suspected substances should be sent to an accredited lab (like Texas DPS) for definitive analysis.
  • Field Test Kits: If authorized, these kits can provide presumptive positive results, but they are not a substitute for lab analysis. Officer safety is paramount when handling unknown substances.
  • Reference Materials: Websites like Drugs.com or a Physicians' Desk Reference (PDR) can help identify pills by their inscriptions, shape, and color.
