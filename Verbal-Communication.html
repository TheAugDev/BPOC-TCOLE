Emotional Intelligence (EQ)

Definition: The capacity to identify and manage one's emotional state, as well as that of others; sensitive to emotional signals from within and from the social environment. High EQ is the foundation of effective communication for de-escalation.

Key Characteristics of EQ:

  • Self-awareness: Understanding one's own attitude, thoughts, and behaviors.
  • Self-control: Managing one's emotions and impulses.
  • Motivation and determination: Driving oneself to achieve goals.
  • Social competency & empathy: Understanding and influencing others' emotions.
"Information is giving out; communication is getting through." - Sydney J. Harris

Crisis & Officer-Led Escalation

Definition of Crisis: A temporary state of upset and disorganization, characterized by an inability to cope with a particular situation using customary methods of problem solving.

Reasons for Officer-Led Escalation:

An officer's inability to communicate properly can unintentionally escalate hostilities. This may be due to:

  • The officer's message is good, but the delivery failed (poor paralinguistics or non-verbals).
  • An authoritarian thought process.
  • A lack of a developed communication skill set.
  • Challenges in emotional intelligence (e.g., acting out of anger, fatigue, or worry).
  • "Contempt of Cop" mindset, where the officer is triggered by a citizen's perceived disrespect.

Authoritative vs. Authoritarian

How an officer is perceived dramatically impacts public interaction.

Authoritative

Able to be trusted as accurate; reliable. Commanding, self-confident; likely to be respected and obeyed. An officer's goal is to be authoritative.

Authoritarian

Enforcing very strict obedience to authority; an autocrat or dictator; oppressive. This approach often escalates conflict.

Connection: Elements, Components & Barriers

Three Essential Elements of Communication:

  • Thought: Something we know. (The logical part of the message)
  • Emotion: Something we feel. (The emotional part that connects)
  • Action: Something we do. (The non-verbal follow-through)

Four Components of Connection (John C. Maxwell):

1. Connecting Visually: Humans remember 85-90% of what they see. Professional appearance and body language are critical.
2. Connecting Intellectually: Know your subject matter. The message must be logical and sensible to be credible.
3. Connecting Emotionally: People may hear words, but they feel attitude. Energy, intensity, and conviction are key.
4. Connecting Verbally: Tone, inflection, timing, volume, and pacing (paralinguistics) communicate meaning.

Four Barriers to Connection:

  • Immaturity: The inability to see and act on behalf of others.
  • Ego: Developing an unhealthy, strong ego that prevents building bridges with people.
  • Failure to Value Everyone: Every person needs to feel valued, important, and significant.
  • Insecurity: Placing too much focus on oneself instead of others.

Active Listening

Definition: The most important communication skill. It requires the listener to fully concentrate, understand, respond, and then remember what is being said. It involves observing verbal and nonverbal cues without judgment.

Elements Checklist:

  • Listen to what is being said (word choices).
  • Listen to how it's being said (tone, inflection, rate).
  • Observe gestures, body language, and eye contact.
  • Note what is *not* being said or questions that are avoided.
  • Suppress your own emotional internal dialog to truly hear the other person.

Practical Example: Positive Body Language for Listening

To show you are engaged, use open body language: hands with open palms, feet pointed toward the speaker, head tilted slightly, arms uncrossed, and a relaxed, involved facial expression.

Paraphrasing & Empathy

Paraphrasing

Definition: Putting another's meaning into your own words and delivering it back to them. It is one of the most powerful tools of communication and the only way to interrupt someone without generating further resistance.

View the 14 Benefits of Paraphrasing
  1. You have hooked the personâ€”they are listening.
  2. You take control because you are talking and they are listening.
  3. You are making sure you heard right on the spot.
  4. If you have not heard accurately, they can correct you.
  5. You are helping to make the other person a better listener.
  6. You have created some degree of empathy.
  7. It overcomes "sonic intention" (when people think they said something because they heard it in their mind).
  8. It has a clarifying effect for people standing around.
  9. It prevents "metaphrasing" (putting inaccurate words into other people's mouths).
  10. It can be used in reverse ("To be sure I said what I intended... could you tell me what you understood?").
  11. It is a safeguard for yourself to ensure you understood a supervisor's orders.
  12. It generates a "fair-play response" (they psychologically reciprocate).
  13. It etches facts into your mind.
  14. It helps deflect insults and keeps the conversation focused.

Practical Example: "Let me be sure I heard what you said. You're feeling angry because you believe your neighbor intentionally blocked your driveway."

Empathy

Definition: The psychological identification with or vicarious experiencing of the feelings, thoughts, or attitudes of another. It's the ability to see the world from their perspective and tell them what you see.

Empathy is a key tension-buster and is crucial for getting people to listen and accept help.

Power of Questions, Respect & Apologies

Using Questions Effectively:

Asking questions is more effective than giving orders. When a question is asked, the brain instinctively scans for an answer. This builds rapport, shows interest, and can direct an encounter. Just 3-4 good questions with active listening can often de-escalate hostilities.

Personalized Respect & Courtesy:

All humans have an innate need for respect. Using a person's name signifies recognition and personalizes the encounter. Simple courtesy, often overlooked, is a sign of professionalism and strength, not weakness.

The Strength in Apologizing:

Apologizing when appropriate is not a sign of weakness, but of self-confidence, maturity, and good character. It can de-escalate situations by catching people off guard and showing humanity.

The 7%-38%-55% Rule of Communication

Dr. Albert Mehrabian's research shows that in face-to-face communication, the impact of a message is determined by more than just words.

Key Concepts Checklist:

  • 7% Verbal: The actual words spoken.
  • 38% Paralinguistic: How the words are said. This includes tone of voice, inflection, rate, pitch, timing, and hesitation.
  • 55% Non-Verbal (Kinesics): Communication without words. This includes facial expressions, eye movement, gestures, and body language.

Practical Application: When there is a conflict between a person's words and their body language, believe the non-verbal cues. Your own body language can support or negate your verbal message.

Kinesics: Reading Body Language

Non-verbal communication reveals a person's mood, attitude, and intent, often subconsciously. Be aware of their body language and your own.

"I'm Not Listening" Cues Checklist:

  • Repeatedly rubbing neck, ears, or eyes
  • Avoiding eye contact or rolling eyes
  • Crossing arms
  • Tapping fingers or feet
  • Grooming or checking equipment
  • Looking at a phone or watch

Practical Example: An officer says, "I understand your frustration," while tapping their foot, checking their watch, and avoiding eye contact. The citizen's subconscious mind will reject the officer's words because the body language (55%) signals disinterest and impatience, escalating the situation.

Six Principles of Persuasion

These principles can be used ethically to influence behavior and gain compliance.

1. Reciprocity: The obligation to give when you receive. People feel compelled to repay what another person has provided.
2. Scarcity: People want more of things there are less of. We are programmed to see value in rarity.
3. Authority: People follow credible, knowledgeable experts. Projecting command presence and expertise is key.
4. Consistency: People like to be consistent with things they have previously said or done. Ask for small, voluntary commitments.
5. Liking: People prefer to say "yes" to those they like. We like people who are similar to us, pay us compliments, and cooperate with us.
6. Consensus: People look to the actions of others to determine their own, especially when uncertain.

Five Universal Truths of Human Interaction

From Dr. George J. Thompson's *Verbal Judo*, these truths are fundamental to de-escalation.

  1. All people want to be treated with dignity and respect.
  2. All people want to be asked rather than told to do something.
  3. All people want to be told why they are being asked to do something.
  4. All people want to be given options rather than threats.
  5. All people want a second chance.

Practical Example: Instead of shouting "Move your car now or I'll tow it!" (a threat), an officer could use the Universal Truths: "Sir, I need to ask you to move your car for safety reasons so an ambulance can get through. You can either move it to that spot over there, or I will have to have it towed. What would you prefer?" This gives a reason, provides an option, and asks instead of tells.

You Can't Win an Argument

Arguments create defensiveness, and defensive minds are neither creative nor cooperative. When you prove someone wrong, you jeopardize their sense of worth, and they will entrench more firmly in their position. The goal is connection, not correction.

"I don't blame you one iota for feeling as you do. If I were you, I would undoubtedly feel just as you do." - Dale Carnegie's "magic phrase" to stop arguments.

Communication Blunders & Frameworks

The Four Horsemen (Behaviors that destroy connection):

  • Criticism: Attacking the person, not their behavior. ("You always...")
  • Contempt: Name-calling, mockery, eye-rolling. Conveys disgust.
  • Defensiveness: Blaming others ("The problem isn't me, it's you.").
  • Stonewalling: Tuning out, disengaging from the conversation.

LEAPS Method (For disengaging politely):

  • Listen Actively
  • Empathize
  • Ask questions
  • Paraphrase
  • Summarize

HALT (When not to communicate):

Avoid making important decisions or having difficult conversations when you are:

  • Hungry
  • Angry
  • Lonely
  • Tired
