SFST Participant Manual Guide

A Comprehensive Guide for New Officers

SFST Development and Validation

NHTSA-sponsored research began in 1975 to identify the most effective roadside tests for DWI. From an initial pool of six common tests, three emerged as the most reliable and accurate.

Original Research Objectives:

  • Evaluate currently used physical coordination tests to determine their relationship to intoxication.
  • Develop more sensitive tests that would provide more reliable evidence of impairment.
  • Standardize the administration and scoring of these tests.

Standardized Elements:

  • Standardized Administrative Procedures: How the tests are administered.
  • Standardized Clues: The specific, observable indicators of impairment.
  • Standardized Criteria: The number of clues required to indicate likely impairment.

Key Field Validation Studies

Original SCRI Laboratory Accuracy (1977)

The initial lab research found the following individual test accuracies for detecting a 0.10 BAC: HGN: 77%, Walk and Turn: 68%, One Leg Stand: 65%.

Colorado SFST Validation Study (1995)

Found 86% correct arrest/release decisions. 93% of those arrested had a BAC of 0.05 or higher.

Florida SFST Field Validation Study (1997)

Validated the SFSTs at the 0.08 BAC limit, finding 95% correct arrest decisions.

San Diego SFST Validation Study (1998)

The most current primary research, finding 91% correct arrest decisions at the 0.08 BAC limit.

Physiology of Alcohol & Deterrence

Alcohol Physiology

  • Absorption: 20% through stomach wall, 80% through small intestine (pylorus muscle). Food slows this process.
  • Distribution: Alcohol is distributed via the blood to tissues in proportion to their water content. (Avg. Male: 68% water, Avg. Female: 55% water).
  • Elimination: The liver metabolizes most alcohol at an average rate of **0.015 BAC per hour**. Nothing can speed this up.
  • Mellanby Effect: A person feels *more* impaired while their BAC is rising than they do at the same BAC level while it is falling.

General Deterrence

  • Concept: Deterrence is based on the driving public's fear of being arrested.
  • Fort Lauderdale Study: Showed officers failed to detect 78% of DWI violators they contacted (arresting only 22% of drivers with BACs between 0.10-0.20).
  • Stockton Study: A targeted enforcement effort increased DWI arrests by 500% and decreased nighttime crashes by 34%, proving deterrence works.

DWI vs. Per Se Statutes

DWI (Driving While Impaired/Influenced)

This statute makes it illegal to operate a vehicle while under the influence of alcohol and/or drugs. The focus is on the driver's impairment.

  • Requires proof of impairment through observations (driving, personal contact, SFSTs).
  • A chemical test result is powerful, but just one piece of additional evidence.
  • A person can be convicted of DWI even with a BAC below the legal limit if impairment can be proven.

Illegal Per Se

This statute makes it illegal to operate a vehicle with a blood alcohol concentration (BAC) at or above a prohibited level (e.g., 0.08).

  • The focus is solely on the BAC number.
  • The chemical test is the presumptive evidence.
  • The prosecution does not need to prove the driver was actually impaired, only that their BAC was over the limit.

Implied Consent & Legal Presumptions

Implied Consent Law

By operating a motor vehicle on a public roadway, a driver is deemed to have already given their consent to submit to a chemical test upon a valid arrest for DWI.

  • Its purpose is to encourage submission to chemical tests.
  • Refusal to submit typically results in administrative penalties, such as driver's license suspension or revocation.

Legal Presumptions (Example)

These define the presumptive weight of chemical test evidence. A typical structure is:

  • **0.08 or more:** Presumed to be under the influence.
  • **0.05 to 0.079:** No presumption either way; other evidence is critical.
  • **Less than 0.05:** Presumed *not* to be under the influence (but can be overcome by other evidence).

Case Law Principles

The admissibility of SFST evidence, particularly HGN, is a common point of legal challenge. While specific case law varies by state, the prevailing trend is to accept SFSTs as valid evidence of impairment, provided a proper foundation is laid.

Common Defense Challenges:

  • Scientific Validity: Defense may challenge the scientific reliability of the tests. Prosecutors counter this with the extensive NHTSA-sponsored research.
  • Relationship to BAC: HGN cannot be used to prove a specific BAC level (e.g., "6 clues means 0.12"). It is evidence of impairment, not a BAC measurement tool.
  • Officer Training & Application: The defense will challenge whether the officer was properly trained and administered the tests according to standardized procedures. Your training and adherence to the manual are your best defense.

The Three Phases of DWI Detection

The SFSTs are part of a systematic, three-phase process designed to gather evidence of impairment and build probable cause for a DWI arrest. Each phase presents opportunities to observe indicators of impairment and requires a key decision.

Phase One: Vehicle in Motion

Decision: Should I stop the vehicle?

Phase Two: Personal Contact

Decision: Should the driver exit?

Phase Three: Pre-Arrest Screening

Decision: Is there probable cause to arrest?

Phase One: Vehicle in Motion

This phase involves observing driving behaviors and the stopping sequence. The main decision is whether there is reasonable suspicion to initiate a traffic stop.

Automobile Driving Cues (NHTSA):

Problems Maintaining Lane Position (p=.50–.75)
  • Weaving / Weaving across lanes, Straddling a lane line, Drifting, Swerving, Turning with a wide radius, Almost striking object.
Speed and Braking Problems (p=.45–.70)
  • Stopping problems, Unnecessary acceleration/deceleration, Varying speed, Slow speed (10+ mph under).
Vigilance Problems (p=.55–.65)
  • Driving without headlights, Failure to signal, Driving in opposing lanes, Slow response to signals.
Judgment Problems (p=.35–.90)
  • Following too closely, Improper lane change, Illegal turn, Driving on other than roadway, Appearing to be impaired.

Motorcycle Impairment Cues (NHTSA):

Excellent Cues (≥50% Probability)
  • Drifting during turn, Trouble with dismount, Trouble with balance at stop, Turning problems, Weaving.
Good Cues (30-50% Probability)
  • Erratic movements, No lights at night, Recklessness, Following too closely, Evasion, Wrong way.

Phase Two: Personal Contact

Elements Checklist (Sensory & Exit Clues)

  • Sight Clues: Bloodshot eyes, soiled clothing, fumbling fingers, open containers, unusual actions.
  • Hearing Clues: Slurred speech, admission of drinking, inconsistent responses, abusive language.
  • Smell Clues: Odor of alcoholic beverages, marijuana, cover-up odors.
  • Exit Sequence Clues: Can't follow instructions, leaves car in gear, "climbs" out of vehicle, leans against car for support.

Pre-Exit Sobriety Tests (Optional Divided Attention Tasks)

  • Alphabet Test: Ask the subject to recite a portion of the alphabet (e.g., "from E to P").
  • Count Down Test: Ask the subject to count backwards between two non-round numbers (e.g., "from 68 to 53").
  • Finger Count Test: Ask the subject to touch thumb to each fingertip while counting up (1, 2, 3, 4) and then back down (4, 3, 2, 1).

Phase Three: Pre-Arrest Screening & The Arrest Decision

This phase consists of administering the SFSTs and culminates in the arrest decision, based on the totality of the evidence gathered.

Preliminary Breath Test (PBT)

A PBT is an optional tool to confirm the presence of alcohol. It should be administered *after* the SFSTs.

  • Purpose: To associate the observed physical and mental impairment with alcohol. It is NOT the sole basis for arrest.
  • Factors Causing HIGH Results: Residual mouth alcohol (must wait 15-20 mins), breath contaminants.
  • Factors Causing LOW Results: Breath sample cooling, improper breath sample (not deep-lung air).

The Arrest Decision

The decision to arrest is based on ALL evidence from all three phases: the driving, the personal contact, and the SFSTs. It is a culmination of the entire process.

Horizontal Gaze Nystagmus (HGN)

88% Accurate

at 0.08 BAC

Quick Reference

Test Type: Involuntary Eye Reflex | Total Clues: 6 | Decision Point: 4 or more clues

Elements Checklist (3 Clues per Eye)

  • Lack of Smooth Pursuit: Do the eyes jerk or bounce as they follow a moving stimulus?
  • Distinct & Sustained Nystagmus at Maximum Deviation: When the eye is held at maximum deviation for a minimum of 4 seconds, is the jerking distinct and sustained?
  • Onset of Nystagmus Prior to 45 Degrees: Does the eye begin jerking before it reaches a 45-degree angle from the center?

Practical Example

An officer administers HGN. In the driver's left eye, the officer observes a lack of smooth pursuit and distinct nystagmus at maximum deviation. In the right eye, the officer observes all three clues. The officer has observed a total of 5 clues (2 in the left, 3 in the right). This is above the decision point of 4 clues and is a strong indicator of impairment.

Types of Nystagmus

Vestibular Nystagmus

Caused by movement/action to the vestibular (inner ear) system. Not a factor in a properly administered HGN test.

  • Rotational, Post-Rotational, Caloric, Positional Alcohol Nystagmus (PAN).

Neural Nystagmus

Caused by neural activity. HGN test procedures are designed to isolate for Gaze Nystagmus.

  • Optokinetic, Physiological, Gaze (HGN, VGN, Resting).

Pathological Nystagmus

Caused by medical conditions like brain tumors or inner ear diseases. This is why the initial medical assessment (pupil size, equal tracking) is crucial. These conditions are rare in the driving population.

HGN Administrative Procedures

  1. Check for Eyeglasses/Contacts: Instruct subject to remove glasses.
  2. Verbal Instructions: Tell subject: "Feet together, arms at your sides, head still. Follow the stimulus with your eyes only."
  3. Position Stimulus: 12-15 inches in front of nose, slightly above eye level.
  4. Medical Assessment: Check for Equal Pupil Size and Resting Nystagmus.
  5. Medical Assessment: Check for Equal Tracking of both eyes.
  6. Check for Lack of Smooth Pursuit: Two-second pass from center to side. Check left eye, then right. Repeat.
  7. Check for Distinct & Sustained Nystagmus: Hold at maximum deviation for a minimum of 4 seconds. Check left eye, then right. Repeat.
  8. Check for Onset Prior to 45 Degrees: Four-second pass from center to 45-degree point. Check left eye, then right. Repeat.
  9. Total the Clues: Document the total number of clues observed out of 6.
  10. Check for Vertical Gaze Nystagmus (VGN): Elevate stimulus to maximum upward gaze and hold for 4 seconds.

Walk and Turn (WAT)

79% Accurate

at 0.08 BAC

Quick Reference

Test Type: Divided Attention | Total Clues: 8 | Decision Point: 2 or more clues

Elements Checklist (2 Instruction, 6 Walking)

  • (Instruction Stage) Cannot Keep Balance: Steps off the line or breaks the heel-to-toe stance while listening to instructions.
  • (Instruction Stage) Starts Too Soon: Begins walking before told to do so.
  • (Walking Stage) Stops While Walking: Pauses for several seconds.
  • (Walking Stage) Misses Heel-to-Toe: Leaves > 1/2 inch gap.
  • (Walking Stage) Steps Off the Line: Foot comes completely off the line.
  • (Walking Stage) Uses Arms to Balance: Raises arms > 6 inches from sides.
  • (Walking Stage) Improper Turn: Loses balance, removes pivot foot, or turns incorrectly.
  • (Walking Stage) Incorrect Number of Steps: Takes more or fewer than 9 steps.

Practical Example

During the instruction stage, a driver breaks their heel-to-toe stance (1 Clue). They then take 10 steps on the first pass (1 Clue), make an improper turn (1 Clue), and stop while walking on the way back (1 Clue). Total clues observed: 4. This is above the decision point of 2 clues.

WAT Conditions, Limitations, and Instructions

Test Conditions & Limitations

Requires a reasonably dry, hard, level, non-slippery surface with a real or imaginary line.

  • Age: Individuals over 65 may have difficulty.
  • Health: People with back, leg, or inner ear problems may have difficulty.
  • Footwear: Subjects wearing heels > 2 inches high should be given the opportunity to remove them.

Standardized Instructions (Verbatim)

Instruction Stage: "Place your left foot on the line... heel of your right foot against the toe of the left foot... arms down at your sides... Do not start to walk until told to do so. Do you understand...?"

Walking Stage: "When I tell you to start, take nine heel-to-toe steps on the line, turn, and take nine heel-to-toe steps back... When you turn, keep the front foot on the line, and turn by taking a series of small steps with the other foot, like this. (Demonstrate)... keep your arms at your sides, watch your feet... count your steps out loud... don't stop... Do you understand...?"

One-Leg Stand (OLS)

83% Accurate

at 0.08 BAC

Quick Reference

Test Type: Divided Attention | Total Clues: 4 | Decision Point: 2 or more clues

Elements Checklist (Total 4 Clues)

  • Sways While Balancing: Noticeable side-to-side or back-and-forth body movement.
  • Uses Arms to Balance: Raises arms more than 6 inches from their sides.
  • Hops: Leaves the standing foot to maintain balance.
  • Puts Foot Down: The raised foot touches the ground one or more times during the 30-second test.

Practical Example

An officer instructs a driver to begin the OLS test. During the 30-second test, the driver sways visibly (1 clue) and puts their foot down at the 15-second mark (1 clue). The driver has exhibited 2 clues. This meets the decision point and indicates impairment.

OLS Conditions, Limitations, and Instructions

Test Conditions & Limitations

Requires a reasonably dry, hard, level, non-slippery surface.

  • Age: Individuals over 65 may have difficulty.
  • Weight: People 50 or more pounds overweight may have difficulty.
  • Health: People with back, leg, or inner ear problems may have difficulty.
  • Footwear: Subjects wearing heels > 2 inches high should be given the opportunity to remove them.

Standardized Instructions (Verbatim)

Instruction Stage: "Please stand with your feet together and your arms down at the sides... Do not start... until I tell you to do so. Do you understand...?"

Balance & Counting Stage: "When I tell you to start, raise either leg... approximately six inches off the ground, keeping your foot parallel to the ground... keep both legs straight... arms at your side... count out loud... 'one thousand one, one thousand two...' until told to stop... keep watching the raised foot. Do you understand?"

Introduction to Drugged Driving

The SFSTs can reveal impairment from drugs other than alcohol. This section provides a brief overview of the seven drug categories and their most common indicators. This does NOT qualify you as a Drug Recognition Expert (DRE).

Seven Drug Categories & Key Indicators

Category Common Examples HGN VGN Pupil Size
CNS Depressants Alcohol, Barbiturates, Xanax Present Present (High Dose) Normal
CNS Stimulants Cocaine, Methamphetamine Absent Absent Dilated
Hallucinogens LSD, Psilocybin, MDMA Absent Absent Dilated
Dissociative Anesthetics PCP, Ketamine, DXM Present Present Normal
Narcotic Analgesics Heroin, Morphine, Fentanyl Absent Absent Constricted
Inhalants Toluene, Nitrous Oxide, Aerosols Present Present (High Dose) Normal
Cannabis Marijuana, Hashish, Synthetics Absent Absent Dilated (May be Normal)

Medical Conditions Mimicking Impairment

Always be aware that certain medical conditions can produce signs similar to drug or alcohol impairment. If you suspect a medical issue, summon medical aid immediately.

  • Head Trauma: Can cause confusion, disorientation, unequal pupils, and poor balance.
  • Stroke: May result in slurred speech, confusion, and facial droop.
  • Diabetes: A diabetic emergency (hypoglycemia) can cause confusion, unsteadiness, and a sweet odor on the breath (sometimes mistaken for alcohol).
  • Conjunctivitis (Pink Eye): Causes red, watery eyes.
  • Multiple Sclerosis & Other Neurological Issues: Can cause nystagmus and coordination problems.

Field Note-Taking Guide

This guide is a tool to ensure you document all necessary information during a DWI investigation. Your notes should be clear, concise, and descriptive. They will be the foundation for your report and testimony.

WAT Note-Taking Conventions

  • Stops Walking: At the step where the subject stops, draw a vertical line from the toe and write an "S".
  • Misses Heel-to-Toe: At the step where the gap occurs, draw a vertical line from the toe and write an "M".
  • Steps Off Line: Draw a line from the footprint diagram at an angle, showing the direction the foot stepped off.
  • Incorrect Steps: If more than 9, draw extra footprints. If fewer than 9, cross out the missing footprints.

OLS Note-Taking Conventions

In the OLS section of your notes:

  • Indicate which leg the subject chose to raise (L or R).
  • Puts Foot Down: Each time the subject puts their foot down, write the number they were on in their count (e.g., 1012, 1019, 1025). This is more descriptive than just tally marks.
  • Use checkmarks or numbers to indicate the occurrence of Sways, Uses Arms, and Hops.

SFST Proficiency Examination Checklist

Use this checklist to practice and self-assess your administration of the SFST battery. To pass, you must be able to explain and proficiently complete every step.

Maintaining an SFST Log

An SFST log is a critical tool for documenting your experience and proficiency. It is highly recommended by IACP and NHTSA and can be invaluable in establishing your credibility in court.

Essential Components of an SFST Log:

  • Date of SFST administration
  • Subject's name/identifier
  • Results of each test (number of clues)
  • Your classification based on SFSTs (BAC +/- .08)
  • Final action (Arrest / No Arrest)
  • Subject's measured BAC (if available)

Processing, Reporting, & Testimony

The arrest is not the end of the process. Proper processing, clear report writing, and professional testimony are essential for a successful prosecution.

Post-Arrest Processing Sequence:

  1. Inform driver they are under arrest, pat down/frisk, and handcuff.
  2. Secure defendant in patrol vehicle and secure their vehicle/property.
  3. Transport to appropriate facility.
  4. Read Implied Consent rights and request chemical test.
  5. Administer evidentiary test (or log refusal).
  6. Read Miranda rights before post-arrest questioning.
  7. Complete all required reports.

Sample Narrative Report: The Case of "Jarod Primo"

This sample report demonstrates the block outline format and the level of detail required to "paint a picture" for the prosecutor and court.

First Observations: On XX-XX-XX at approximately 9:00 p.m., I was patrolling northbound on State Road 113... I observed a white sport utility vehicle traveling ahead of me... I noticed the S/V drift outside of its travel lane to the left, crossing the line divider the entire width of its left side tires. The vehicle corrected its path, returned to the right lane, and then drifted to the left again... As I moved closer to the vehicle, I noticed there was no license plate displayed on the rear of the S/V. I signaled for the vehicle to stop by activating my emergency lights. The driver did not respond for approximately 700-800 feet, so I activated an audible siren... the driver reacted... and exited the freeway... At the foot of the ramp the S/V did not stop at the stop sign or signal his turn but rolled through it...

Observations After the Stop: I approached the S/V on the passenger side... I immediately noticed the driver had red, bloodshot, watery eyes. I advised him of the reasons for the stop... I requested his driver’s license, registration, and insurance. The driver handed me a visa credit card... He did not attempt to produce his registration or insurance card... I noticed his speech was slurred when he was speaking to me and I detected the odor of an alcoholic beverage that was greater when he spoke... I asked him how much alcohol he had consumed tonight, and he replied, “Just a couple of drinks.”... When he stepped from the vehicle he was unsteady on his feet and staggered slightly as he walked towards the front of the vehicle...

Field Sobriety Tests: This evaluation was performed on the west shoulder of CR 100 Drive... The evaluation surface was level packed dirt... HGN: I observed lack of smooth pursuit, distinct and sustained nystagmus at maximum deviation, and an onset of nystagmus prior to 45 degrees in both of Primo’s eyes... WAT: (Instruction Stage) Lost balance (feet broke apart) and swayed noticeably from side to side. (Walking Stage) Raised left arm over 6 inches away from body to assist with balance at steps 3 and 5... Missed heel to toe once... Turn: Primo only took one step during the turn instead of several small steps as instructed... OLS: Primo raised his right foot... He put his foot down on counts 1010 and 1014. He used his arms for balance... and was swaying... He hopped on 1013. He counted to 1014 during the thirty seconds of the test.

Arrest: Based on the following information, I formed the opinion that Primo was driving under the influence: Driving at night with no license plate, Weaving out of his lane..., Failing to stop for stop sign..., divided attention problems while retrieving his license..., His red, bloodshot, watery eyes and slurred speech, His admissions..., Staggering after exiting vehicle, Odor of alcoholic beverage..., signs of impairment as he performed the SFSTs. I arrested Primo for driving under the influence... at 9:20 p.m. He provided two breath samples of 0.095 and 0.092...

Trial Tips & Courtroom Decorum

Preparation

  • Review your entire report and notes before court.
  • Mentally organize the testimony chronologically (Phase 1, 2, 3).
  • Have a pre-trial conference with the prosecutor. Discuss strengths, weaknesses, and potential defense questions.

On the Stand

  • **TELL THE TRUTH.** Your credibility is paramount.
  • Listen carefully to the full question before answering. It's okay to say "I don't know" or "I don't recall."
  • Speak loudly, clearly, and in plain language. Avoid jargon.
  • Look at the judge or jury when you answer, not the attorney.
  • Remain professional and courteous, even during cross-examination. Do not argue.
  • Testify to what you observed; do not say the subject "passed" or "failed" the tests.
