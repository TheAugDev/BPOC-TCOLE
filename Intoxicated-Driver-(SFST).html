Intoxicated Driver & SFST

Driving While Intoxicated (DWI) - PC ยง49.04

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person is intoxicated while operating a motor vehicle in a public place.

Punishment Levels:

Class B Misdemeanor Standard first offense.
Class A Misdemeanor If BAC is 0.15 or more (first offense).
State Jail Felony If a passenger is younger than 15 years old.
3rd Degree Felony If the person has two or more previous DWI convictions.

"Intoxicated" Defined (PC ยง49.01):

  • Not having the normal use of mental or physical faculties by reason of the introduction of alcohol, a controlled substance, a drug, a dangerous drug, a combination of two or more of those substances, or any other substance into the body; OR
  • Having an alcohol concentration of 0.08 or more.

Practical Example: An officer stops a car for swerving. The driver has slurred speech, red glassy eyes, and a strong odor of alcohol. The driver is intoxicated because they do not have the normal use of their mental and physical faculties, regardless of their BAC. If a breath test later shows a 0.10 BAC, they are also intoxicated *per se*.

NHTSA DWI Detection Phases

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) provides a systematic approach to identifying intoxicated drivers, broken into three phases. Each phase builds on the last to develop probable cause for arrest.

Phase One: Vehicle in Motion

  • Goal: Observe the vehicle in operation to determine if reasonable suspicion exists to initiate a traffic stop.
  • Key Cues: Weaving, drifting, swerving, turning with a wide radius, driving without headlights at night, speeding or driving unusually slow, and braking erratically.

Practical Example: At 2 AM, an officer observes a vehicle drifting from the center line to the shoulder line multiple times. This "weaving" provides the officer with reasonable suspicion that the driver may be intoxicated, justifying a traffic stop.

Phase Two: Personal Contact

  • Goal: After the stop, observe and speak with the driver to determine if there is sufficient cause to ask them to exit the vehicle for further testing.
  • Key Cues: Odor of alcoholic beverage, slurred speech, red/glassy/watery eyes, fumbling with license/insurance, soiled clothing, admission of drinking.

Practical Example: Upon contacting the driver from the previous example, the officer smells a strong odor of alcohol from within the car and notices the driver's eyes are red and glassy. The officer asks the driver to step out for sobriety tests.

Phase Three: Pre-Arrest Screening (SFSTs)

  • Goal: Administer Standardized Field Sobriety Tests (SFSTs) to determine if there is probable cause to arrest for DWI.
  • The Three Tests: Horizontal Gaze Nystagmus (HGN), Walk and Turn, and One-Leg Stand.

Practical Example: The officer administers the SFSTs. The driver shows 6/6 clues on HGN, steps off the line multiple times on the Walk and Turn, and puts their foot down three times on the One-Leg Stand. Based on the totality of circumstances (driving, physical cues, and SFST performance), the officer develops probable cause and arrests the driver for DWI.

Intoxication Assault - PC ยง49.07

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person, by accident or mistake...while operating a...motor vehicle in a public place while intoxicated, by reason of that intoxication causes serious bodily injury to another.

Punishment Level:

3rd Degree Felony Standard offense level.

Elements Checklist:

  • A person is operating a motor vehicle in a public place.
  • The person is intoxicated.
  • By reason of that intoxication, they cause serious bodily injury (SBI) to another person.
  • "Serious Bodily Injury" means injury that creates a substantial risk of death or that causes serious permanent disfigurement or protracted loss or impairment of the function of any bodily member or organ.

Practical Example: An intoxicated driver runs a stop sign and T-bones another car. The driver of the other car suffers a compound leg fracture. Because the intoxicated operation of the vehicle caused an injury that results in protracted loss of function of a limb, the intoxicated driver has committed Intoxication Assault.

Intoxication Manslaughter - PC ยง49.08

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person...operates a motor vehicle in a public place...is intoxicated and by reason of that intoxication causes the death of another by accident or mistake.

Punishment Level:

2nd Degree Felony Standard offense level.

Elements Checklist:

  • A person is operating a motor vehicle in a public place.
  • The person is intoxicated.
  • By reason of that intoxication, they cause the death of another person.

Practical Example: An intoxicated driver is speeding and loses control, crossing the center line and hitting an oncoming car head-on. The driver of the other car is killed. The intoxicated driver has committed Intoxication Manslaughter.

Consent to Taking of Specimen (Implied Consent) - TC ยง724.011

Legal Definition: If a person is arrested for an offense arising out of acts alleged to have been committed while the person was operating a motor vehicle in a public place...while intoxicated...the person is deemed to have consented...to submit to the taking of one or more specimens of the person's breath or blood for analysis...

Key Concepts:

  • Applies to any offense under Penal Code Ch. 49 (DWI, BWI, Intox Assault, etc.).
  • By driving on a Texas road, a person gives their "implied consent."
  • An officer is not asking for consent, but asking a person to comply with the consent already given by law.
  • A person can still withdraw this consent by refusing, which triggers other consequences.

Practical Example: A boater is stopped for Boating While Intoxicated (BWI). Upon arrest, the officer requests a breath test. The boater is subject to the implied consent law just like a driver on a highway and faces the same consequences for refusal.

Prohibition on Taking Specimen if Person Refuses - TC ยง724.013

Legal Definition: Except as provided by Section 724.012(b), a specimen may not be taken if a person refuses to submit to the taking of a specimen designated by a peace officer.

Key Concepts:

  • If a person refuses a chemical test, the officer cannot force them to provide one.
  • The officer's only recourse is to seek a search warrant for the person's blood.
  • The only exception is the "Mandatory Blood Draw" statute (TC ยง724.012), which applies in cases of death or SBI.

Practical Example: An officer arrests a driver for a standard, no-crash DWI. The driver refuses to provide a breath sample after the statutory warning is read. The officer cannot physically force the driver to blow. Instead, the officer must begin the process of writing an affidavit to obtain a search warrant from a judge to have the driver's blood drawn.

Information Provided Before Requesting Specimen (DIC-24) - TC ยง724.015

Legal Definition: Before requesting a specimen, an officer must inform the person orally and in writing of the consequences of refusing or failing a test. This is known as the "Statutory Warning."

Elements of the Warning:

  • A refusal can be used against them in court.
  • A refusal results in a 180-day license suspension.
  • Failing the test (0.08 BAC or more) results in a 90-day license suspension.

Practical Example: After arrest, an officer reads the DIC-24 form verbatim to the suspect. After hearing the warnings about license suspensions, the driver, who was initially hesitant, agrees to provide a breath sample.

Taking of Specimen (Mandatory Blood Draw) - TC ยง724.012

Legal Definition: A peace officer SHALL require the taking of a specimen if they reasonably believe a crash caused by an intoxicated driver resulted in the death or serious bodily injury of another person.

Elements Checklist:

  • The person is under arrest for an intoxication offense (DWI, Intoxication Assault/Manslaughter).
  • The person was operating a vehicle involved in a crash.
  • The officer reasonably believes the crash resulted in Death or Serious Bodily Injury to another person.
  • In these cases, the officer does not need a warrant to take blood, even if refused. (Other mandatory draw situations also exist, e.g., for repeat offenders).

Practical Example: An intoxicated driver causes a crash resulting in SBI. The driver is arrested and refuses consent for blood. Because the crash resulted in SBI, the officer can use the "mandatory blood draw" provision and transport the suspect to a hospital to have their blood drawn without a warrant.

Person Incapable of Refusal - TC ยง724.014

Legal Definition: A person who is dead, unconscious, or otherwise incapable of refusal is considered not to have withdrawn the consent provided by Section 724.011.

Key Concepts:

  • This applies to suspects who are arrested for an intoxication-related offense.
  • If the person cannot communicate a refusal due to injury, extreme intoxication, or being unconscious, their implied consent remains valid.
  • A specimen may be taken without a warrant in these circumstances.

Practical Example: A driver is found passed out behind the wheel of a crashed vehicle, clearly intoxicated and unresponsive. Officers arrest him for DWI. Because he is unconscious and incapable of refusing, his implied consent stands. Officers can transport him to the hospital for a blood draw based on this statute.

Rules for Taking Breath & Blood Specimens - TC ยง724.016 & .017

Legal Definition: The law sets specific requirements for how chemical specimens must be taken to be admissible in court.

Key Concepts:

  • Breath Specimen (TC ยง724.016): Must be taken by a person certified by DPS to operate a breath testing instrument.
  • Blood Specimen (TC ยง724.017): Only a physician, qualified technician, chemist, registered professional nurse, or licensed vocational nurse may take a blood specimen at the request of a peace officer. The location must be a sanitary place.
  • Additional Analysis (TC ยง724.019): A person who provides a specimen has the right to request their own independent analysis by a qualified person of their choosing.

Practical Example: Following a DWI arrest and warrant, an officer takes the suspect to a local hospital for a blood draw. The officer ensures that the person drawing the blood is a registered nurse to comply with the statute. The officer also informs the suspect of their right to have their own expert test the blood sample later.

DWI Enforcement Forms

Properly completing and submitting administrative and evidentiary forms is a critical part of a DWI arrest.

Key Forms:

  • DIC-23 (Peace Officer's Sworn Report): The officer's affidavit of probable cause for the arrest, detailing the facts and circumstances of the stop and investigation. It is a required document for the administrative license revocation (ALR) hearing.
  • DIC-24 (Statutory Warning): The form that is read to the suspect which details the consequences of refusing or failing a chemical test. The officer must document that it was read and whether the suspect consented or refused.
  • DIC-25 (Notice of Suspension): If a suspect refuses or fails a test, this form is served on them. It acts as their official notice of suspension and serves as their temporary driving permit for a limited time.
  • THP-51 (Mandatory Blood Draws Form): A form used to document the statutory authority and circumstances justifying a mandatory blood specimen collection.

Case Law: Curtis v. State

Key Holding: The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals held that an officer's observation of a vehicle weaving within its own lane can be sufficient grounds to establish reasonable suspicion for a traffic stop, especially when considering the officer's training and experience in DWI enforcement.

Elements Checklist:

  • An officer observes a vehicle failing to maintain a single marked lane (weaving).
  • The officer's training and experience lead them to believe this driving behavior is indicative of intoxication.
  • Based on the totality of the circumstances, the traffic stop is considered reasonable and lawful.

Practical Example: An officer trained in SFST is on patrol and sees a car ahead weave within its lane three times over a quarter-mile. The officer does not observe the car cross any lines, but based on their training that this is a common cue for intoxicated driving, they initiate a traffic stop. The stop is legally justified under *Curtis v. State*.

Allowing Dangerous Driver to Borrow Motor Vehicle - TC ยง705.001

Legal Definition: A person commits an offense if the person: (1) is the owner of a motor vehicle; (2) knows that the person who will operate the vehicle is an unlicensed driver; and (3) permits that person to operate the vehicle on a highway.

Punishment Level:

Class C Misdemeanor Standard offense level.

Elements Checklist:

  • A person owns a motor vehicle.
  • They know the person they are lending it to does not have a valid license.
  • They permit that person to operate the vehicle on a highway.

Practical Example: John knows his 15-year-old nephew only has a learner's permit and is not legally allowed to drive alone. John lets his nephew take his car to the store by himself anyway. If the nephew is stopped, not only can he be cited, but John can also be cited for Allowing a Dangerous Driver to Borrow his Vehicle.
