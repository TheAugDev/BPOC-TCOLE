Traumatic Brain Injury

Defining Brain Injury

Brain injuries encompass a wide range of conditions and are referred to by many terms, including TBI, Concussion, Stroke, Abusive Head Trauma, Shaken Baby Syndrome, and Neurological Damage. These all fall under the umbrella term "Acquired Brain Injury."

Types of Acquired Brain Injury (ABI)

Acquired Brain Injury (ABI)

  • Definition: An injury to the brain that occurs after birth and is not related to a congenital or degenerative disease.
  • Effects: Can cause temporary or permanent impairments resulting in physical, emotional, and intellectual difficulties.

Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

  • Definition: A type of ABI that disrupts normal brain function, caused by an external force like a blow to the head or a penetrating injury.
  • Injury Types: Can be a closed-head injury (skull remains intact) or an open-head injury (an object penetrates the skull and brain).

Non-Traumatic Brain Injury

  • Definition: A type of ABI that disrupts normal brain function, caused by an internal event rather than an external force.
  • Causes Include: Stroke, infection (meningitis, encephalitis), anoxia/hypoxia (lack of oxygen from near-drowning, strangulation, etc.), brain tumors, aneurysms, exposure to toxins, and drug-induced events.

Comparison of Brain Injury Causes

Traumatic Brain Injury Causes

(Caused by external forces)

  • Concussion / Sports injuries
  • Violence / Assault
  • Blast injury (explosion)
  • Being struck by or against something
  • Falls
  • Motor vehicle crash
  • Abusive Head Trauma / Shaken Baby Syndrome

Non-Traumatic Brain Injury Causes

(Caused by internal events)

  • Stroke
  • Drug induced / Exposure to toxins
  • Anoxic brain injury (lack of oxygen)
  • Meningitis / Encephalitis
  • Brain tumors
  • Near drowning
  • Aneurysm

Prevalence of Brain Injury

Traumatic brain injury is the leading cause of death and disability in children and adults. Over 2.2 million people sustain a TBI in the U.S. each year, with over 144,000 in Texas alone. This number is greater than annual diagnoses of Alzheimer's, breast cancer, HIV/AIDS, prostate cancer, lung cancer, and ALS combined.

Causes of Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

Primary Causes:

  • Falls: From heights or simple slips and tumbles.
  • Struck by/against something: Falling debris, violence, etc.
  • Motor vehicle crashes: Cars, motorcycles, ATVs.
  • Violence: Assault, domestic violence, shaken baby syndrome.
  • Explosion/Blast Injury: Military or civilian exposure.
  • Concussion: Sports injuries, crashes.

Falls: 40%

Struck by/against: 16%

Motor vehicle crashes: 14%

Assaults: 11%

Other/Unknown: 19%

Populations with Higher Risk

  • Children (0-4) & Adolescents (16-19): Most likely to have TBI-related ER visits.
  • Older Adults (75+): Highest rates of TBI-related hospitalizations and deaths.
  • Domestic Violence Survivors: Prevalence of TBI is estimated to be over 35%.
  • Athletes: Over 1.6 million sports-related concussions occur annually in the U.S.
  • Offenders (Adult & Juvenile): Estimated TBI prevalence in imprisoned populations is 60.3%.
  • Homeless Individuals: TBI prevalence is between 30% and 40%.
  • Veterans: An estimated 10-20% of Iraq veterans have some level of brain injury.

The Reality of Brain Injury

Brain injuries can happen to anyone, anywhere, at any time. They are not planned and often come as a complete shock. Many historical and public figures have lived with the effects of brain injuries, highlighting its widespread impact.

Examples include Harriet Tubman (TBI from injury), Abraham Lincoln (TBI from being kicked by a mule), Charles Dickens (stroke), Gabby Giffords (TBI from gunshot wound), and Tracy Morgan (TBI from car crash).

Brain Injury Severity

Severity is determined by confusion/disorientation, loss of consciousness, memory loss, and imaging results. A "mild" TBI can have severe consequences, is often unrecognized, and should always be taken seriously, as multiple mild injuries can have exponential effects.

Factor Mild Moderate Severe
Confusion/Disorientation Less than 24 hours More than 30 min but less than 24 hrs More than 24 hours
Loss of Consciousness 0-30 minutes More than 30 min but less than 24 hrs More than 24 hours
Memory Loss Less than 24 hours More than 24 hours but less than 7 days More than 7 days

Note on Imaging: For all levels of severity, imaging results (MRI/CT) may come back normal or abnormal. These tests do not always pick up certain chemical and physical reactions, so imaging should not be the only determining factor.

Functions of the Brain & Effects of Injury

An injury to a specific lobe of the brain affects its corresponding functions. For example, an injury to the Frontal Lobe can impact personality, decision-making, memory, and impulse control. Often, injuries like a Diffuse Axonal Injury can damage cells throughout multiple parts of the brain.

Frontal Lobe: Thought, memory, behavior, personality, planning, problem-solving.
Parietal Lobe: Language, touch, spatial awareness.
Temporal Lobe: Hearing, learning, emotions.
Occipital Lobe: Visual processing.
Cerebellum: Balance and coordination.
Brain Stem: Breathing, heart rate, temperature.

Three Categories of Change & Their Manifestations

Cognitive Changes

Common changes include difficulty with memory, lack of concentration, delayed thinking/processing, reckless decision-making, and difficulty communicating.

Practical Manifestations (Checklist):
  • Struggles following conversations or needs things repeated.
  • Has difficulty finding a word to say.
  • Is unable to recognize words they once knew.
  • Is easily distracted or has trouble following directions.
  • Can't remember answers to simple questions.

Emotional/Behavioral Changes

Common changes include irritability, aggression, anxiety/depression, mood swings, inappropriate social behavior, and impulsivity.

Practical Manifestations (Checklist):
  • Has physical and verbal outbursts.
  • Displays inappropriate sexual behavior.
  • Makes impulsive decisions.
  • Shows inappropriate laughing or crying.

Physical Changes

Common changes include poor balance, impaired motor skills, hearing/vision issues, slurred speech, muscle tremors, chronic headaches, and seizures.

Practical Manifestations (Checklist):
  • Has impaired fine motor skills.
  • Experiences double vision, nystagmus, or trouble with depth perception.
  • Cannot recognize the meaning of sounds (auditory agnosia).
  • Is unable to speak or has slurred speech.

Officer Perception: These combined changes can make individuals seem uncooperative, disrespectful, indifferent, defiant, rude, vulgar, reckless, detached, irrational, evasive, dazed, "out of it," or as if they are under the influence of alcohol and/or drugs.

Comorbidities & Recovery

Common Comorbidities

The effects of a brain injury often lead to the development of two or more simultaneously present chronic conditions. Common ones include:

  • Substance use/abuse
  • Sleep problems
  • Depression and suicidality
  • Epilepsy
  • Neurodegenerative diseases (ALS, Alzheimer's)
  • Fatigue

The Recovery Process

Recovery is different for everyone and is best thought of as an ongoing, life-long process rather than a complete return to "normal." Survivors often learn to live with a "new normal," adapting to their limitations. A brain injury is more like a chronic condition than a broken bone that heals completely.

The First Responder's Role

As a first responder, you have a profound impact on the physical, mental, and social well-being of the people you interact with. You will encounter individuals with potential brain injuries when responding to:

  • Domestic Violence
  • Elderly falls
  • Public Intoxication
  • Strokes / Heart Attacks
  • Juvenile Misconduct
  • Drug activity
  • Assault
  • Suspicious person
  • Child abuse
  • Trauma calls
  • DWIs
  • Motor vehicle accidents
  • Mental health concerns
  • Suicide attempts

Brain Injury Prevention

In Children & Abusive Head Trauma

  • Keep stairs/floors clear, use safety gates, and install window guards. Use nonslip mats in bathtubs.
  • Ensure playgrounds have shock-absorbing surfaces (mulch, sand).
  • Follow CDC's Heads Up program and Natasha's Law for youth sports.
The Period of PURPLE Crying

An approach to prevent Shaken Baby Syndrome by educating caregivers that this period of crying is normal and will pass. The letters stand for:

  • P - Peak of Crying: May cry more each week, most in month 2.
  • U - Unexpected: Crying can come and go for no reason.
  • R - Resists Soothing: May not stop crying no matter what you try.
  • P - Pain-like Face: May look like they are in pain, even when they are not.
  • L - Long Lasting: Crying can last as much as 5 hours a day.
  • E - Evening: May cry more in the late afternoon and evening.

In Older Adults & Stroke Prevention:

  • Remove tripping hazards, improve home lighting, and install grab bars/handrails.
  • Maintain a regular physical activity program.
  • To prevent stroke: Eat a healthy diet, maintain a healthy weight, get enough exercise, don't smoke, and limit alcohol.

General Safety:

  • Motor Vehicles: Always wear seat belts, never drive intoxicated or distracted, and use appropriate child safety seats.
  • Helmets: Wear a properly fitted helmet when skiing, snowboarding, skateboarding, playing contact sports, or riding a motorcycle, bike, ATV, horse, or scooter.

Signs, Symptoms & Intervention Case Studies

Immediate Signs & Symptoms

After a hit or jolt to the head/body, encourage ER visit for:

  • Confusion or disorientation
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Severe headache, nausea, vomiting
  • Blood/clear fluid from nose/ears
  • Weakness, numbness, slurred speech

Symptoms can appear days or weeks later. Watch for ongoing headaches, sleep disturbances, irritability, or changes in performance.

Signs of a Stroke (F.A.S.T.)

  • F - Face Drooping: Is the smile uneven?
  • A - Arm Weakness: Does one arm drift down?
  • S - Speech Difficulty: Is speech slurred?
  • T - Time to call 911: Act immediately.

Case Studies

Case 1 (Delayed Symptoms): "Marni" was in a car crash, felt shaky but declined hospital transport to run errands. Months later, struggling with concentration and memory, she was diagnosed with a TBI. This highlights the importance of encouraging immediate medical evaluation even if the person feels "fine."

Case 2 (Proactive Intervention): Officer Ortega saw an older adult fall. He checked for immediate symptoms. Finding none, he still encouraged them to monitor for delayed symptoms and provided a handout. This demonstrates excellent proactive service.

Effective Communication Techniques

Do:

  • Create a safe environment; explain limits and rules.
  • Be encouraging, respectful, and patient.
  • Use a positive or neutral tone.
  • Reduce distractions (move to a quiet area).
  • Speak slowly, simply, and clearly.
  • Focus on one subject at a time.
  • Check for understanding and ask for clarification.
  • Use active listening skills (see checklist below).
  • Show empathy for feelings, but not negative behavior.

Do Not:

  • Threaten, interrupt, argue, or challenge.
  • Shame or use a condescending tone.
  • Attempt to finish their sentences.
  • Speak harshly or too loudly.
  • View the interaction as a competition to be won.

Active Listening Skills (Checklist)

  • Pay attention: Give the speaker your undivided attention.
  • Show you are listening: Use open body language, nod, and provide small verbal cues ("uh huh," "I see").
  • Provide feedback: Paraphrase your understanding of their message ("What I'm hearing is...").
  • Get clarification: Ask questions to clarify anything you don't understand.
  • Defer judgment: Avoid interrupting with counterarguments.
  • Respond appropriately: Be candid, open, and honest in your response.

Case Study & Recognizing Difficulties

Recognizing Communication Difficulties

A person with a brain injury may have slurred speech, trouble finding words, misunderstand sarcasm, use inappropriate language, or need things repeated. If you notice these, consider the possibility of a brain injury and adapt your communication. It is not your job to diagnose. Some survivors carry a wallet card with emergency contacts.

Case Study: DWI Stop

"Jordan," a brain injury survivor, was pulled over for speeding. The officer's questions and the noisy environment increased Jordan's anxiety, causing symptoms to worsen: slurred speech, stumbling, and difficulty following instructions. The officer misinterpreted these as signs of intoxication, even when Jordan presented an emergency contact card. The interaction escalated, resulting in an unnecessary arrest, use of force, and a second brain injury for Jordan. This illustrates how recognizing symptoms and using proper communication techniques could have de-escalated the situation and led to a better outcome.

Resource Facilitation & Unmet Needs

You will likely encounter individuals living with brain injury who have unmet needs. Referring them to resources can have a hugely positive effect. These needs can be understood through a framework similar to Maslow's hierarchy:

  • Physiological: Food, shelter, health care.
  • Safety: Personal, physical, and financial security.
  • Belongingness/Love: Friendship, family, intimacy.
  • Esteem: Self-respect, confidence, independence.
  • Self-Actualization: Realizing one's potential.

Your role in communication, intervention, and resource facilitation can have a profoundly positive impact on a person's life.
