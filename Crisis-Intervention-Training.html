Crisis Intervention Training

BPOC Chapter 29 | TCOLE Preparation

Origins & Problem Statement

The underlying element behind mental illness-related behavior is usually not criminal or malicious. The CIT program was conceived in Memphis, TN, after a 1987 incident where police shot and killed a 26-year-old man with a history of mental illness who was threatening suicide. This event catalyzed the creation of CIT.

The Problem for Law Enforcement:

  • Individuals with mental illness generate no less than 1 in 10 calls for police service.
  • The risk of being killed when stopped by law enforcement is 16 times higher for individuals with untreated serious mental illness.
  • In 2015, 25% of all fatal officer-involved shootings involved a subject displaying signs of mental illness.

Texas Senate Bill 1849

This bill established significant mandates for Texas law enforcement regarding mental health response.

  • De-escalation Training: Law enforcement officers are required to learn de-escalation techniques to reduce the use of force.
  • Diversion Efforts: Agencies must make a good faith effort to divert persons suffering from a mental health crisis or substance abuse to proper treatment facilities rather than incarcerating them.
  • Personal Bond: The bill establishes an easier process for individuals with mental illness or intellectual disability to receive a personal bond.
  • Statutory Changes: Key changes were made to the Occupations Code, Chapter 1701, specifically sections 1701.253 and 1701.273, cementing these training requirements.

Primary Goal of CIT

"The primary goal of CITs involves calming persons with mental illness who are in crisis and referring them to mental health care services, rather than incarcerating them." This includes lessening injuries to officers and individuals, decriminalizing mental illness, reducing stigma, and using a team approach.

Key Objectives:

  • Educate officers on recognizing signs of mental illness.
  • Provide practical de-escalation skills to mitigate violence.
  • Increase officer and public safety.
  • Provide information on safely transporting individuals to appropriate facilities.

CIT's Impact on Community Relations

CIT has been shown to positively impact officer perceptions, decrease the need for higher levels of police intervention, and reduce officer injuries. It redirects individuals from the justice system to the healthcare system, building public trust.

  • National Presence: 46 of the 50 states have CIT programs.
  • Reduced Use of Force: A study of Houston PD found CIT officers were 82% less likely to use their guns on CIT-related calls.
  • Cost Savings: The Bexar County Sheriff's Office CIT program saved the county nearly $100 million over eight years and drastically reduced use-of-force incidents.
  • Improved Officer Confidence: Research shows CIT improves officer comfort, knowledge, and attitudes towards individuals with mental illness.

Fundamental Concepts

Defining "Crisis"

Crisis: An emotionally significant event or radical change that results in feelings of confusion, desperation, or terror.

  • A person in crisis may act instinctually (self-preservation) rather than with logical thought.
  • Potential Triggers: Death of a loved one/pet, job loss, financial difficulty, divorce, legal troubles.

Mental Illness vs. Insanity

Mental Illness: A diagnosed condition based on the DSM-5 that impacts thinking, feeling, or mood and impairs daily function.

Insanity: A legal term. In Texas, per TPC ยง8.01, it is an affirmative defense where the actor "did not know that his conduct was wrong" due to a severe mental disease or defect.

Prevalence & Stigma

Key Statistics & Terms

  • 1 in 5 U.S. adults experiences mental illness in a given year.
  • 1 in 25 U.S. adults experiences a serious mental illness, which means severe impairment in one or more major life activities.
  • A persistent mental illness is indicated by long durations of impairment.
  • Nearly two-thirds of all people with a diagnosable mental illness do not seek treatment.

Stigma & Barriers to Treatment

Stigma: A mark of disgrace or shame. It encourages inaccurate perceptions, such as the false belief that mental illness is a sign of weakness.

  • Negative Stereotypes: The common misconception that people with mental illness are dangerous, although only 3-5% of violent acts are attributed to serious mental illness.
  • Access Issues: Difficulty accessing care, cost of medication, and lack of insurance are major barriers.

Psychotropic Medications & Myths

Psychotropic Medications

Psychotropic Medication: Any medication capable of affecting a person's mind, emotions, and behavior. Used to treat psychosis, anxiety, depression, etc.

Reasons for Non-Compliance (A Primary Cause of Crisis):
  • Feeling better and believing they no longer need it.
  • Unpleasant side effects (weight gain, sexual dysfunction, feeling "dissociated").
  • Lack of health insurance or high cost.
  • Stigma associated with taking medication.

Common Myths vs. Realities

Myth: A person with a mental illness is also intellectually challenged.

Reality: They are fundamentally different conditions.

Myth: Most people with mental illness are homeless.

Reality: About two-thirds live in the community and lead productive lives.

Myth: I can't do anything for a person with a mental health problem.

Reality: It may be life-changing to help someone access services.

Personality Disorders

An enduring pattern of thinking, feeling, and behaving that is inflexible and deviates from the person's culture. Law enforcement may encounter these individuals because their way of thinking can lead to law-breaking behavior.

Paranoid Personality Disorder

  • Pervasive distrust and suspiciousness of others.
  • Believes others are exploiting or deceiving them.
  • Quick to react angrily to perceived attacks.

Antisocial Personality Disorder

  • Pervasive disregard for and violation of the rights of others.
  • Pattern of rule-breaking, deceitfulness, and aggression.
  • Lack of remorse or accountability.

Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)

  • Pervasive instability in relationships, self-image, and impulse control.
  • Frantic efforts to avoid abandonment.
  • Self-damaging impulsivity and recurrent suicidal behavior.

Narcissistic Personality Disorder

  • Persistent grandiosity, a need for admiration, and a lack of empathy.
  • Exaggerates achievements and feels entitled.
  • Exploits others and demonstrates arrogant behaviors.

Mood & Thought Disorders

Depression

  • The single most common factor in suicidal behavior.
  • Symptoms: Hopelessness, loss of interest, low energy, appetite/sleep changes.

Bipolar Disorder

  • Cycles between mania (highs) and depression (lows).
  • Suicide risk is 15 times higher than the general population.
  • Manic Phase Symptoms: Inflated self-esteem, decreased need for sleep, racing thoughts, excessive risk-taking.

Thought Disorders (Psychosis)

An illness involving a distortion of reality. Can include Delusions (fixed false beliefs) and Hallucinations (sensory distortions like hearing voices).

  • Symptoms: Nonsense speech ('word salad'), responding to unseen stimuli, disheveled appearance.

Substance Use Disorders

Continued substance use despite significant problems. It is rare to see intoxication from only one type of substance.

CNS Depressants (e.g., Alcohol, Xanax)

Signs: 'Drunk' behavior, slurred speech, disorientation. Overdose: Shallow breathing, cold skin, coma.

CNS Stimulants (e.g., Cocaine, Meth)

Signs: Anxiety, euphoria, grinding teeth, talkative. Overdose: Agitation, high body temp, seizures.

Hallucinogens (e.g., LSD, Ecstasy)

Signs: Dazed, disoriented, paranoia, poor perception of time.

Dissociative Anesthetics (e.g., PCP, Ketamine)

Signs: Blank stare, confused, possibly violent, increased pain threshold. Can lead to excited delirium.

Narcotic Analgesics (e.g., Heroin, Oxycontin)

Signs: Drowsiness, droopy eyelids, facial itching, slow speech. Overdose: Slow/shallow breathing, coma.

Inhalants (e.g., Paint, Gasoline)

Signs: Bloodshot eyes, confusion, odor of substance, flushed face.

Cannabis (e.g., Marijuana, K2/Spice)

Signs: Reddening of conjunctiva, increased appetite, relaxed inhibitions.

Other Substances (e.g., Bath Salts, Kratom)

Often analogs of other drugs; behavior is hard to predict. Kratom affects opioid brain receptors.

Cognitive & Developmental Disorders

Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

Caused by an impact to the head or blast injuries. Can cause deficits in attention, memory, judgment, and learning from mistakes.

Dementia & Alzheimer's Disease

Degeneration of mental function. 6 in 10 people with Alzheimer's will wander. If not found in 24 hours, up to half risk serious injury or death.

Practical Example: Law Enforcement Interactions
  • Car Accidents: May drive erratically, fail to obey traffic laws, or flee the scene due to confusion.
  • False Reports: May call 911 to report a theft after misplacing an item, or report an "intruder" who is actually a family member.
  • Indecent Exposure/Shoplifting: May forget social norms or forget to pay for items.

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

Developmental disorder with repetitive behaviors and difficulty with social interaction. Communication Tips: Be patient and calm, avoid physical contact, use praise, interact with a caregiver if possible.

Intellectual Disability (ID)

A fixed condition diagnosed by age 18 involving deficits in intellectual and adaptive functioning. Degrees range from Mild (IQ 50-69) to Profound (IQ below 20).

Practical Example: Questioning Methods
  • Be patient; repeat questions. Ask short, simple, open-ended questions. Speak slowly.
  • To help determine if an ID problem exists, you can ask them to perform simple tasks (e.g., tell time, give directions home, explain how to make change from a dollar).
  • Be aware that subjects may readily confess due to a lack of full understanding.

Post-Traumatic Stress (PTS)

Can develop after exposure to a traumatic event. 7-8% of the general population will experience PTSD in their lifetime.

Core Symptom Categories

  • Intrusion: Recurrent unwanted memories, dreams, or flashbacks.
  • Avoidance: Efforts to avoid reminders of the trauma.
  • Negative Changes in Mood/Thought: Persistent negative beliefs, self-blame (survivor's guilt), fear, shame, loss of interest.
  • Increased Arousal/Reactivity: Irritability, heightened startle response, recklessness, hypervigilance. This is the symptom cluster most likely to be encountered by law enforcement.

Trauma in Veterans

Rates of PTSD are significantly higher in veteran populations. 20% of all national suicides are completed by veterans.

Moral Injury

Moral Injury: Damage to one's conscience from perpetrating, witnessing, or failing to prevent acts that violate one's own moral code. Can lead to shame, guilt, and self-harm.

Survivor's Guilt

Survivor's Guilt: Arises when someone survives an event that others did not, leading to feelings that they should have died instead.

Practical Example: Officer Interaction

A veteran may feel extremely threatened by anyone approaching from behind (hypervigilance). An officer should approach from the front, announce their presence calmly, and maintain distance. Explaining each step before it happens (tactical transparency) can help build trust.

Intimate Partner Violence (IPV) and PTSD

IPV is a major cause of PTSD in women, who have a 10% lifetime chance of developing it.

  • IPV victims can develop the same post-traumatic stress symptoms as combat veterans.
  • Women with PTSD due to IPV often present as depressed or emotionally numb.
  • Repeated trauma can impair a victim's ability to assess the danger of a situation.

Suicide Statistics & Risk Factors

In 2010, suicide was the 9th leading cause of death in Texas. It is the 2nd leading cause of death for ages 15-24. Firearms account for nearly 50% of all suicide deaths.

Known Risk Factors

  • Male, age 15-34.
  • Depression.
  • Substance intoxication.
  • Previous suicide attempts.
  • Feelings of hopelessness/helplessness.
  • Specific plan, intent, and means.
  • Lack of support system & recent loss.
  • Feeling like a burden to family.

Protective Factors

  • Healthy support system.
  • Connection to spiritual faith.
  • Employment & financial stability.
  • Access to effective mental health care.
  • Positive coping and problem-solving skills.

Suicide Risk Assessment (PLAN)

A structured method to evaluate the immediacy of suicide risk.

  1. PLAN: Has the person been thinking of hurting themself? Have they made a specific plan?
  2. METHOD: Has the person decided on a method (e.g., firearm, pills) or a location?
  3. MEANS: Does the individual have the means to carry out the plan (e.g., access to a firearm, pills in hand)?
  4. INTENT: How determined is the person to follow through? Listen for cues of doubt or statements of certainty. Ask directly.

Law Enforcement Suicide

More officers die of suicide than from shootings and traffic accidents combined. The average age for police suicide is 42 with 16 years on the job.

  • Almost 1 in 4 police officers has thoughts of suicide at some point in their life.
  • Between 7-19% of police officers have symptoms of PTSD.
  • Changing the culture requires addressing mental health and suicide head-on, from the academy onward.

Initial Assessment & De-escalation

Three-Point Assessment

A quick field assessment of a person's mental state.

  • Level of Comprehension: Do they understand you? Does their speech make sense?
  • Behavior: How is their self-care? Is non-compliance potentially due to a mental health issue?
  • Emotion: What is their emotional state? Is it appropriate? Is it fluctuating rapidly?

5 Universal Truths of Human Interaction

  1. ALL people want to be treated with Dignity and Respect.
  2. ALL people would rather be asked than told.
  3. ALL people want to know why.
  4. ALL people would rather have options than threats.
  5. ALL people want a second chance.

The LEAPS Communication Model

A framework for effective verbal de-escalation. Remember the 80/20 Rule: Listen 80% of the time, talk 20% of the time.

  • Listen: The fundamental skill. Let the person vent while you gather information.
  • Empathize: Put yourself in their shoes. Verbalize understanding of their feelings (e.g., "It sounds like you feel betrayed."). You don't have to agree.
  • Ask Questions: Use open-ended questions (Who, What, When, Where, How) to gather more information.
  • Paraphrase: Reiterate what they said in your own words ("Let me be sure I understand..."). This confirms understanding and builds rapport.
  • Summarize: Bring it all together. Summarize their view, the options, and what happens next.

Advanced Communication Techniques

"I" Statements vs. "You" Statements

Use "I" statements to de-escalate (e.g., "I am worried you are going to harm yourself. Please..."). Avoid accusatory "You" statements (e.g., "You are not listening.").

Tactical Transparency

Explain an action before initiating it, so the person knows what to expect (e.g., "For your safety and mine, you will be handcuffed..."). Use only when the scene is secure.

Reflecting Statements & Responding to Verbal Abuse

Reflecting Statements: Neutral responses to encourage more communication (e.g., 'I see...').

Responding to Verbal Abuse: Ignore demeaning language. Focus on deflecting and redirecting (e.g., 'I understand that, but...').

Pro-LEAPS vs. Anti-LEAPS Phrases

  • Pro: "What can I do to help?"
  • Anti: "Calm down."
  • Pro: "Would you assist me...?"
  • Anti: "What's your problem?"
  • Pro: "For your safety and mine..."
  • Anti: "I'm not going to tell you again."

Systemic Issues & Victimization

Deinstitutionalization

Deinstitutionalization: The policy of relocating people with severe mental illness from residential facilities into the community, often without adequate resources. This has led to the criminal justice system becoming the de facto mental health system for many.

Homelessness & Victimization

  • At minimum, 25% of the American homeless population is seriously mentally ill.
  • People with serious mental illness are over 10 times more likely to be victims of violent crime than the general population.
  • Practical Example: A child with a mental illness may be more vulnerable to abuse and their report may be less likely deemed valid. An adult may be easily robbed or conned.

Legal Standards for Officers

Police Officer Emergency Detention (HSC ยง573.001)

Elements Checklist:
  • An officer has reason to believe and does believe the person has a mental illness; AND
  • Because of that illness, there is a substantial risk of serious harm to the person or others unless immediately restrained; AND
  • The officer believes there is not sufficient time to obtain a warrant.

Transportation Considerations & Liability

  • An officer who detains someone under HSC 573.001 must immediately transport them to the nearest appropriate inpatient mental health facility.
  • Officer Safety Checklist: Follow departmental policy, be aware of distance to the facility, and evaluate the person's behavior (violent, ambulatory, comatose, sedated) to determine the appropriate method of transport.
  • HSC ยง571.019(a) provides a limitation of liability: People acting in good faith, reasonably, and without negligence are not civilly or criminally liable.

Jail Diversion Programs

Strategies to redirect individuals out of the criminal justice system and into treatment.

  • Pre-arrest Diversion: Diverting someone from the system with no arrest, guiding them toward a mental health facility or hospital.
  • Post-arrest Diversion: Voluntary programs for offenders that provide an alternative to criminal case processing.
  • Mental Health Courts: A court that combines judicial supervision with community mental health treatment. Offenders who complete the program have a much lower recidivism rate (20% vs. 72%).
